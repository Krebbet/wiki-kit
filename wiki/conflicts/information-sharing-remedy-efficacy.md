# Conflict: Information-Sharing Remedy Efficacy

**Status:** Open — awaiting user ruling.

**Pages affected:** [[agristats-consent-decree]], [[regulatory-responses]]

## The dispute

**Forward-looking remedy-efficacy dispute, not a factual contradiction in the decree text.** Both positions agree on what the Agri Stats consent decree requires. They disagree on whether the open-access mandate and anonymisation floor will structurally disrupt the information asymmetry that enabled coordination — or instead expand the intermediary's customer base while leaving the core coordination infrastructure intact.

---

## Position A — DOJ / decree theory

*Sources: summaries 02 (DOJ press release), 09 (proposed final judgment text).*

The five remedy components together dismantle the information infrastructure that enabled tacit coordination:

1. The **sales-data prohibition (§IV.A)** removes the most granular coordination input — raw facility-level price and output data — from circulation entirely.
2. The **anonymisation and aggregation floor (§VI.D)** — ≥3 contributors, no single contributor >70% of any metric — prevents the remaining benchmarking data from being reverse-engineered to identify individual processor positions.
3. The **data-aging restriction (§VI.E)** — ≥45-day average lag; ≥90 days for forward production decisions — breaks the real-time coordination loop that made the benchmarking service actionable for parallel pricing.
4. The **open/non-discriminatory access requirement (§VI.C)** eliminates the structural access asymmetry: once buyers (restaurants, grocery chains, food distributors) can purchase reports at processor-equivalent pricing, the information advantage that enabled seller-side coordination is dissolved.
5. The **7-year court monitor (§§VII–VIII)**, defendant-funded and U.S.-selected, with annual reporting and whistleblower protections, provides a structural enforcement mechanism for the above.

The EMI carve-out — treating Agri Stats' pre-existing less-granular public-facing price reports as compliant — defines the safe-harbour floor: sufficiently aggregated, temporally lagged, non-discriminatory-access data exchange is lawful; anything above that threshold is addressed by the remedy.

**DOJ framing (Acting AG Todd Blanche, quoted in press release):** "meat prices will go DOWN for consumers."

## Position B — Dayen / American Prospect + Lee Hepner (AELP)

*Source: summary 03 (American Prospect, 2026-05-08).*

The open-access mandate converts a cartel-information monopoly into a broader subscription service without dismantling the coordination infrastructure. David Dayen and Lee Hepner (American Economic Liberties Project) advance three specific objections:

**1. The open-access mandate expands Agri Stats' revenue without blocking the harm.** Requiring that buyers (restaurants, grocery distributors) may now purchase Agri Stats data at processor-equivalent pricing means Agri Stats gains a new customer base — the very buyers who were previously excluded. Dayen characterises this as "laundering the cartel": the intermediary's commercial viability improves while its core role is preserved. Hepner quote: *"It is laundering the cartel."*

**2. Processor-ranking reports and consulting relationships remain intact.** The decree reaches the granularity and recency of reported data, but does not dissolve the processor-ranking reports (Books, Runs, Flags) that tell processors when to cut supply or raise prices. The consulting relationships through which Agri Stats staff advise processors on benchmark interpretation carry only verbal prohibitions against explicit price advice — unenforceable without structural separation.

**3. The ATPCO 1992 parallel.** The Airline Tariff Publishing Company (ATPCO) was the subject of a 1992 DOJ airline price-fixing settlement structured on similar access-expansion logic: the intermediary was permitted to continue operating, subject to open-access requirements, without structural separation from the coordination role. Dayen identifies the subsequent failure of the ATPCO remedy to disrupt airline price coordination as the precedent-setting failure mode — making the Agri Stats remedy not a novel solution but a repetition of a documented failure pattern.

**On the Tunney Act.** Dayen notes the 60-day public-comment window (after Federal Register publication) as the live procedural avenue for state AGs or third-party challengers to contest the decree's adequacy before it is entered. He characterises this as historically under-used in information-sharing cartel cases.

## Resolution

**Unresolved — pending user ruling.**

This is a forward-looking remedy-efficacy dispute about whether the consent decree's structural conduct remedies will produce their intended market effects. The factual content of the decree (what it requires) is not in dispute; the parties disagree on whether the requirements are sufficient.

The reference-layer page [[agristats-consent-decree]] states the remedy neutrally, enumerates its five components with the §IV/§VI/§VII/§VIII citations, and documents the critique in a clearly-labelled "Criticism / remedy-efficacy critique" subsection. The wiki does not adopt either position as editorial.

**Note on doctrinal scope:** the [[regulatory-responses]] page currently frames DOJ enforcement actions as part of the "consolidated counter-power landscape" without a sustained critique of remedy-efficacy for information-sharing cases. This conflict should inform any future revision of that framing — the Agri Stats case is the first captured instance where the remedy design itself is contested as potentially counter-productive.

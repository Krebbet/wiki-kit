# Community Choice Aggregation (CCA)

Community Choice Aggregation is a **public-agency collective-bargaining model for retail electricity** in which local governments aggregate the electricity demand of residents, businesses, and municipal accounts within their jurisdiction to negotiate supply contracts on residents' behalf. Distinct from the [[consumer-collective-bargaining|GPO and consumer-coop models]] on [[consumer-collective-bargaining]] in that the aggregating entity is a public agency with governance by elected officials, not a membership organisation.

## Scale

Nine US states authorise CCAs: **Massachusetts, Ohio, California, Illinois, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Rhode Island, and Virginia**. Collectively they serve about **15% of Americans across 1,850+ municipalities** as of 2024. Nationwide, only deregulated electricity states have enacted CCA enabling laws — 17 states + DC are deregulated; the other 33 are regulated monopoly states where utilities retain bundled generation + transmission + distribution.

## How it works

- **Formation:** local elected officials establish a CCA as a local not-for-profit public agency, typically via municipal ordinance or joint-powers authority across multiple municipalities.
- **Role split with the incumbent utility:** the CCA chooses the **generation source** and negotiates power-supply contracts. The incumbent utility continues to own and maintain **transmission, distribution, metering, and billing**. Customers receive a single utility bill that reflects the change in supplier.
- **Enrollment:** **opt-out by default** — residents are automatically enrolled when their municipality launches a CCA; they can opt back to the incumbent utility supplier at any time.
- **Governance:** a governing board of local elected officials (sometimes staff at a joint-powers authority) makes procurement, rate, and local-programme decisions.

## History

- **1995:** Massachusetts senate energy committee director Paul Douglas Fenn drafts the first CCA bill (Senate 447, Montigny).
- **1997:** Massachusetts enacts the CCA law (Utility Restructuring Act of 1997). Cape Light Compact (Cape Cod + Martha's Vineyard) forms as the nation's first CCA.
- **1995–2000:** Fenn forms the American Local Power Project and helps draft similar laws in Ohio, New Jersey, and other states.
- **2000:** Ohio adopts CCA law; NOPEC (Northeast Ohio Public Energy Council) forms.
- **2002:** California adopts CCA.
- **Mid-2010s:** California CCAs grow rapidly, often focused on greener generation mix.
- **2020:** Columbus, Ohio approves city-wide 100%-renewable electricity aggregation via a ballot measure.

## Nationwide CCA counts and scope

Per the captured Wikipedia article:

- **Cape Light Compact (Massachusetts):** serves 200,000 customers; runs energy-efficiency programmes and installs solar on Cape Cod schools, fire stations, and libraries.
- **NOPEC (Ohio):** nation's largest CCA when formed in 2000; now 228 communities in 14 counties; serves 510,000 electricity customers and 400,000 natural-gas customers. Reported a 70% air-pollution reduction in its regional power mix after switching from coal+nuclear to natural gas + small-percentage renewables.
- **Massachusetts state-wide:** ~150 cities and towns had adopted CCA by March 2019, including Boston and Worcester.
- **California:** of the 72 US cities and counties powered by 100% clean energy, **64 are in California and members of a CCA**.

## Renewable-energy performance

- **Nationwide:** only ~13% of CCAs offer "green power" as their primary focus; most focus on cost reduction. (Per Wikipedia.)
- **California:** CCAs "voluntarily purchase renewable energy at levels that exceed renewables portfolio standard (RPS) mandates." Between 2011 and 2018, California CCAs directly procured **24 TWh of RPS-eligible electricity**, of which **11 TWh was voluntary** (in excess of RPS compliance) — per the UCLA Luskin Center for Innovation cited in the Wikipedia article.
- **Sonoma Clean Power (2019):** 91% carbon-free power, with 2,000 customers on a premium EverGreen 100%-renewable service from local solar and geothermal. Plans to own and manage six solar plants in the Petaluma area.
- **New York state:** about 50% of the only active CCA sales are green (per the captured article; low CCA activity overall in NY).
- **EPA recognition:** MCE Clean Energy, Oak Park (IL), and Cincinnati (OH) have received the EPA Green Power Leadership Award.

## Counter-power mechanism

CCAs are a **structural intervention at the retail-electricity market level**, not an individual transparency tool. They work by:

1. **Separating generation choice from transmission monopoly.** State deregulation is the enabling precondition — without it, the incumbent utility's integrated monopoly blocks customer choice.
2. **Collectivising the choice default.** Individual customer-choice shopping (the alternative policy construct) has very low uptake because of switching friction. Opt-out aggregation means the default is the community's chosen supplier.
3. **Governing procurement via public accountability.** Elected officials (rather than share-holder-accountable utility executives) make the generation-mix decision — which produces observable greener outcomes per NREL/EPA awards cited above.

Relationship to other wiki mechanisms:

- **Different from [[consumer-collective-bargaining|GPOs]]** in that the aggregating entity is a **public agency**, not a commercial or non-profit intermediary funded by admin fees. No funding-mechanism incentive-incompatibility concern of the kind documented on [[consumer-collective-bargaining]].
- **Different from [[platform-cooperatives]]** in that governance is via **elected officials**, not member-owners. Scale is driven by geographic default rather than by voluntary member recruitment.
- **Similar in ambition to [[data-cooperatives|buyer-side data cooperatives]]** in that both aggregate individual bargaining power to counter a large-supplier-side concentration (utilities vs data platforms). The CCA is the public-agency analogue; the data coop is the membership-organisation analogue. *(editorial / synthesis)*

## Scope and limitations

- **US-only.** The "community choice aggregation" label is US-specific, though aggregation of electricity demand by municipalities occurs under different names elsewhere (not captured here).
- **Deregulated-state precondition.** CCAs cannot form in the 33 regulated-monopoly states without first passing deregulation — a major political lift.
- **Electricity-only.** NOPEC's Ohio model extends to natural gas, but CCAs are not authorised for telecom, insurance, or pharmaceutical aggregation in any captured US jurisdiction.
- **Utility-relationship friction.** Incumbent utilities have opposed CCA formation in several states, including through litigation and customer-disclosure-rule proposals. Not detailed in the captured sources but flagged at other points in the article (not included here).
- **Green-power focus is not default.** Only ~13% of CCAs nationwide offer green power as their primary focus; cost is the dominant driver outside California.

## Design-input implications (editorial)

- **CCA is an under-replicated counter-power template.** The opt-out + public-agency + deregulated-market combination produced 1,850+ municipal CCAs in 9 states within ~25 years of the first Massachusetts law. No adjacent consumer market has been subject to comparable treatment. [[regulatory-responses|Design-input candidates]] on this wiki (consumer pricing observatory, buyer-side data coop) assume *opt-in* membership. An opt-out CCA-for-other-sectors policy lift would materially change the uptake-vs-democratic-control tradeoff Ada Lovelace 2021 documents on [[data-cooperatives]].
- **Political economy is the binding constraint, not mechanism design.** The CCA law itself is short and formulaic; the hard part is state-level electricity deregulation and incumbent-utility pushback. The same is likely true for analogue default-aggregation rules in other sectors.

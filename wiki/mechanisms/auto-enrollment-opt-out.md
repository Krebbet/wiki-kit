# Auto-Enrollment with Opt-Out (State Auto-IRAs)

**State-mandated auto-IRA retirement programmes** — starting with **OregonSaves** (launched November 2017) and including **CalSavers** (California) — are the clearest live US implementation of **opt-out default aggregation outside electricity**. Each state programme automatically enrolls workers at participating employers into a Roth IRA with a default contribution rate, and workers must actively opt out within 30 days to decline. The non-electricity analogue this wiki has been looking for to [[community-choice-aggregation|CCA]] ([[possible-strategic-levers|strategic lever #29 — CCA-equivalent default opt-out port]]).

## Structure (CalSavers primary; OregonSaves WebSearch preamble)

Per the captured CalSavers `enrollment.html` primary page and WebSearch preamble:

- **Legal form:** state-created retirement programme; employers with specified thresholds are mandated to either offer a private retirement plan or register with the state programme.
- **Enrollment default:** auto-enrollment after 30 days if the worker does not opt out. Workers can opt out and opt back in at any time.
- **Default contribution rate:** **5% of gross pay** (both OregonSaves and CalSavers).
- **Account type:** Roth IRA (post-tax payroll deductions).
- **Launch dates:** OregonSaves November 2017 (first in the US); CalSavers subsequently.
- **Worker rights:** change contribution rate at any time; opt out at any time.

## Mechanism parallel to CCA

| | [[community-choice-aggregation|CCA (electricity)]] | Auto-IRA (retirement savings) |
|---|---|---|
| Aggregating entity | Local public agency (municipality / JPA) | State-created retirement programme administrator |
| Default behaviour | Residents auto-enrolled in CCA | Workers auto-enrolled at 5% contribution |
| Opt-out right | At any time | Within 30 days (then at any time) |
| Market the mechanism restructures | Retail electricity | Retirement savings / IRA |
| Launch vehicle | State enabling legislation (nine US states) | State mandate (multiple US states, growing) |
| Direct consumer effect | Customers get CCA-chosen generation mix at CCA-negotiated rates | Workers without employer-sponsored plans get Roth IRAs at default contribution |
| Incumbent response | Incumbent utilities retain transmission + distribution | Financial-services industry retains option to offer private plans |

## Counter-power mechanism

Auto-IRAs are **default-restructuring** as counter-power — not a direct bargaining or observatory tool, but a policy-vehicle change that shifts the default behaviour for an entire category of transactions. Structural properties:

1. **Opt-out > opt-in uptake dramatically.** The documented reason public-agency opt-out aggregation works at scale (15% of US electricity customers via CCAs per [[community-choice-aggregation]]; millions of workers via state auto-IRAs) is the default behavioural-economics effect. Voluntary opt-in programmes for the same populations would reach a tiny fraction of the auto-enrolled base. This is the primary strategic advantage the mechanism offers.
2. **Public-agency governance avoids the [[consumer-collective-bargaining|GPO funding-mechanism critique]].** Neither CCAs nor state auto-IRAs are funded by administrative fees from the supplier side they negotiate against — so neither has the Elhauge / DOJ-FTC 2004 incentive-incompatibility concern.
3. **Incumbent preservation of specific functions.** CCAs preserve the incumbent utility's transmission + distribution role. Auto-IRAs preserve the option for employers to offer a private plan *instead of* using the state programme. Neither construct attempts to eliminate the incumbent — they restructure the default while preserving the incumbent's structurally sound roles.
4. **Category-bound enabling legislation.** Each applies only to the specific market its enabling statute names. Extending to a new sector requires new legislation. No generic "opt-out default aggregation statute" exists.

## Relevance for dynamic-pricing strategy

*(editorial / synthesis.)*

Auto-IRAs close the reference-layer gap flagged on [[community-choice-aggregation|the CCA page]]: "No comparable opt-out default exists for broadband, insurance, pharmaceuticals, or data-privacy." *That claim is false at a category level — auto-IRAs are the opt-out default in the retirement-savings category in the states that have enacted them.* The accurate statement is that **opt-out default aggregation works in electricity (CCAs) and retirement savings (auto-IRAs) in the US, and no comparable default exists in broadband / insurance / pharmaceuticals / data-privacy**. The lever [[possible-strategic-levers|#29]] is therefore narrower than the original framing suggested — CCA is not the sole precedent; auto-IRAs are a parallel precedent.

Implications:

1. **Two US precedents strengthens the lever's tractability.** Advocating a default-aggregation statute in a new sector is easier when two separate existing statutory models can be cited. Auto-IRAs in particular demonstrate that the mechanism transfers outside utility markets.
2. **Legal-vehicle mapping is genuinely different from CCA mechanics.** Auto-IRAs ride on the employer-benefits compliance surface (payroll deductions, ERISA landscape, state-specific mandate-or-exempt structure). CCAs ride on utility-deregulation + municipal-authority. Any new default-aggregation statute would need its own legal vehicle, tailored to the category.
3. **The binding constraint is still political economy.** Both auto-IRA adoption (state-by-state) and CCA adoption (9 states over 25+ years) required decades of sustained lobbying and political-coalition work. Neither mechanism is a quick-build technical project — they are multi-year political projects that ride on well-designed legal instruments. A new sector's opt-out-default-aggregation campaign should budget accordingly.

## Scope and limitations

- **Captured source is thin** (CalSavers enrollment page, 4.9KB). Load-bearing claims on mechanism (30-day opt-out, 5% default, Roth IRA structure) are clearly supported. State-by-state rollout scale, worker-participation numbers, and federal-political context are in the WebSearch preamble rather than captured.
- **OregonSaves not directly captured.** The primary-source mechanism claims apply to CalSavers from the capture; OregonSaves mechanism claims (launch date, 5% default, 30-day opt-out, Roth IRA) come from the WebSearch preamble. These are likely accurate but less strongly source-traced than the CalSavers claims on this page.
- **No retirement-policy reference layer in this wiki.** Auto-IRAs are introduced here as a supporting case for a single lever; the fuller retirement-policy context (ERISA, IRA limits, secure-act changes) is out of scope.

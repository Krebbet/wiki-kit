# Consumer Collective Bargaining

Consumer-side collective bargaining aggregates buyer demand to negotiate better prices or terms from suppliers. Distinct from **labour** collective bargaining (workers negotiating with employers) and from [[collective-bargaining-for-data|data-side collective bargaining]] (information producers negotiating with AI operators). Covers four structurally distinct mechanisms: **standing group purchasing organisations (GPOs)**, **member-owned consumer cooperatives**, **transactional group-buying platforms**, and **public-agency aggregation** (see [[community-choice-aggregation]] for the energy-sector variant).

## Taxonomy

| Mechanism | Who initiates | Who owns | Duration | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **GPO** | Professional intermediary | Member-buyers or third-party | Standing contract portfolio | Vizient, Premier, HealthTrust (healthcare); Foodbuy (foodservice) |
| **Consumer cooperative** | Members | Members (one vote each) | Standing | [[park-slope-food-coop]], [[rei]], Rochdale Society |
| **Transactional group-buy** | Individual consumer or platform | Platform operator | Per-deal, ~24h windows | Pinduoduo, Groupon, Tuángòu forums |
| **Public-agency aggregation** | Local government | Public | Standing default-service contracts | [[community-choice-aggregation]] (US electricity) |

The four differ materially on **who captures the bargaining gain**: GPO admin-fee model captures it partly at the intermediary; cooperatives distribute it as dividend / lower prices to members; transactional group-buy captures it at the platform plus partially to the consumer; public agencies return it as lower rates to residents of the jurisdiction.

## Group purchasing organisations (GPOs)

### Scale and history (Wikipedia + Blair & Durrance 2013)

- **Origin:** first healthcare GPO founded 1910 (Hospital Bureau of New York).
- **Growth:** 10 in 1962, 40 in 1974, **600+ by 2011** (per Blair & Durrance 2013 citing HIGPA 2011); **~90% of all US hospital purchases** go through a GPO (Blair & Durrance 2013).
- **Adoption:** per Wikipedia, **96% of US acute-care hospitals, 98% of community hospitals, 97% of not-for-profit non-governmental hospitals** held at least one GPO membership by 2007.
- **Volume:** the two largest GPOs (Novation, Premier) negotiated ~**$70 billion** in contracts in 2011 (Blair & Durrance 2013).
- **Purchase share:** US hospitals make ~**72% of their purchases via GPO contracts** (per Wikipedia, citing a 2009 *Supply & Demand Chain Executive* report).
- **Cost-savings estimates:** Schneller (2009, cited by Blair & Durrance) estimated GPOs save the US healthcare industry **~$36 billion per year**.

### Antitrust and regulatory framing

- **1986:** US Congress granted healthcare GPOs **"safe harbor" from federal anti-kickback statutes** after industry lobbying. Admin-fee cap of 3.0% of sales volume; fees above that must be disclosed to members per 1991 HHS regulations.
- **2002:** GAO study found GPOs did not always reduce hospital costs — in some cases **increased them by up to 37%** (per Wikipedia).
- **2002:** US Senate Judiciary Antitrust Subcommittee imposed stricter standards; required GPOs to adopt a Code of Conduct.
- **DOJ/FTC 2004 joint statement** (per Blair & Durrance 2013) identified three policy concerns:
  1. **Monopsony power.** GPOs must aggregate enough purchasing volume to negotiate lower prices — but monopsony power reduces social welfare.
  2. **Exclusionary contracting.** Sole-source contracts, minimum-purchase commitments, volume-based discounting, and loyalty rebates may foreclose equally efficient competitors.
  3. **Funding-mechanism incentive incompatibility.** Most GPOs are funded by admin fees paid by the suppliers they negotiate against. This creates a structural conflict of interest: the GPO may not minimise hospital cost if its own revenue rises with supplier volume.

### Academic debate

Two clear positions (both documented by Blair & Durrance 2013):

- **Elhauge (2002, 2003):** critical. Argues GPO contracting practices (sole-source, minimum-purchase, loyalty rebates) are exclusionary and foreclose rivals.
- **Hovenkamp (2002, 2004):** supportive. Argues medical-device markets are largely unconcentrated and the contracting practices are only anticompetitive under narrow conditions.
- **Blair & Durrance (2013):** propose an "all-or-nothing monopsony" model and conclude GPOs are **procompetitive** on net; acknowledge the funding-mechanism concern.
- **Litan & Singer (2010):** empirically found GPOs did **not** secure the best price in a competitive-bid analysis.

Consumer-group concern (separately noted on Wikipedia's GPO page): three GPOs — **Vizient, Premier, HealthTrust** — manage procurement for **~90% of medical equipment**, leaving small producers and small providers with little bargaining power. This is the concentration-within-the-intermediary-layer critique.

### Vertical vs horizontal GPO types (Wikipedia)

- **Vertical GPOs:** healthcare, foodservice/grocery, hospitality, manufacturing, legal, non-profit.
- **Horizontal GPOs:** cross-industry indirect-spend. Per a SpendMatters analysis (Wikipedia citation), **15–20% of Fortune 1000** companies use buying consortiums and typically see **10%+ savings** in categories they consolidate.

## Consumer cooperatives

### Rochdale lineage (Wikipedia Consumers' co-operative)

- Foundational model: **Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers**, founded 1844 in Rochdale, England by 28 cotton-mill workers. Established the "Rochdale Principles" — democratic decision-making, fair pricing, profit-sharing — that still anchor the International Cooperative Alliance's Statement of Cooperative Identity (see [[data-cooperatives]] for the seven principles in their modern form).
- Rose with the Industrial Revolution's labour movement; dozens of Owen-inspired societies formed across England in the 1820s–30s "often as buying clubs or stores providing foodstuffs and necessities at wholesale rates to members" (per Wikipedia).

### Live US cases captured in this wiki

- **[[park-slope-food-coop]]** — founded 1973, Brooklyn; ~17,000 members as of July 2025. Member-labour model: each member contributes **2h45m every 6 weeks** in exchange for shopping rights. Store sells at a **25% markup over wholesale** (vs 26–100% at supermarkets). Incorporated as a New York State cooperative corporation.
- **[[rei]]** — founded 1938 in Seattle; 181 retail stores across 41 states. Member-owned consumers' cooperative, one vote per member, lifetime-membership fee currently $30. Historically issued patronage dividend refunds; rebranded to "Co-op Member Reward" (store credit, not cash) in March 2022. Largest US consumer cooperative.

### Supporting cases (referenced in sources, not standalone captures)

- **The Co-op UK** — one of the world's largest consumer cooperative federations; operates 5,500+ branches including Co-op Food.
- **Rochdale Society** itself (1844) — the institutional anchor.
- **Food-cooperative buying clubs** — specially structured preorder groups where members place large collective orders and receive them at a centralised point. Persisted as a live pattern in US cooperative grocery.

## Transactional group-buying

### Tuángòu / Pinduoduo (Wikipedia Group buying)

- **Origin:** China — **Tuán Gòu (团购)** = "team buying" — a bargaining tradition where groups approach a vendor to collectively bargain (haggle) for discounts on a specific item.
- **Online growth in China:** 1,215 group-buying sites by August 2010, up from 100 in March 2010 (per Wikipedia).
- **Pinduoduo model** (per WebSearch preamble, not captured directly): teams of ≥5 consumers form within a 24-hour window to unlock a lower price; shoppers share deals via WeChat, turning ordinary consumers into advocates. Rivals Alibaba in China.

### Groupon and the US daily-deal arc (Wikipedia Group buying + WebSearch preamble)

- **Mercata (2000)** — Paul Allen / Microsoft-backed US attempt, shut 2001.
- **Groupon** — peaked early 2010s; rejected a **$6 billion Google acquisition offer in 2011**. Revenue declined **80% from $3B in 2014 to $600M in 2022** (WebSearch preamble summary, not captured).
- **Google Offers** (launched 2011), **Facebook Deals** (launched in 5 European countries January 2011), **LivingSocial** — all pursued similar models.
- **500+ group-buying sites worldwide** by August 2010 per SmartMoney (Wikipedia citation).

### The failure pattern (editorial / synthesis)

The Chinese Tuángòu model persisted and scaled (Pinduoduo) while US daily-deal platforms collapsed. Contributing factors from WebSearch preamble on the Groupon decline (not directly captured — treat as lower-trust context): post-2010 economic recovery reduced consumer urgency for deep discounts; merchant dissatisfaction because only ~20% of deal users returned for full-price purchases; merchant exhaustion by 2020; small-business advertising alternatives on Facebook/Instagram eroded the value proposition. Pinduoduo's integration with WeChat's pervasive social graph (no US equivalent) appears structural.

**Key structural observation:** transactional group-buying captures value at the **platform layer**, not at the consumer layer. The consumer gets a one-off discount; the platform gets the recurring merchant relationship. In that respect it is closer to advertising than to consumer cooperation. *(editorial / synthesis)*

## Public-agency aggregation

**[[community-choice-aggregation]]** is the US electricity-sector instance: local governments aggregate residential/business/municipal electricity demand within their jurisdiction and negotiate supply contracts on behalf of residents. Opt-out by default. Serves ~15% of Americans across 1,850+ municipalities in 9 states as of 2024. Distinct from GPOs (public agency, not membership intermediary) and from cooperatives (elected officials, not one-vote-per-member). See the dedicated page for full treatment.

Consumer collective bargaining is the **oldest** counter-power mechanism in the wiki (Rochdale 1844 predates every other anchor page by a century+). It is also the **most structurally diverse**: healthcare GPOs, Brooklyn food coops, Chinese social-commerce platforms, and California energy aggregators are all instances of "aggregate demand, negotiate better price" but they differ on nearly every other dimension. *(editorial / synthesis)*

## Design-input implications (editorial)

- **The GPO funding-mechanism concern is generic.** Any consumer-side intermediary funded by admin fees from the supplier side it negotiates against carries the same incentive incompatibility. This mirrors the [[paypal-honey|Honey extractive-drift pattern]] in [[transparency-tools]]: a "consumer" tool whose revenue comes from the supplier side is structurally misaligned. Cooperative governance (members own the intermediary) and public-agency governance (voters indirectly own) both address it.
- **The Rochdale Principles still carry weight.** Per the International Cooperative Alliance, all modern cooperative definitions (including [[data-cooperatives|data cooperatives]] and [[platform-cooperatives|platform cooperatives]]) trace back to Rochdale. A pricing-side buyer coop designed today would inherit the same structural options (open membership, democratic control, member economic participation, autonomy, education, cooperation, community concern).
- **The Tuángòu-WeChat vs Groupon-US contrast** suggests that transactional group-buying is more viable when **embedded in a standing social network** rather than sold as a standalone service. A US pricing-observatory or buyer-coop design that assumes standalone uptake may face the same merchant- and consumer-exhaustion failure mode Groupon hit. *(editorial / synthesis)*
- **CCA-style public-agency aggregation is under-exploited outside electricity.** Nine US states authorise CCAs for electricity; no comparable default-aggregation authorisation exists for broadband, insurance, or pharmaceuticals. This is an open policy gap.

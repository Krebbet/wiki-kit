# Platform Cooperatives

Platform cooperatives are cooperatively-owned, democratically-governed online businesses that establish two-sided markets (website, mobile app, protocol) owned by the people who depend on them most — workers, users, and other stakeholders. The term was coined by Trebor Scholz (New School) in December 2014 as a direct counter-framing to "platform capitalism" — Uber, Airbnb, TaskRabbit, and similar venture-capital-funded platforms. This page treats platform cooperatives as a counter-power mechanism: an exit-pathway alternative to extractive digital platforms, sitting alongside regulatory responses and consumer backlash on the wiki's counter-power map.

## What makes a platform cooperative

Three features distinguish platform cooperatives from conventional platforms (OECD 2023):

1. **Shared ownership** — workers, users, customers, or institutional partners collectively own the platform rather than outside investors.
2. **Democratic decision-making** — members vote on design, operation, and management; one member, one vote in most models (Mondragon 10 Basic Co-operative Principles, per which Mondragon operates and which platform-coops inherit — see [[mondragon]]).
3. **Member-priority business model** — member interests shape the business, not investor returns. Follows the Rochdale Principles of 1844.

Wikipedia distinguishes them further from adjacent movements: they differ from Yochai Benkler's commons-based peer production (which is non-market and radically distributed) and from Wikipedia-style volunteer projects (which rely on unpaid labour). Platform cooperatives are for-revenue businesses with democratic ownership, not gift-economy projects.

## Typology

Two orthogonal typologies appear across sources:

**By industry** (Wikipedia): transportation, on-demand labour, journalism, music, creative projects, timebank, film, home health care, photography, data cooperatives, marketplaces.

**By membership composition** (OECD 2023 — four types):
1. **Worker cooperatives** — workers own the platform (e.g. [[drivers-cooperative]] — NYC rideshare).
2. **Producer cooperatives** — producers selling via the platform own it (e.g. Stocksy — stock photographers, 50–75% license revenue to contributors per Shareable).
3. **Multi-stakeholder cooperatives** — combine workers, users, partners (e.g. [[coopcycle]] — a federation bundling local worker coops with the technology commons).
4. **Data cooperatives** — members pool their data for collective benefit, not investor monetisation (e.g. MIDATA.coop in Switzerland, jointly created by ETH Zurich and Bern University to govern personal health data).

## Founding and institutional development

- **December 2014** — Trebor Scholz publishes "Platform Cooperativism vs. the Sharing Economy" on Medium, coining the term.
- **2015** — Scholz publishes "Platform Cooperativism: Challenging the Corporate Sharing Economy" (the Rosa Luxemburg Stiftung NYC pamphlet, published in five languages) and convenes with Nathan Schneider the New School conference "Platform Cooperativism: The Internet, Ownership, Democracy."
- **2016** — Book *Ours to Hack and to Own: The Rise of Platform Cooperativism* (Scholz and Schneider, eds.).
- **November 2016** — Platform Cooperativism Consortium (PCC) launched at The New School at the "Building the Cooperative Internet" conference.
- **2019** — Institute for the Cooperative Digital Economy (ICDE) founded at The New School.

**Precursors named in the literature:** Italian Legacoop's 2012 "Cooperative Commons" manifesto; Mayo Fuster Morell's 2012 essay on digital commons horizons; Michel Bauwens / P2P Foundation's "open co-operativism" formulations; Marjorie Kelly's *Owning Our Future* (2012) distinction between democratic and extractive ownership.

## The "capital conundrum"

The most durable critique across sources: platform cooperatives cannot raise venture capital (cooperative ownership is incompatible with the VC returns model), and struggle to match the scale and network effects of VC-funded competitors.

- **Nick Srnicek** (*Platform Capitalism*, 2017): "Even if all that software would be made open-source, a platform like Facebook would still have the weight of its existing data, network effects, and financial resources to fight off any co-op arrival."
- **Rufus Pollock**: coops face slow decision-making, inability to raise equity capital, and a risk of becoming exclusive clubs (e.g. a rideshare coop controlled by drivers at the expense of riders).
- **Evgeny Morozov**: "There is also no good reason to believe that this local cooperative can actually build a self-driving car: this requires massive investment and a dedicated infrastructure to harvest and analyze all of the data."
- **OECD 2023**: "Offers unique solutions with positive impact, but their scale remains limited."

**Counter-arguments within the sources:**
- **Arun Sundararajan** (MIT Press, 2016): cooperatives are efficient "when there isn't a great deal of diversity in the levels of contribution across workers, when the level of external competition is low, and when there isn't the need for frequent investments in response to technological change." Cab driving is a reasonable fit.
- **Federation as scaling strategy** (OECD 2023): CoopCycle demonstrates that local cooperatives can share technology, marketing, and R&D via second-level federations, capturing network effects without centralised ownership.
- **Alternative funding models** (Wikipedia): Fairshare (differentiated voting rights across founders/workers/users/investors), crypto-coin crowdfunding, philanthropic investment, "provider stock ownership programs."

This tension — Scholz and the PCC frame platform cooperativism as necessary and possible; Srnicek/Morozov frame it as marginal; OECD takes a mid-position — is the main live debate in the field and is documented here as a known non-resolution rather than treated as a position of the wiki.

## Legal vehicles and policy support

- **Colorado — "the Delaware of cooperative law"** (per Wikipedia). The Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act (UCLAA) creates a **Limited Cooperative Association (LCA)** structure allowing a second class of "investor-members" with distinct voting rights. Critics (cited as Moore and Tambini 2021, and others) call this "coop washing" and a "Trojan horse," but it solves part of the capital conundrum and has enabled growth.
- **Berkeley Worker Cooperative Revolving Loan Fund** (2016): public capital below-market-rate loans to worker-owned businesses, including platform coops.
- **Barcelona — "La Communificadora"** (2015): entrepreneurship training for platform coops, plus match-funding from the Social Economy and Consumption Commission. 120 policy proposals produced at the 2016 BarCola event for European governments; some implemented at the city level.
- **French cooperative law** (OECD 2023 Box 3.2): adapted to platform cooperatives.
- **NYC — Brad Lander 2016 report** "Raising the Floor for Workers in the Gig Economy" presented platform cooperativism as a gig-worker-protection model.
- **USDA** — September/October 2016 *Rural Cooperatives* magazine endorsed platform cooperatives as a rural-America counter-power tool.
- **UK — Corbyn 2016 Digital Democracy Manifesto** proposed National Investment Bank financing for platform cooperatives.

## Industries where platform coops have traction

Named in sources:
- **Rideshare / taxi** — Drivers Cooperative (NYC, see [[drivers-cooperative]]), Co-op Taxi Edmonton (1,200 drivers, 4M rides/year), Union Taxi Denver, PDX Yellow Cab Portland, VTC Cab Paris, Eva Coop Montreal.
- **Bike delivery** — CoopCycle federation (see [[coopcycle]]; 72 coops in 12 countries).
- **Stock photography / creative** — Stocksy (1,800 artists in 80+ countries).
- **E-commerce** — Fairmondo (Germany, 2013, cooperative eBay alternative).
- **Carsharing** — Modo (Vancouver, 1997, 16,000 members, 500-vehicle fleet), Tapazz (Belgium).
- **Neighbourhood goods-sharing** — Peerby (Netherlands).
- **Data cooperatives** — MIDATA.coop Switzerland (health data).
- **Social enterprises / freelancer networks** — Enspiral (New Zealand).
- **Disaster response / mutual aid** — NeedsMap (Türkiye — coordinated solidarity during natural disasters and COVID-19).
- **Public transportation** — Yatri and AuSa (India — supporting public transportation networks, per OECD Box 3.3).

## Relationship to the wiki's broader theme

Platform cooperativism is the most developed **exit-pathway** counter-power mechanism against platform capitalism. It does not primarily work by regulating extractive platforms (see [[regulatory-responses]]) nor by helping consumers detect surveillance pricing (see [[surveillance-pricing-retail]]); it works by offering an ownership-level alternative. For the wiki's framing: dynamic and surveillance pricing are extractive *outputs* of platform capitalism; platform cooperatives are a structural exit from platform capitalism altogether. See [[dynamic-pricing-overview]] for the framing.

## Design-input candidates *(editorial — not source content)*

Surfaced during research, relevant to the user's "what could I build" interest:

- **Consumer data cooperative for pricing surveillance.** Mirror MIDATA.coop's health-data model for the personalised-pricing problem. Consumers contribute anonymised price observations across retailers; the cooperative publishes statistical signatures of price discrimination and returns a "fairness score" per retailer to members. Legal vehicle: Colorado LCA.
- **Federation-first architecture for any new consumer-side tool.** CoopCycle's second-level cooperative pattern is the answer to the capital conundrum: build shared tech / marketing / R&D at the federation layer, let local co-ops own last-mile operations. Applicable to tenant unions (post-RealPage), buyer clubs, or coordination apps.
- **Rideshare-adjacent paratransit / NEMT coop** as the Drivers Cooperative example shows — picking a sub-market where Sundararajan's conditions (low worker-diversity, low external competition, low technology change) actually hold.
- **Legal-structure scouting:** before building, check whether a Colorado LCA, a French SCIC (cooperative of collective interest), or a Spanish Mondragon-style multi-stakeholder coop fits the use case. Legal form materially affects access to capital.

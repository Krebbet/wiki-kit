# Buyer Cartels & Antitrust

Mechanism page for the **antitrust treatment of buyer-side coordination** — the legal-economic frame that determines whether a wiki-documented consumer-side aggregation lever is per se illegal, rule-of-reason defensible, or affirmatively permitted. Anchored on OECD 2022 *Purchasing Power and Buyers' Cartels* (OECD Competition Committee Background Note). Critical for any strategy-layer plan derived from the wiki: many proposed levers (#1, #4, #14, #16, #26 on [[possible-strategic-levers]]) are *structurally* buyer cartels under antitrust law and could trigger enforcement under the wrong framing. This page documents the per-jurisdiction landscape, the rule-of-reason safe harbours, and the pattern of recent enforcement so the strategy layer can map levers to legal risk.

## Why this matters for the wiki's mandate

The wiki documents and proposes consumer-side aggregation mechanisms — [[consumer-collective-bargaining]], [[data-cooperatives]], [[collective-bargaining-for-data]], [[community-choice-aggregation]] — and a strategy layer of 29 levers, several of which (per [[possible-strategic-levers]]) coordinate consumer purchasing or refusal-to-purchase decisions. Antitrust law treats coordinated buyer behaviour through three distinct lenses: monopsony abuse, joint purchasing, and explicit buyer cartels. **The legal exposure of a lever depends on which lens applies, which depends on framing and structure choices the wiki's strategy layer controls.**

## The three buyer-side market-power categories

| Category | Mechanism | Antitrust treatment |
|---|---|---|
| **Monopsony power** | One or few buyers face many sellers with limited alternatives. Buyers reduce demand to depress prices below competitive levels → deadweight loss (symmetrical to monopoly). | Buyers' cartels achieving monopsony power are **per se illegal** in US, EU, Korea, UK, Australia, Singapore. |
| **Bargaining power** | Few buyers and sellers negotiate bilaterally. Can be pro-competitive (countervailing power) or harmful (innovation disincentives, vertical foreclosure, waterbed effects, risk transfer). | Rule-of-reason analysis. Not per se illegal. |
| **Joint purchasing agreements** | Buyers' groups pool purchases for cost efficiencies (economies of scale, transaction cost savings). | More favourable treatment with safe harbours: **EU < 15% combined market share, US < 20% (35% for healthcare)**. |

## Per-jurisdiction enforcement landscape (OECD 2022 synthesis)

**United States.** Buyers' cartels per se illegal under Sherman Act §1. Recent enforcement surge in **labour markets**: wage-fixing and no-poach agreements treated as buyers' cartels, with criminal prosecutions of individuals since 2021. Joint purchasing safe harbour at <20% combined market share (35% for healthcare per Statements of Antitrust Enforcement Policy in Health Care).

**European Union.** Buyers' cartels per se illegal under Art. 101 TFEU. Joint purchasing safe harbour at <15% combined market share. Recent enforcement: battery recycling cartel, ethylene buyers' cartel. French authorities challenged supermarket joint purchasing >15%, requiring commitments.

**Korea, Japan, Hungary.** Have **"superior bargaining power" statutes** that don't require dominance — alternative enforcement frame for buyer-power abuse outside the per-se cartel category.

**Canada.** Notable outlier. Section 90 of the Competition Act (2009) moved buyers' cartels to **civil rule-of-reason** review — broader latitude than per se.

**Australia.** **Class exemption for collective bargaining by small businesses** (June 2021) — recognising that small-business collective bargaining rarely causes substantial competition lessening and generates public benefits. **Mandatory collective boycotts** explicitly excluded from the exemption.

**United Kingdom, Singapore.** Per-se treatment aligned with EU / US approach.

## Critical statistical claim

OECD 2022: **buyers' cartels represent only 3–8% of detected international cartels**, despite economic theory suggesting they may be *easier to sustain* than sellers' cartels (lower deviation incentives, since buyers benefit from lower prices). The under-detection reflects enforcement prioritisation, not lower actual prevalence. **Median documented undercharge in buyers' cartels: 19.7%** (Connor 2021 meta-analysis cited in the report).

## What pro-competitive joint purchasing looks like

The OECD report identifies three load-bearing characteristics that distinguish defensible joint purchasing from impermissible buyer cartels:

1. **Documented cost efficiencies.** Economies of scale, transaction cost savings, supplier-side investment in serving the joint purchaser. The efficiencies must be substantial and verifiable.
2. **Market-share discipline.** Combined buyer-side market share below the safe-harbour threshold (EU 15%, US 20% / 35% healthcare). Above the threshold, rule-of-reason scrutiny intensifies.
3. **No coordinated downstream pricing.** The buyers may coordinate *purchasing* but must not coordinate *resale prices* to consumers — otherwise the buyer cartel becomes a seller cartel laundered through joint purchasing.

## Welfare-standard tension

The OECD report identifies a core policy tension: under the dominant **consumer welfare standard**, purchasing-power harms are **under-enforced** if downstream market power is absent or cost reductions are passed through to consumers. Under a **competition-process standard** (protecting competitive structures), purchaser coordination is presumed harmful regardless of consumer-side pass-through.

**Implication for the wiki's strategy layer.** Consumer-side aggregation lever proposals are most defensible under the consumer welfare standard *if* they can demonstrate consumer pass-through of efficiencies. Under a stricter competition-process standard, the same proposals face higher scrutiny. The legal landscape is jurisdiction- and standard-divergent — not a single fixed test.

## Recent precedent worth tracking

- **US labour-market enforcement (2021–)** — wage-fixing and no-poach agreements as buyers' cartels; criminal prosecutions of individuals. The doctrinal evolution is rapid.
- **EU battery recycling + ethylene cartel cases** — buyer-side enforcement at scale.
- **Korea iron scrap case** — superior bargaining power statute applied.
- **French supermarket joint purchasing challenge** — above-threshold joint purchasing required commitments.

These are tracked in part on [[regulatory-responses]]; this page is the cross-cutting framework the per-jurisdiction precedents fit into.

## Caveats

1. **Detection bias.** The 3–8% buyers'-cartel detection rate likely under-counts actual prevalence due to enforcement prioritisation.
2. **Welfare-standard debate is unresolved.** Legal exposure depends on jurisdiction-specific evolution.
3. **Safe harbours are not absolute.** EU 15% and US 20% / 35% thresholds permit extraordinary-circumstances exceptions.
4. **Bargaining-power harms are fact-intensive.** Unlike monopsony, bargaining-power analysis under rule-of-reason is authority-dependent.
5. **Not legal advice.** The wiki uses this framework as a strategic risk assessment tool, not determinative guidance.

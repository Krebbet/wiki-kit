# NOYB (None of Your Business)

NOYB – European Center for Digital Rights — **Austrian non-profit, founded 2017, Vienna**, co-founded by privacy activist Max Schrems. Funded by **4,400+ supporting members** (annual donations + recurring support). NOYB operates a **strategic-litigation and mass-complaint playbook** against private-sector privacy violations. Closest live implementation on this wiki of [[possible-strategic-levers|strategic lever #26 (consumer union with dues + war chest)]] — except that NOYB's portfolio is rights-enforcement rather than pricing, it is otherwise structurally what the lever proposes.

## Structure

- **Legal form:** non-profit, Austrian.
- **Founding:** established 2017, Vienna, after a funding period during which it raised **€250,000/year annual donations** by supporting members.
- **Current funding:** 4,400+ supporting members.
- **Co-founders:** Max Schrems (Austrian lawyer / privacy activist); Petra Leupold; Christof Tschohl.
- **Legal standing:** **Article 80 of GDPR** foresees that non-profit organisations can take action or represent users. NOYB is also recognised as a **"qualified entity" to bring consumer class actions in Belgium.**
- **Focus:** "While many privacy organisations focus attention on governments, NOYB puts its focus on privacy issues and privacy violations in the private sector" (per the captured Wikipedia source).

## Mechanism

Four observed repeat patterns from the captured case history:

1. **Strategic individual litigation taken to CJEU.** Max Schrems vs Facebook (Schrems I, 2015 invalidation of Safe Harbor; Schrems II, 2020 invalidation of Privacy Shield). Individual complaints escalated through Irish DPC to the Court of Justice of the European Union, producing EU-wide case law.
2. **Mass-complaint filings.** Within hours of GDPR taking effect on 25 May 2018, NOYB filed complaints against Facebook, WhatsApp, Instagram, and Google over "forced consent" (Article 7(4) violations). Post-Schrems II, NOYB filed **101 complaints against EU/EEA companies using Google Analytics or Facebook Connect** to force enforcement across the union.
3. **Action-forcing pressure on regulators.** When regulators delay, NOYB files complaints-for-inaction. The Swedish DPA (IMY) had sat on a Spotify complaint for 4 years; NOYB filed a complaint for inaction in 2022; the IMY subsequently issued a **58M SEK (~€5M) GDPR fine against Spotify**. NOYB also published an open letter in 2020 accusing the Irish DPC of having 10 meetings with Facebook before GDPR took effect.
4. **Novel legal-route experimentation.** The 2020 Apple IDFA case was filed under **Article 5(3) of the ePrivacy Directive** rather than GDPR — explicitly because this lets German and Spanish DPAs directly fine Apple without GDPR cross-border cooperation mechanics.

## Quantified outcomes (per captured source)

- **Schrems II (July 16, 2020):** CJEU invalidated Privacy Shield — the EU's data-transfer mechanism covering most US-EU data flows. Direct consequence of Max Schrems's and NOYB's case.
- **French CNIL issued a €50 million fine against Google** over forced-consent.
- **Swedish IMY issued a ~€5 million GDPR fine against Spotify** after NOYB's complaint-for-inaction.
- **Austrian Data Protection Authority (DSB), January 12, 2022:** decided that continued use of Google Analytics violates GDPR — affecting most EU websites.

(The captured source continues with additional 2020-era cases — this page lists the most outcome-legible ones.)

## Counter-power mechanism

NOYB's playbook is the working implementation of the **standing-institution-with-war-chest** lever. Specific design choices a pricing-side analogue could adopt:

1. **Member-funded, strategically litigated.** 4,400 supporting members × sustained annual contribution is enough to fund Schrems-II-class cases through the CJEU. A consumer pricing-union at similar scale could pursue comparable cases under US state unfair-practices laws, EU UCP Directive, or equivalents.
2. **Article-80 / qualified-entity standing.** EU privacy law explicitly anticipates NGO-led consumer action. Any equivalent pricing-side action would need equivalent legal standing — the US has some analogues (state-AG-coordinated consumer-advocacy amici; California's UCL; the FTC's section-5 consumer-complaint pipeline) but no single instrument is as clean as GDPR Art. 80.
3. **Complaint-for-inaction against regulators.** This tactic is specific to NOYB and has worked repeatedly. A US-analogue consumer-union would need to identify the equivalent pressure point: FTC ombudsman? State AG failure-to-enforce statutes? *(Open question — not captured.)*
4. **Novel-route litigation experimentation.** NOYB does not always litigate under GDPR — the Apple IDFA case used ePrivacy Directive specifically because it provides a faster enforcement path. A strategic-litigation pricing-counter-org needs similar legal-surface fluency to pick the most advantageous vehicle per case.
5. **Public open-letter pressure is a complement to litigation, not a substitute.** The Irish DPC open letter is a reputational instrument that supplements the legal ones. A pricing-side org should plan for the same mix.

## Relevance for dynamic-pricing strategy

*(editorial / synthesis.)*

NOYB proves that a **small-member-base (thousands, not millions) strategic-litigation advocacy organisation can shift continental law** in a specialised domain. Schrems II invalidated Privacy Shield — one of the most consequential transatlantic-data rulings of the 2020s — on the back of a single activist's litigation and an NGO with 4,400 members.

For this wiki's strategic programme ([[possible-strategic-levers]]):

- **Lever #26 (consumer union with dues + war chest)** gets its clearest working template from NOYB, not from [[rei|REI]] or [[consumer-collective-bargaining|US GPOs]]. The advocacy-plus-litigation mix is what gives the mechanism its edge; pure membership cooperatives without litigation capacity produce quieter and slower effects.
- **Legal-vehicle mapping for dynamic-pricing cases is the binding research requirement.** NOYB relies on GDPR Art. 80 + Belgian class-action qualification + ePrivacy Directive as complementary legal vehicles. A pricing-side org needs an equivalent mapped set. This is exactly what [[possible-strategic-levers|lever #29 (CCA-equivalent default opt-out port)]] also requires.
- **Non-pricing issues benefit from pricing-side counter-orgs.** NOYB's targets (forced consent, data transfers, tracking IDs) shape the information-asymmetry substrate that dynamic pricing exploits. A well-funded privacy-advocacy win constrains the inputs to personalised-pricing algorithms. The two lever families are complementary, not alternative.

## Scope and limitations

- **EU-centric.** NOYB's legal instruments are GDPR, ePrivacy Directive, and EU member-state data-protection laws. A US-operating equivalent would need to map onto state-AG standing, FTC / CFPB complaint procedures, and class-action mechanics.
- **Privacy-issue-bound.** NOYB's focus is explicitly privacy, not pricing or consumer-welfare-broadly. The legal-surface overlap with pricing extraction (surveillance pricing → personalisation → GDPR) is real but partial.
- **Capture scope:** The captured Wikipedia source covers roughly 2017–2022 in depth; more recent cases (OpenAI GDPR complaint, cookie-banner enforcement 2022+) are referenced only in preamble. Load-bearing pre-2022 claims are strongly source-traced; post-2022 coverage is less thorough.

## Cross-jurisdictional empirical pattern (April 2026)

NOYB's empirical follow-up showing **83.5% of GDPR Article 15 access requests not properly answered** (April 16, 2026) and the webXray / The Markup California audit showing **80%+ Big Tech non-compliance with the Global Privacy Control opt-out signal** (Google 86%, Microsoft 50%, Meta 69% — April 21, 2026; see [[regulatory-responses]] § GPC compliance audit) form a cross-jurisdictional empirical baseline for compliance-failure under nominally enforced legal opt-out / access-rights regimes. Same structural pattern (high-rate non-compliance with statutory consumer-side data rights), two regulatory regimes (GDPR Art. 15 in EU; CCPA / CPRA + GPC in CA). The pair establishes that **paper rights underdeliver in production at industrial scale** is not a GDPR-specific finding — it generalises. This is load-bearing context for any strategic-litigation org operating against the same compliance surface.

## LinkedIn Art. 15 paywall complaint (May 2026)

noyb filed a complaint with the Austrian Data Protection Authority (DSB) in May 2026 alleging that LinkedIn (Microsoft) violates GDPR Article 15 by making profile-visitor data available for payment via LinkedIn Premium while refusing to supply the same data free of charge in response to a statutory access request.

**Legal theory.** If a company (a) tracks a data category and (b) sells access to the resulting data as a commercial product, it cannot simultaneously invoke data-protection grounds to deny a free Article 15 access request for that same data. LinkedIn's two-track treatment — *you have no right to see who viewed your profile for free; you can pay Premium to see it* — is the contradiction noyb targets. The implicit acknowledgement that the data is accessible (it is sold) defeats the data-protection-grounds refusal.

**Structural significance.** The complaint is a single filing on behalf of one user, but the template value is the load-bearing signal:

1. **Model-complaint portability.** The legal structure (monetisation of data = implicit acknowledgement of accessibility → Art. 15 must be satisfied free of charge) is portable to any platform that paywalls personalised data. noyb has a documented precedent of repeating successful complaint structures at scale (Schrems I → Schrems II; the 101 Google Analytics / Facebook Connect complaint wave; the IMY Spotify action-forcing sequence). If the DSB upholds this theory, the next wave of complaints against other platforms paywalling access to personal data becomes available immediately.

2. **DSAR-coordination design input.** A DSAR-coordination tool could incorporate a templated LinkedIn Art. 15 request citing this legal theory — converting a single noyb enforcement action into a mass-exercise infrastructure. This strengthens [[data-disruption-strategy-map|Tier-1 #1 (DSAR coordination)]] as a build candidate: the LinkedIn paywall complaint provides the first concrete legal theory pinning DSAR enforcement to a platform's own monetisation behaviour, making the entitlement more robust than a pure "data subject has rights" framing.

3. **Tier-1 build signal.** The [[data-disruption-strategy-map]] already designates DSAR-coordination as a Tier-1 build candidate. This complaint strengthens that designation by demonstrating that the legal surface (Art. 15 + monetisation contradiction) is actionable with a filed complaint rather than merely theoretical.

# Park Slope Food Coop

The Park Slope Food Coop (PSFC) is a food cooperative in the Park Slope neighbourhood of Brooklyn, New York. Founded 1973; **~17,000 members as of July 2025**. One of the oldest and largest active food co-ops in the United States, and the clearest live US case of the **[[consumer-collective-bargaining|Rochdale-lineage consumer cooperative]]** model adapted to a modern urban grocery context.

## Structure

- **Legal form:** New York State cooperative corporation.
- **Ownership:** one-vote-per-member. Board of directors: five members elected to staggered three-year terms by and from the membership. The longest-serving General Coordinator (usually co-founder Joe Holtz) serves as a voting *ex officio* member.
- **Governance:** monthly General Meeting (GM) advises the board. The GM agenda is governed by an Agenda Committee; the meeting itself is run by a Chair Committee whose members rotate. Day-to-day operations run by paid employees ("coordinators"); senior coordinators ("General Coordinators") are hired by the General Meeting and approved by the board.
- **Self-description (per foodcoop.com via Wikipedia):** goal is to be a "buying agent to its members, not a selling agent to any industry."

## Economic model

The distinctive feature versus other US food coops is **member labour as a condition of membership**:

- **Work requirement:** each adult member contributes **2 hours 45 minutes every 6 weeks**. Work positions span food processing, inventory, maintenance, office, check-out, receiving and stocking, soup-kitchen prep, and community-organisation labour (e.g., at CHiPS, a local charity).
- **Markup:** **25% over wholesale** (compared to 26–100% at a supermarket, per the Wikipedia capture). Pre-COVID-19 the markup was **21%**; members facing financial hardship can remain at the lower markup.
- **Enabling economics:** per Wikipedia, **members do ~75% of the work**, keeping payroll low — the main mechanism by which the low markup is sustainable.
- **Shopping access:** only members may shop. Non-members may visit as a guest but cannot purchase.

## Scale and timeline

- **1973:** founded by ~10 people including Joe Holtz. Initially subleased space from the Mongoose Community Center.
- **1977:** Mongoose defunct; Coop begins renting directly from the landlord.
- **1978:** began renting 782 Union Street with option to buy.
- **1980:** purchased 782 Union Street after two-year lease and renovations.
- **1988, 1999:** purchased the two buildings immediately to the west; renovations completed 1991 and 2001 respectively.
- **December 2009:** paid off the remaining $707,000 mortgage on all three buildings (pre-paid without penalty; the mortgage holder, National Cooperative Bank, had requested early repayment after the 2008–2009 banking crisis).
- **July 2025:** ~17,000 members (per the captured Wikipedia article).

## Political-action history

The cooperative has a long history of using its collective purchasing as political leverage:

- **Anti-apartheid boycott** (during the South African apartheid regime) — South African goods banned.
- **Pinochet-era Chilean grapes** removed.
- **Nestlé boycott** — over the company's infant-formula promotion displacing breastfeeding.
- **Coca-Cola boycott (since 2004)** — citing labour practices and third-world resource exploitation. Includes Minute Maid and Odwalla.
- **Flaum Appetizing Products boycott (since 2010)** — for labour-law violations at a local kosher producer.
- **2008:** GM resolved to stop selling bottled water and stop providing plastic checkout bags.
- **2016:** GM changed rules so that any future boycott now requires a **75% super-majority** to pass.
- **Post-2023 Gaza war:** a sustained debate over joining the BDS movement; two factions emerged (PSFC Members for Palestine; Coop 4 Unity). Allegations of antisemitism / discrimination led to human-rights-agency complaints. In June 2025, board elections became a proxy battle; neither Members for Palestine candidate won, and a concurrent referendum to allow hybrid meetings and online voting failed by 15 votes (66.43% vs the 66.6% required). *(Per Wikipedia article on Park Slope Food Coop. The specific June 2025 vote percentages rest on Wikipedia alone in our captured source set; for load-bearing use, the primary sources would be PSFC's own meeting minutes at foodcoop.com or contemporary local press coverage — 2026-04-23 lint flagged this as a claim-attribution gap.)*

*(Editorial observation: the boycott mechanism at PSFC is the direct lineal descendant of the Rochdale tradition of collective consumer action — the same mechanism reappears in the [[regulatory-responses|consumer-backlash episodes documented in the regulatory-responses counter-power landscape]]. PSFC demonstrates that the mechanism works at 17,000-member scale and has been active for five decades.)*

## Counter-power mechanism

PSFC is a **standing consumer cooperative** (first row of the [[consumer-collective-bargaining|consumer-collective-bargaining taxonomy]]). Distinct from:

- **GPOs** — PSFC is member-owned and member-governed, not a professional intermediary funded by supplier admin fees. No funding-mechanism incentive-incompatibility concern.
- **Transactional group-buying (Tuangou/Groupon)** — PSFC relationships are standing and identity-based, not per-deal transactional.
- **[[platform-cooperatives]]** — PSFC is a physical-retail coop, not an online platform. Predates the platform-coop movement by 40 years.

**What it addresses:** retail grocery markup (the core extractive margin between wholesale cost and retail price). Does not address [[rental-housing-algorithmic-pricing|algorithmic-pricing]] or [[surveillance-pricing-retail|surveillance-pricing]] patterns; the store is brick-and-mortar with static prices.

**What it doesn't address:** online retail, cross-product personalisation, the data-broker layer (see [[data-cooperatives]] and [[surveillance-pricing-retail]]).

## Scope and limitations

- **Urban, single-location.** PSFC operates from three connected buildings on Union Street in Park Slope; there is no physical expansion to other neighbourhoods. Distinct from REI's 181-store model (see [[rei]]).
- **Labour-requirement gates membership.** The 2h45m-per-6-weeks requirement is the defining membership barrier; other US food coops have moved away from required labour. This keeps the model high-trust but limits scalability and excludes members whose schedules can't accommodate the commitment.
- **Internal politics visible in the captured article.** The 2023+ BDS boycott debate, 2018 staff unionisation effort, and the Barneys "coop" naming lawsuit question (2010) all show the cooperative form produces ongoing internal conflicts over scope. The [[data-cooperatives|Ada Lovelace 2021 report]] flags "democratic control as a burden at scale" as a generic cooperative challenge; PSFC's record illustrates it.

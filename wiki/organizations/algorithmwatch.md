# AlgorithmWatch

AlgorithmWatch is a **non-governmental non-profit organisation based in Berlin and Zurich**, founded in 2016 with an Automated Decision-Making (ADM) Manifesto. Focus: research, journalism, and policy advocacy on algorithmic accountability — "the black-box nature of ADM systems and the need to crack open those black boxes to ensure greater accountability" (per captured primary source + WebSearch preamble). The closest live template in this wiki for an **algorithmic-pricing watchdog organisation**.

## Structure

- **Organisational form:** non-governmental, non-profit.
- **Locations:** Berlin (primary), Zurich (separate entity: AlgorithmWatch CH).
- **Founding:** 2016, debut at Re:publica conference with the Automated Decision-Making Manifesto.
- **Funding:** partially through foundations — the captured source references Alfred Landecker Foundation as a project partner.

## Stated mission (per captured AlgorithmWatch "What we do" page)

From the captured source:

> AlgorithmWatch fights for a world where algorithms and Artificial Intelligence (AI) do not weaken justice, human rights, democracy, and sustainability but strengthen them.

Focus areas per the captured source and WebSearch preamble: sustainability, the COVID-19 pandemic, human resources, social media platforms, public discourse. The core observation: "When companies use automated systems to process data and make decisions about credit, apartments, or jobs, such systems are not neutral and can reproduce inequalities and assumptions about people that already exist in society."

## Mechanism

Three observed repeat activities from the captured source:

1. **Research projects.** Sector-specific ADM investigation — sustainability, pandemic-era automated systems, HR, platforms.
2. **Fellowship programmes.** Algorithmic-accountability reporting fellowships provide editorial and financial support, mentorship from journalists/researchers in the field, and publication opportunities across European outlets. Recent cohort focus: digital surveillance, AI-driven intimate image abuse.
3. **Policy advocacy.** The 2016 ADM Manifesto is the public framing; ongoing policy work engages with EU AI Act, national ADM regulation, and cross-border coordination via the Algorithm Network (Arena for Journalism in Europe).

## Counter-power mechanism

AlgorithmWatch is a hybrid of two mechanisms that this wiki otherwise documents separately:

- **[[transparency-tools|Crowdsourced audit / observatory]]** — AlgorithmWatch conducts original research and publishes findings, but does not (per captured sources) run a standing Citizen-Browser-style automated data-collection panel. Its research is topic-specific, not continuous observatory work.
- **[[noyb|NOYB-style strategic-advocacy NGO]]** — institutional, grant-funded, publishes and pressures; but AlgorithmWatch is journalism-and-research-forward rather than strategic-litigation-forward. No captured equivalent to Schrems-II-class cases in the AlgorithmWatch portfolio.

The hybrid role is distinct: **research + fellowships + advocacy without direct enforcement authority**. This is the gap that [[possible-strategic-levers|strategic lever #26 (consumer union with war chest)]] would close if it adopted both AlgorithmWatch-style research/fellowships *and* NOYB-style litigation. *(editorial / synthesis.)*

## Relevance for dynamic-pricing strategy

*(editorial / synthesis.)*

AlgorithmWatch's model transfers cleanly to the pricing domain. Specifically:

1. **Algorithmic pricing is an ADM system.** Personalised pricing, tenant-screening scores, dynamic rent recommendation (RealPage-class) all meet the AlgorithmWatch "automated decision-making" definition. No captured AlgorithmWatch project specifically on dynamic pricing — this is an open opportunity for an explicit partnership or replica organisation.
2. **Journalism-fellowship-and-research stack.** [[markup-citizen-browser|The Markup]] is the US equivalent in journalism; AlgorithmWatch is the EU equivalent with more research-forward (vs Markup's more investigation-forward) posture. A US pricing-watchdog org would ideally combine both models.
3. **Gap: no continuous-observatory infrastructure.** AlgorithmWatch publishes research on ADM systems but, from the captured sources, does not run a continuous Citizen-Browser-equivalent monitoring panel. A well-funded pricing-watchdog would need to fund that half too. See [[regulatory-responses|design-input #1 (consumer-side pricing observatory)]].

## DSA Article 40.4 data access mechanism (2026)

AlgorithmWatch filed one of the first Art.40.4 data access requests under the EU DSA, targeting Google AI Overviews and their impact on media pluralism and web traffic.

### What Art.40.4 is

Article 40.4 of the DSA allows non-commercial research organisations to request internal company data from Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs) and Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs) for investigating "systemic risks." It is the DSA equivalent of a DSAR, but for systemic-risk investigation rather than individual data-subject rights. The provision was supposed to take effect 17 February 2024; implementation delays pushed it ~20 months. AlgorithmWatch filed under it in the first week it properly came into force.

### The Google AI Overviews case

AlgorithmWatch, as part of an alliance of NGOs, publisher associations, and media organisations, filed a complaint with the German Digital Services Coordinator (German DSC) about Google's rollout of AI Overviews — summaries that appear above organic search results and suppress click-through to source websites. The complaint preceded the Art.40.4 request and may have prompted Google to reduce AI Overview frequency for political topics; the causal link is unconfirmed.

The Art.40.4 request asks Google for metrics on how many users visit external websites after a search versus remaining inside Google Search to read AI Overviews. The concern: AI Overviews reduce traffic to journalistic outlets and expert organisations, threatening the economic viability of reliable-information production.

Documented factual accuracy failure: in September 2025 tests, Google AI Overviews returned results stating Olaf Scholz was still German Chancellor — a demonstrably false claim from reliance on its own summaries. In more recent tests the error no longer surfaces as frequently for political topics.

### Counter-power relevance *(editorial)*

Art.40.4 is a replicable mechanism. Any qualifying non-commercial research organisation in the EU can file a data access request against a VLOP or VLOSE to audit algorithmic systems for systemic risk. The German DSC assesses procedural compliance; findings can then underpin enforcement. This makes Art.40.4 structurally analogous to the collective DSAR approach documented at [[regulatory-responses]] but at the platform-accountability rather than individual-rights level.

### Limitations AlgorithmWatch notes

- Enforcement has been weak to date: DSA violations by X have been pending sanctions for months.
- Art.40.4 effectiveness depends on whether research findings are actually used to compel enforcement action. Data access without enforcement follow-through is a transparency measure, not a counter-power measure.
- The 20-month implementation delay is symptomatic of a recurring DSA pattern: long, unpredictable waits between rule adoption and operational availability create uncertainty for organisations trying to use the DSA as intended.

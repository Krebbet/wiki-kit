# REI

Recreational Equipment, Inc. (REI) is an American retail and outdoor-recreation services corporation, **brand-governed as a consumers' cooperative**. Founded 1938 in Seattle; **181 retail stores across 41 US states**. The largest US consumer cooperative captured in this wiki. The clearest contrast to [[park-slope-food-coop]]: both are Rochdale-lineage consumer coops, but REI has traded member-labour and one-location depth for member-dividend-and-national-retail scale — and the co-op identity has correspondingly softened over time.

## Structure

- **Headquarters:** Kent, Washington.
- **Legal/brand form:** consumers' cooperative. Per Wikipedia: "It was formerly governed, and continues to brand itself, as a consumers' co-operative." Member-owned; each active member holds a single voting share.
- **Membership:** lifetime fee **currently $30**. Each active member is entitled to vote for members of the board of directors from among candidates **approved by the incumbent board**.
- **Product scope:** camping gear, hiking, climbing, cycling, water, running, fitness, snow, travel equipment, and men's/women's/kids' clothing. Explicitly does not sell hunting or fishing equipment.

## History

- **1938:** Lloyd Anderson (1902–2000) and Mary (Gaiser) Anderson (1909–2017) founded REI in Seattle. Origin story: Andersons imported an Akadem Pickel ice axe from Austria for The Mountaineers' Basic Climbing Course, then decided to set up a cooperative to help other club members acquire good-quality climbing gear. On June 23, 1938, five Mountaineers met at attorney Ed Rombauer's Seattle office; each paid $1 to join the Recreational Equipment Cooperative.
- **1942:** moved to a new space down the hall from The Mountaineers' club rooms on Pike Street.
- **1955:** hired Jim Whittaker as the first full-time employee.
- **1956:** incorporated as Recreational Equipment Cooperative.
- **1963:** Whittaker became the first American to summit Mount Everest, providing REI "so much free advertising" that gross income topped $1M in 1964.
- **1970s:** branded prominently as "REI Co-op"; focused on serious climbers, backpackers, and mountaineering.
- **1980s:** expanded to camping, kayaking, bicycling, and general outdoor recreation. **The "co-op" moniker was dropped from much of the literature and advertising** during this era.
- **1981:** acquired Mountain Safety Research (MSR), a gear manufacturer; sold to Cascade Designs in 2001 when REI exited manufacturing.
- **1996–2005:** Sally Jewell joined the board; became COO in 2000, CEO in 2005. Left in April 2013 when named US Secretary of the Interior.
- **2013:** Jerry Stritzke (former Coach New York president/COO) named CEO; resigned February 2019.
- **2014:** launched the REI Co-Op clothing line, re-emphasising cooperative branding.
- **October 2015:** redesigned logo including the word "co-op" for the first time since 1983. Launched the **#OptOutside** campaign, closing all stores and halting online order processing on Black Friday and giving all employees a paid day off. REI became "the first major US retailer to forgo operations on Black Friday." Continued through the 2022 holiday season.
- **2018:** joined the post-Parkland NRA boycott; suspended orders from Vista Outdoor (a rifle-maker that also makes outdoor products).
- **May 2019:** Eric Artz (former COO) named CEO.
- **August 2020:** cancelled planned new headquarters campus in Bellevue; shifted corporate employees to remote work mid-pandemic.
- **January 31, 2023:** laid off 8% of corporate staff (167 positions), attributed to return-to-profitability pressure after a pandemic sales boom.
- **January 2025:** Artz announced retirement; Mary Beth Laughton (former board director) succeeded him as CEO in March 2025. REI also announced 400+ layoffs and the shutdown of its "REI Experiences" classes/tours/adventure-vacation business, which had served only 40,000 customers in 2024 (<0.4% of co-op customers) and was unprofitable.
- **October 2025:** announced closure of three stores in Boston, New York City, and Paramus, NJ.

## Member dividend / reward model

- Historically REI issued an **annual patronage dividend** to active members, calculated as a percentage of qualifying full-price purchases.
- **March 2022:** REI announced the dividend would be **permanently rebranded as "Co-op Member Reward"**, ending the cash option. Members receive the reward as **store credit, not redeemable in currency**.
- Per the captured Wikipedia article: "REI's Co‑op Member Reward (rebranded from dividend) typically credits ~10% on eligible full‑price purchases annually."

*(Editorial: the 2022 shift from cash dividend to store-credit-only is a softening of the cooperative economic-participation principle. Per the [[data-cooperatives|ICA seven cooperative principles]] referenced on this wiki's data-coop page, one of the defining tests is members' economic participation — including distribution of surplus. Store credit is still participation in a loose sense but is closer to a loyalty-rewards scheme than to a cooperative dividend. This is a live instance of the "cooperative drift" pattern the Ada Lovelace report warned about on [[data-cooperatives]].)*

## Scale

- **181 retail stores across 41 US states** (per the captured article).
- Mail-order catalogue + e-commerce channels.
- **40,000 customers in 2024** on the Experiences business (shut January 2025).
- Non-retail diversification: REI Adventures (global vacations, 1987); Outdoor School (one-day classes in ~15 metros); acquired Adventure Projects Inc. (Mountain Project climbing-route database, 2015; covers 128,000+ routes) and related Project-family sites (MTBProject, HikingProject, PowderProject, TrailRunProject).

## Counter-power mechanism

REI is a **large-scale, nationally distributed consumer cooperative**. Structurally the same lineage as [[park-slope-food-coop]] but with opposite tradeoffs:

| Axis | REI | PSFC |
|---|---|---|
| Membership fee | $30 lifetime | $25 join + $100 refundable equity |
| Member labour | None required | 2h45m / 6 weeks |
| Scale | 181 stores, 41 states | 1 location, Brooklyn |
| Margin mechanism | Dividend-as-store-credit | Wholesale+25% markup |
| Board selection | Vote from incumbent-approved slate | Vote from open-member candidates |
| Boycott-policy action | 2018 NRA / Vista Outdoor | Multi-decade (anti-apartheid, Nestlé, Coca-Cola, Flaum, etc.) |

**What REI addresses (on its own framing):** consumer access to specialty outdoor gear at member-governed pricing; aligns outdoor-retail revenue with outdoor-advocacy mission (#OptOutside; NRA-boycott participation).

**What REI's trajectory illustrates (editorial):** a consumer cooperative can scale to national retail chains, but doing so comes with governance softening — board-approved candidate slates, dividend-to-store-credit conversion, co-op branding oscillating in and out of public materials. The structural drift is the generic "cooperative at scale" challenge flagged in the [[data-cooperatives|Ada Lovelace 2021 report]] and recurs in the [[platform-cooperatives|Srnicek / Morozov critique]] — though REI is a positive-existence proof that meaningful consumer-cooperative scale is possible. *(editorial / synthesis)*

## Scope and limitations

- **Narrow product category.** REI's counter-power mechanism applies only to outdoor-recreation retail; does not address grocery, housing, healthcare, energy, or data domains covered elsewhere in this wiki.
- **Board governance drift.** Candidates for board election are approved by the incumbent board — a standard corporate-governance pattern but a departure from the more open-slate model at [[park-slope-food-coop]]. Per Rochdale Principles, "democratic member control" is a defining cooperative criterion, and what counts as genuinely democratic in the approved-slate case is contested.
- **No direct pricing counter-power.** REI does not negotiate against a monopolistic supplier in the way a [[consumer-collective-bargaining|GPO]] does. Its "counter-power" claim is that it redirects retail margin back to members (via dividend/reward) and aligns corporate advocacy with members' stated values.

# CoopCycle

CoopCycle is a federation of worker cooperatives in the bike-delivery sector, profiled by the OECD (2023) as its featured case study for how **second-level (federation) cooperatives** can address the capital-conundrum scaling problem endemic to [[platform-cooperatives]]. It is the clearest concrete answer in the captured sources to the Srnicek / Morozov critique that platform coops cannot compete with network-effect-driven VC-funded platforms.

## What it is

Per the OECD 2023 report:
- **72 local worker cooperatives** across **12 countries** (as of the 2023 paper's capture window).
- Sector: bike-delivery (competing with Deliveroo, Uber Eats, DoorDash, Just Eat, and similar).
- Structure: a **second-level cooperative** — a federation whose members are themselves local first-level cooperatives, not individuals.
- Role of the federation: pools resources for **technology development, marketing, and sales**. Allows local cooperatives to collectively reduce costs, exchange knowledge, and leverage network effects *without* requiring centralised ownership.

## The second-level federation model

OECD 2023 treats CoopCycle as the proof-of-concept for a general pattern:

> Local platform cooperatives can form second-level cooperatives to help collaboration, pool resources and facilitate scaling through replication in different geographical locations.

The structural argument:
- **First-level cooperatives** (individual member-owned platform coops) face the capital conundrum — they can't raise VC, can't match Big Tech's scale, can't unilaterally fund the technology commons they depend on.
- **Second-level cooperatives** let them share the technology and brand layer while keeping local governance and operations independent. The federation itself is democratically governed by its member coops.
- This is the same pattern that [[mondragon]] uses for its Cooperative Congress (650 members appointed by constituent cooperatives) — CoopCycle is the digital-platform variant.

## Why this matters for the wiki's theme

CoopCycle is a concrete instance of the **exit-pathway counter-power** mechanism against algorithmic-gig-economy platforms. It is the operational answer to the question "can cooperatives scale enough to be a real alternative?" — at least in sectors (like bike delivery) where Sundararajan's conditions for cooperative viability hold (see [[platform-cooperatives]]: low worker-diversity, lower rate of technology change, locally bounded markets).

For the wiki's **design-input agenda**: CoopCycle's federation architecture is the single most transferable pattern surfaced in this research run. Any consumer-side counter-power tool that wants to scale beyond a single city or sector — a buyer-side data cooperative, a tenant-data-pooling network, a cross-metro class-action signal aggregator — probably needs to be built federation-first, not as a single centralised platform.

## What the captured sources do not cover

- The OECD paper presents CoopCycle as a case study but the capture includes only the executive-summary and Chapter 2 depth. Detailed financial or governance primary sources for CoopCycle itself were not captured; a dedicated `/research coopcycle` run would be needed to build this page out beyond what the OECD summary supports.
- No independent critical assessment of CoopCycle's actual performance against investor-owned competitors (Deliveroo, Uber Eats) was captured. All claims about its effectiveness here are from the OECD report, which frames it favourably.
- Labour-disputes literature on bike-delivery workers (rider injuries, algorithmic wage-setting) appears adjacent to this topic but was not captured in this run.

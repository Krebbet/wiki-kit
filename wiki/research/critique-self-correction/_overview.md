# Critique, Self-Correction, Self-Refine, Debate

Methods that improve LLM outputs (or train LLMs) by using a critique signal — generated by the same model, a sibling model, or guided by a small set of natural-language principles — in place of dense human labels. The unifying primitive: **a textual critique substitutes for additional supervised data**.

## Papers in this theme
- [[self-refine]] — SELF-REFINE (Madaan et al., 2023). Inference-time loop: same LLM generates, self-critiques, and revises; ~20% avg gain across 7 tasks; needs an instruction-tuned base.
- [[constitutional-ai]] — Constitutional AI (Bai et al. / Anthropic, 2022). Replaces harm-related human labels with ~16 natural-language principles + AI-generated critiques; trains both via SFT (revisions) and RLAIF (AI-labelled preferences).
- [[reflexion]] — Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023). "Verbal RL": episodic memory of self-reflective text replaces gradient updates; sparse binary reward → actionable textual lesson; 91% pass@1 on HumanEval.
- [[prometheus-2]] — Prometheus 2 (2024). Open 7B/8x7B evaluator LM unifying direct + pairwise assessment via weight merging; r=0.685 on MT Bench. Gives the critique signal a *trained* judge instead of a prompted one.
- [[critic-tool-interactive]] — CRITIC (Gao et al., 2023). Frozen LLM + external tool feedback for self-correction; +10pp over ReAct on AmbigNQ. Empirically: tools are necessary, self-only critique degrades performance.
- [[critic-cot]] — Critic-CoT (Zheng et al., 2024). SFT on weak-supervision critique-refine pairs trains explicit System-2 step-wise critique; 93.3% GSM8K, 57.8% MATH500. Distils the inference-time critique loop into weights.

## Cross-cutting synthesis

**Three flavours of critique source.** The critique signal can be grounded in three different ways:

| Method | Critique source | Verifier ground truth | Scale of human input |
|---|---|---|---|
| SELF-REFINE | Same LLM, few-shot prompts | None — pure self-judgement | ~6–8 demo triples per task |
| Constitutional AI | Same LLM, principle-guided | Constitution as proxy ground truth | ~16 principles + few-shot |
| Reflexion | Sparse environment signal + self-reflection LLM | External (unit tests, EM, env reward) | ~2–6 few-shot trajectories |

The progression is from *purely self-grounded* (SELF-REFINE) → *language-grounded* (CAI principles) → *externally grounded* (Reflexion's environment signal that the LLM then verbalises). The amount of usable signal scales with how anchored the critique is.

**Judge model vs verifier vs constitutional principle.** All three ultimately produce a scalar or textual "this is/isn't good" signal, but the *source of authority* differs.
- **Self-judge** (SELF-REFINE): authority comes from the base model's internal estimate. Fails on math (94% "looks good" rate) because errors are invisible from inside the policy that made them.
- **Constitutional principle** (CAI): authority comes from a human-authored short list of natural-language rules. Works because principles are *external to the policy* but *interpretable by the policy*. CoT extraction further amplifies (Fig. 4).
- **External verifier amplified by self-reflection** (Reflexion): authority comes from outside the LM (unit test, EM, env), but the LM's job is to *translate* the binary signal into specific textual guidance. False-positive verifiers (Reflexion's MBPP self-tests) catastrophically poison the loop.

The pattern: **self-judgement is brittle; external grounding is stable but coarse; the critique-LLM's job is to bridge them by amplifying coarse external signal into specific actionable text**.

**When self-critique works vs fails.**
- *Works*: outputs with multiple violable constraints (CAI's principles, SELF-REFINE's Constrained Generation +30, Acronym Generation), tasks where errors are syntactically detectable (code via execution), tasks where preference is over surface features (sentiment, tone, dialogue engagement).
- *Fails*: math reasoning at the same scale (errors are subtle, locally-coherent traces deceive the judge), Vicuna-13B (sub-instruction-following base can't follow the critique format at all), MBPP Python with self-generated unit tests (16% false-positive rate > 1.4% on HumanEval, undermining the loop).
- *Caveat*: every published self-critique result uses a strong base (GPT-3.5+ or 52B Anthropic). The 1–40B regime David targets is closer to the Vicuna-13B failure mode than to the GPT-4 success regime.

**Relation to "auxiliary information for variability".** David's design needs to amplify a single concept-bearing example into many training signals. These three papers offer three mechanisms:
- **CAI's principle decomposition**: write the concept as a list of natural-language sub-criteria, then critique variants along each axis. The example becomes a battery of (variant × principle) sub-checks.
- **SELF-REFINE's iterative critique**: one example produces (initial, fb_0, y_1, fb_1, y_2, …) — the trajectory itself is the auxiliary information, with each step adding error signal in token space.
- **Reflexion's verbal lesson**: one trial → one lesson stored as text → conditions all future attempts. The lesson is sample-efficient supervision distilled from a sparse signal.

All three substitute *language structure* (principles, critiques, lessons) for *data quantity*. This is precisely the tradeoff a single-sample concept method needs.

## Open questions
- **Concept-level critiques in small models.** All three papers' positive results require strong bases (GPT-3.5+/52B). Can a 7B model self-critique a *concept* — a higher-level abstraction than the task surface — when it cannot reliably self-critique math? Likely needs a stronger sibling judge or external grounding.
- **Critique decomposition vs sub-rewards.** CAI proves a list of principles can replace tens of thousands of preference labels. Open: is principle-decomposition equivalent to defining a multi-dimensional reward signal? If yes, single-sample concept learning could specify a concept as ~5–10 principles and use principle-by-principle preference scores as the per-component reward.
- **When to trust the self-judge.** Reflexion's MBPP failure (false-positive self-tests) and SELF-REFINE's math failure both suggest self-critique is dangerous when the policy and judge share blind spots. Practical question: how to *detect* this situation cheaply without an external verifier? Disagreement between multiple critique samples? CoT-self-consistency on the critique itself?
- **Distilling critique into weights.** SELF-REFINE/Reflexion stay at inference; CAI distils. Is the post-distillation policy *as good* as the iterative inference loop, or is the critique structure itself necessary at inference? Implication for fine-tuning: maybe SFT on `(x, fb, y_refined)` triples encodes the critique behaviour into weights.
- **Single-shot extreme.** No paper tests `|D| = 1` strictly. SELF-REFINE comes closest (one input + few-shot prompts) but assumes a question distribution at evaluation. Can the loop close on a single concept with no further inputs?

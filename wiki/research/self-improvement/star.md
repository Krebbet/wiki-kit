# STaR: Self-Taught Reasoner — Bootstrapping Reasoning With Reasoning

Zelikman et al. (Stanford / Google, 2022). Bootstraps a base LLM's chain-of-thought reasoning by iteratively (i) sampling rationales for a question dataset, (ii) keeping only rationales whose final answer matches ground truth, and (iii) fine-tuning on the filtered set. A complementary "rationalization" step provides the correct answer as a hint so the model can backward-justify problems it could not solve, recovering training signal from initial failures.

## Method
- Inputs: pretrained LLM `M`, dataset `D = {(x, y)}`, and a tiny prompt set `P` (≈10 worked rationale examples).
- Outer loop, restart from `M` each iteration to avoid overfitting:
  1. Few-shot prompt the current model to generate `(r̂, ŷ)` for every `x` in `D`.
  2. **Rationalization** branch: for failures, prepend the gold answer `y` as a hint and resample a rationale; keep ones that now produce `y`.
  3. Filter to rationales where `ŷ = y` (and rationalized rationales where the hinted run yielded `y`).
  4. Fine-tune the original `M` on the union of filtered rationales (hint stripped) and repeat.
- STaR is framed as an approximation to a policy-gradient on `J = Σ E_{r̂,ŷ ~ p_M}[1(ŷ = y)]`; greedy decoding plus multi-step SGD on the same batch makes it a simple expert-iteration variant (Section 3.1).

## Claims
- CommonsenseQA: GPT-J 6B with STaR + rationalization reaches **72.5%** dev accuracy vs 60.0% for direct fine-tuning, 36.6% few-shot CoT, 20.9% direct few-shot; comparable to a 30× larger fine-tuned GPT-3 at 73.0% (Table 1). Uses 86.7% of training data (78.2% generated + 8.5% rationalized).
- GSM8K: 10.7% test accuracy vs 5.8% for direct fine-tuning and 3.1% for few-shot CoT, while training on only 28.7% of the data (Table 2).
- Arithmetic (n-digit addition): 89.5% accuracy after 16 iterations starting from near-zero few-shot performance; rationalization unlocks 2-digit addition from <1% to 32% in one iteration and accelerates curriculum traversal across digit lengths (Figs 4-5).
- Human raters preferred STaR rationales over few-shot ones (+30%, p=0.039) and over crowd-sourced human rationales (+74%, p<0.001).

## Sample efficiency
STaR is the canonical "amplify a tiny seed" recipe. The seed is **10 worked-rationale examples**; everything else is generated by the model itself and filtered by ground-truth answers (which are cheap labels, not rationales). For each `(x, y)` STaR can produce *multiple* rationale variants — the auxiliary variability is structural diversity over latent reasoning paths `r ~ p_M(r | x)` (or `p_M(r | x, y)` under rationalization). Rationalization is the explicit mechanism for keeping a stubborn seed problem in the training set after the on-policy sampler has stopped solving it. Limitation: the first iteration needs above-chance few-shot reasoning, and high base-rate tasks (binary decisions) confound filtering.

## Relevance to the project
STaR is the cleanest precedent for single-sample reuse with auxiliary variability: each `(x, y)` is rewritten into many model-authored rationales, and the set is *expanded* under failure via answer-conditioned generation. For David's concept-based learning, the rationale-generation / rationalization split maps onto "forward concept use" and "backward concept reconstruction" from a single seed. The temperature ablation (Section 5) is a direct warning: just sampling more at high T degrades training because correct answers via incorrect reasoning poison the signal — variability has to be filtered at the *trace* level, not just the answer.

# An Explanation of In-context Learning as Implicit Bayesian Inference

Xie, Raghunathan, Liang, Ma (Stanford, ICLR 2022) explain ICL as implicit Bayesian inference over latent *concepts* in the pretraining distribution. If pretraining documents are generated by sampling a latent concept $\theta$ then emitting tokens from an HMM conditioned on $\theta$, then the LM that fits this distribution must do posterior inference over $\theta$ to predict next tokens. At test time, an ICL prompt — concatenated independent $(x,y)$ examples — is a low-probability sequence under pretraining, but the posterior $p(\theta \mid \text{prompt})$ still concentrates on the prompt's latent concept $\theta^*$, so the predictive distribution converges to $p_{\text{prompt}}(y \mid x)$. The paper proves convergence under a "distinguishability" condition and validates on a synthetic GINC dataset where both transformers and LSTMs exhibit ICL.

## Method

**Generative model.** Pretraining doc: $p(o_{1:T}) = \int p(o_{1:T} \mid \theta) p(\theta) d\theta$, where $p(\cdot \mid \theta)$ is an HMM whose transitions are parameterised by $\theta$. Prompt: $n$ i.i.d. examples $O_i = [x_i, y_i]$ sampled from $p(\cdot \mid h_i^{\text{start}}, \theta^*)$ with start states drawn from a *prompt start distribution* $p_{\text{prompt}}$, separated by a delimiter token whose hidden state is restricted to a delimiter set $D$.

**Predictor.** $f_n(x_{\text{test}}) = \arg\max_y p(y \mid S_n, x_{\text{test}})$, expanded via Bayes:
```
p(y | S_n, x_test) ∝ ∫ p(y | x_test, θ) · exp(n · r_n(θ)) · p(θ) dθ
r_n(θ) = (1/n) log [ p(S_n, x_test | θ) / p(S_n, x_test | θ*) ]
```
The proof shows $r_n(\theta) \rightarrow -\text{const} < 0$ for all $\theta \neq \theta^*$, so $\exp(n \cdot r_n(\theta)) \rightarrow 0$ everywhere except at $\theta^*$, "selecting" the prompt concept by marginalisation.

**Key technical move.** Use the delimiter token's restricted hidden-state set $D$ to factorise the joint over examples up to per-example $O(1)$ error, then bound $r_n(\theta)$ by a difference of KL divergences $\text{KL}_j(\theta^* \| \theta)$ minus per-example mismatch error terms $\varepsilon_{\text{start}}$, $\varepsilon_{\text{delim}}$. The *distinguishability* condition is: per-token KL signal exceeds the start/delimiter mismatch cost.

**Continuous case.** When $\theta \in$ continuous $\Theta$ (no isolated $\theta^*$), distinguishability fails — but the in-context error still decreases with **example length $k$** because each extra token within an example contributes another KL term; thus information in inputs (not just labels) drives ICL.

**GINC dataset.** Mixture of HMMs (5 concepts, vocab 50, hidden states 10). Transformer and LSTM both exhibit ICL.

## Claims

- **Theorem 1 (distinguishability).** Under Assumptions 1–5 + distinguishability, $\lim_n L_{0-1}(f_n) = \inf_f L_{0-1}(f)$ — ICL achieves Bayes error.
- **Theorem 2 (continuous $\Theta$).** Even without distinguishability, ICL error $\rightarrow 0$ as example length $k \rightarrow \infty$.
- **GINC empirics.** Accuracy rises with both number of examples $n$ and per-example length $k$ (as predicted). Removing latent-concept structure from pretraining destroys ICL.
- **Scaling beyond loss.** On GINC, larger transformers achieve identical pretraining loss yet higher ICL accuracy — model size matters for ICL beyond what likelihood captures.
- **Reproduces real-world phenomena.** Sensitivity to example ordering (Zhao et al. 2021) and zero-shot > few-shot regimes (Brown et al.) both appear in GINC.

## Sample efficiency

The Bayesian view makes sample efficiency explicit: posterior concentration is exponential in $n$ once distinguishability holds, so a *handful* of examples often suffices. Critically, even **a single example** carries ICL signal because per-example KL is summed not just over labels but over all $k$ tokens in the example — the first example shifts the posterior by $k \cdot \text{KL}$ worth of evidence. This explains why one-shot prompts often work and why long-form examples can outperform many short ones. The catch: the model must have already learned a posterior over a sufficiently rich concept family during pretraining; ICL is sample-efficient at test time precisely because it is sample-*expensive* at pretrain time.

## Relevance to the project

If a single ICL example acts as evidence for a posterior selection over latent concepts, then a single fine-tuning example should *hard-write* the concept selection that prompting only soft-conditions. Two implications: (1) David's "single-sample concept imprinting" is mechanistically the explicit-update twin of Bayesian conditioning — the right fine-tuning loss should *peak* the posterior on $\theta^*$, not shift weights uniformly. (2) Within-example token information matters as much as the input/output mapping; single-sample fine-tuning that uses *long, structured* examples (showing the concept in multiple tokens) should be more sample-efficient than terse examples — directly mirroring Theorem 2.

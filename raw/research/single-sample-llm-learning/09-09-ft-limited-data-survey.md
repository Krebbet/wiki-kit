## Abstract

Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) with limited data poses a practical challenge in low-resource languages, specialized domains, and constrained deployment settings. While pre-trained LLMs provide strong foundations, effective adaptation under data scarcity requires focused and efficient fine-tuning techniques. This paper presents a structured and practical survey of recent methods for fine-tuning LLMs in data-scarce scenarios. We systematically review parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques that lower training and deployment costs, domain and cross-lingual adaptation methods for both encoder and decoder models, and model specialization strategies. We further examine preference alignment approaches that guide model behavior using limited human or synthetic feedback, emphasizing sample and compute efficiency. Throughout, we highlight empirical tradeoffs, selection criteria, and best practices for choosing suitable techniques based on task constraints, including model scaling, data scaling, and the mitigation of catastrophic forgetting. The aim is to equip researchers and practitioners with actionable insights for effectively fine-tuning LLMs when data and resources are limited.

## 1 Introduction

Pre-trained language models (PLMs) have driven unprecedented advances in NLP, showing strong capabilities across a wide range of tasks [\(Han](#page-20-0) [et al.,](#page-20-0) [2021\)](#page-20-0). However, their development and adaptation typically require large-scale data and computational resources, often unavailable in realworld scenarios, particularly in low-resource languages or specialized domains such as medicine [\(Crema et al.,](#page-16-0) [2023\)](#page-16-0), law [\(Noguti et al.,](#page-25-0) [2023\)](#page-25-0), chemistry [\(Jablonka et al.,](#page-22-0) [2024\)](#page-22-0), or finance [\(Zhao](#page-32-0) [et al.,](#page-32-0) [2021\)](#page-32-0). To address these constraints, transfer learning has become the standard paradigm: models are first pre-trained on general corpora and then adapted through fine-tuning (FT) and preference alignment (PA). Yet, these adaptation stages can still demand significant annotated data, and naive FT under low-data conditions risks overfitting, catastrophic forgetting [\(Ramasesh et al.,](#page-27-0) [2021\)](#page-27-0), and poor generalization.

Given the rapidly evolving field and broad array of methods, a structured and pragmatic review is necessary to navigate current strategies and make informed choices under real-world constraints. This paper offers a practical guide to finetuning large language models under data scarcity. We examine recent developments across four key areas. First, we review (1) Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning (PEFT, [§ 3\)](#page-1-0), which enables modular and cost-effective adaptation. Second, we explore (2) Domain and Cross-lingual FT [\(§ 4\)](#page-2-0), targeting robustness to distributional shifts. Third, (3) FT for Specialization [\(§ 5\)](#page-5-0) covers techniques for specialized tasks, domains, and lowresource languages, including scaling, data efficiency, and design recommendations. Fourth, we analyze (4) Preference Alignment (PA [§ 6\)](#page-7-0), including data requirements, scaling behavior, and downstream effects. We conclude with a focused discussion on practical challenges and emerging strategies, such as the trade-offs between encoder and decoder architectures, cross-lingual transfer in low-resource contexts, and the potential of model merging. Our emphasis throughout is on actionable insights and contextualized, concrete guidance to support effective LLM fine-tuning when data is limited.

## 2 Related Surveys

Several prior works address related aspects or have partial overlap with our work. Some surveys target data augmentation as a solution to data scarcity [\(Feng et al.,](#page-17-0) [2021;](#page-17-0) [Chen et al.,](#page-15-0) [2023b\)](#page-15-0), including domain-specific generation techniques for lowresource settings [\(Stylianou et al.,](#page-29-0) [2023\)](#page-29-0). Modelcentric strategies are covered in surveys on PEFT methods [\(Han et al.,](#page-20-1) [2024;](#page-20-1) [Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024\)](#page-23-0), while [Treviso et al.](#page-29-1) [\(2023\)](#page-29-1); [Wan et al.](#page-30-0) [\(2024\)](#page-30-0); [Xu et al.](#page-31-0) [\(2025\)](#page-31-0) offer broader coverage of efficient training techniques across different stages of model development. Other works focus on lowresource supervised NLP [\(Hedderich et al.,](#page-20-2) [2021\)](#page-20-2), instruction tuning [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-1) [2024\)](#page-32-1), and PA [\(Gao et al.,](#page-17-1) [2024;](#page-17-1) [Jiang et al.,](#page-22-1) [2024\)](#page-22-1). In contrast, our work offers a pragmatic overview of the full post-pretraining pipeline for adapting PLMs with limited data. We cover PEFT, domain and cross-lingual generalization, specialization strategies, and PA in an integrated survey, emphasizing practical insights, comparative trade-offs, and methodological guidance to support informed decisions in data-scarce scenarios.

## <span id="page-1-0"></span>3 Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning

Fine-tuning (FT) all parameters of large PLMs is computationally expensive, sample-inefficient, and often unstable in low-resource regimes [\(Dodge et al.,](#page-16-1) [2020\)](#page-16-1). Parameter-efficient finetuning (PEFT) mitigates these issues by updating only a small fraction of weights, enabling strong adaptation with lower cost and reduced risk of catastrophic forgetting in data-scarce scenarios. We organize PEFT methods by how they *compose* with the base model parameters (following [Pfeif](#page-27-1)[fer et al.,](#page-27-1) [2023\)](#page-27-1), and highlight key performance and efficiency considerations for selecting the appropriate method for a given task and data setup. For comprehensive surveys, see [Han et al.](#page-20-1) [\(2024\)](#page-20-1); [Pfeiffer et al.](#page-27-1) [\(2023\)](#page-27-1).

#### <span id="page-1-1"></span>3.1 Parameter composition

These methods modify existing weights through sparse or low-rank updates.

Selective methods train only a subset of the model weights (specific layers, parameter types, etc.) while keeping the rest fixed. They build on the inductive bias of sparsity: in an over-parameterized model, a small fraction of parameters plays a disproportionately important role for a particular task. Variants include tuning specific (usually last) layers or model components like the bias, or LayerNorm parameters [\(Devlin et al.,](#page-16-2) [2019;](#page-16-2) [Ben Za](#page-14-0)[ken et al.,](#page-14-0) [2022;](#page-14-0) [Liu et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022\)](#page-24-0). Another line of work optimizes subnetworks based on gradient information either by selecting a specific subnetwork [\(Xu et al.,](#page-32-2) [2021b;](#page-32-2) [Ansell et al.,](#page-13-0) [2022;](#page-13-0) [Sung](#page-29-2) [et al.,](#page-29-2) [2021\)](#page-29-2), or adaptively choosing it through a bi-level optimization strategy [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-3) [2022a;](#page-32-3) [Yu et al.,](#page-32-4) [2023\)](#page-32-4). These methods are extremely lightweight and overhead-free at inference time, but usually underperform compared to approaches that introduce new trainable parameters.

Reparametrization methods replace full weight updates with low-dimensional structures, motivated by the low-rank nature of PLM weights [\(Aghajanyan et al.,](#page-13-1) [2021\)](#page-13-1). The most widely used approach, LoRA [\(Hu et al.,](#page-21-0) [2021\)](#page-21-0), decomposes weight updates into the product of two low-rank matrices and can be merged into the backbone at inference, avoiding extra latency. Its effectiveness led to numerous extensions improving efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and calibration [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-1) [2024b;](#page-24-1) [Dettmers et al.,](#page-16-3) [2023;](#page-16-3) [Hayou et al.,](#page-20-3) [2024;](#page-20-3) [Ding et al.,](#page-16-4) [2023a;](#page-16-4) [Zhang](#page-32-5) [et al.,](#page-32-5) [2022b;](#page-32-5) [Edalati et al.,](#page-16-5) [2022\)](#page-16-5). Reparametrization methods work best on larger models and with moderate to high data sizes, and often match full FT performance with careful tuning [\(Van Veen](#page-30-1) [et al.,](#page-30-1) [2023\)](#page-30-1).

#### 3.2 Input composition

These methods extend the input with learned embeddings that act as task-specific context.

Soft prompt prepend learned embedding vectors to the input or key-value pairs in attention layers [\(Li and Liang,](#page-23-1) [2021;](#page-23-1) [Lester et al.,](#page-23-2) [2021\)](#page-23-2). They are a popular choice to adapt models across domains and languages due to their flexibility and efficiency [\(Tu et al.,](#page-29-3) [2024;](#page-29-3) [Goswami et al.,](#page-18-0) [2023;](#page-18-0) [Zhao et al.,](#page-33-0) [2022\)](#page-33-0), but often converge slowly and perform reliably only at very large model scales [\(Mao et al.,](#page-25-1) [2022\)](#page-25-1). Variants such as IA<sup>3</sup> [\(Liu](#page-24-0) [et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022\)](#page-24-0) improve efficiency by learning vectors to scale hidden activations, achieving adapterlevel performance with fewer parameters. Soft prompts typically incur additional inference overhead, making them more suitable for scenarios prioritizing training efficiency over runtime cost.

#### 3.3 Function composition

These methods augment the frozen backbone with lightweight trainable modules.

Adapters are small feedforward modules inserted between transformer layers [\(Houlsby et al.,](#page-21-1) [2019;](#page-21-1) [Pfeiffer et al.,](#page-27-2) [2020\)](#page-27-2). They reduce training memory and enable flexible modular composition across tasks [\(Pfeiffer et al.,](#page-26-0) [2021;](#page-26-0) [Wang et al.,](#page-30-2) [2022;](#page-30-2) [Chronopoulou et al.,](#page-15-1) [2023\)](#page-15-1), but sequential insertion slows down inference by up to 40% [\(Rücklé et al.,](#page-28-0) [2021\)](#page-28-0). Parallel and Ladder Sidetuning variants mitigate this issue [\(He et al.,](#page-20-4) [2021;](#page-20-4) [Sung et al.,](#page-29-4) [2022\)](#page-29-4). Adapters can act as bottlenecks, requiring wider layers and more paramaters than other PEFT methods to maintain performance [\(Hu](#page-21-2) [et al.,](#page-21-2) [2023b\)](#page-21-2). Their efficiency can be improved through low-rank reparametrization, linking them conceptually with methods in [§ 3.1.](#page-1-1)

## 3.4 Hybrid methods

Hybrid methods combine different forms of composition to leverage complementary strengths. Examples include Compacter [\(Karimi Mahabadi](#page-22-2) [et al.,](#page-22-2) [2021\)](#page-22-2), which integrates adapters with lowrank decomposition, and UniPELT, which dynamically activates adapters, prefixes, and LoRA [\(Mao](#page-25-1) [et al.,](#page-25-1) [2022\)](#page-25-1). Such methods can be powerful in very low-resource settings, but are generally more sensitive to hyperparameter tuning [\(Chen et al.,](#page-15-2) [2022a\)](#page-15-2).

#### 3.5 Selection criteria

Choosing the right PEFT method depends on task type, model size, and data scale. Full FT becomes favorable only with million-scale datasets [\(Zhang](#page-32-6) [et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a\)](#page-32-6), whereas PEFT often matches or outperforms it under 100k samples [\(Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024;](#page-23-0) [Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b;](#page-16-6) [Jukic and Snajder](#page-22-3) ´ , [2023\)](#page-22-3). In practice, selective and reparametrization methods update less than 1% of parameters, while adapters and soft prompts can require up to several percent [\(Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024\)](#page-23-0). Selective methods are simple, efficient, but they usually underperform [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022\)](#page-24-0), with LayerNorm tuning sometimes found effective [\(Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024\)](#page-23-0). Soft prompts can outperform LoRA on the scale of a few thousand samples [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022;](#page-24-0) [Zhang](#page-32-6) [et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a;](#page-32-6) [Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b\)](#page-16-6), but converge slowly and are fragile at smaller model scales. Adapters and LoRA stand out as the most robust choices, reliably matching full FT with minimal hyperparameter tuning in moderate- to largescale data settings (over 10k samples; [Zhang et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a\)](#page-32-6). Hybrid methods can yield gains in extremely low-resource scenarios but at the cost of added complexity [\(Chen et al.,](#page-15-2) [2022a;](#page-15-2) [Mao et al.,](#page-25-1) [2022;](#page-25-1) [He et al.,](#page-20-4) [2021\)](#page-20-4).

Inference overhead varies across methods, with adapters imposing the largest slowdown due to added depth, followed by soft prompts (30–40%), reparametrization methods (although they can usually be merged into the model), and selective methods, which incur minimal to no runtime cost [\(Hu et al.,](#page-21-0) [2021;](#page-21-0) [Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024\)](#page-23-0). [Table 4](#page-34-0) and [Appendix A](#page-33-1) offer more practical guidance on selecting PEFT methods across discriminative tasks for encoder models.

#### 3.6 Efficient design choices

Efficient design choices in PEFT methods are vital for maximizing performance gains while minimizing resource requirements. Studies show that PEFT methods exhibit better performance and convergence rate as the model size increases, also progressively narrowing the gap between full FT and PEFT [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a;](#page-32-6) [Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b;](#page-16-6) [Lialin et al.,](#page-23-0) [2024\)](#page-23-0). Intuitively, with increasing data size expressivity should be expanded through more PEFT parameters. However, studies [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a;](#page-32-6) [Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b\)](#page-16-6) show that the gains of scaling the tunable parameters (e.g., LoRA rank 4 → 128 or prompt-tuning prompt length 50 → 600) are marginal when scaling the data size up to 100k samples, especially compared to scaling the model. With a fixed compute budget, it is preferable to opt for larger models with reduced precision, e.g. 8 or 4-bit quantization [\(Dettmers et al.,](#page-16-3) [2023\)](#page-16-3).

#### <span id="page-3-0"></span>4.1 Continued pre-training

Continued pre-training (CPT) bridges the gap between general PT and specialized domains or languages using unlabeled data. It reuses the original self-supervised PT objective (or combines different ones for added capabilities like fill-in-the-middle; [Bavarian et al.,](#page-14-2) [2022\)](#page-14-2) allowing adaptation without supervised labels [\(Gururangan](#page-20-5) [et al.,](#page-20-5) [2020\)](#page-20-5), facilitating vocabulary extension and sample-efficient reuse of labeled FT data [\(Gnehm](#page-18-1) [et al.,](#page-18-1) [2022\)](#page-18-1).

For cross-lingual settings, aligning over diverse language data helps improve low-resource performance without considerably degrading highresource capabilities [\(Imani et al.,](#page-21-3) [2023;](#page-21-3) [Blevins](#page-14-3) [and Zettlemoyer,](#page-14-3) [2022\)](#page-14-3). Parallel corpora allow further gains via translation-based or other crosslingual masking strategies [\(Wang et al.,](#page-30-4) [2023a;](#page-30-4) [Conneau and Lample,](#page-16-7) [2019\)](#page-16-7). However, as the number of languages increases, the curse of dimensionality can dilute representational capacity, making language-specific adaptation or modular training strategies increasingly important [\(Alabi](#page-13-2) [et al.,](#page-13-2) [2022;](#page-13-2) [Conneau et al.,](#page-16-8) [2020\)](#page-16-8). For domain adaptation, CPT on modest amounts of high-quality in-domain text enables models to better capture domain-specific terminology and discourse patterns [\(Gururangan et al.,](#page-20-5) [2020;](#page-20-5) [Bai et al.,](#page-13-3) [2021\)](#page-13-3). This approach typically outperforms supervised FT alone and is more effective than mixing domain-specific with general data and training from scratch [\(Türkmen et al.,](#page-30-5) [2023\)](#page-30-5). Targeting specific vocabulary in the replaced or next token prediction objectives can further enhance performance [\(Lu et al.,](#page-24-2) [2023\)](#page-24-2). In low-resource scenarios, proxy corpora or mixed-domain CPT can help, but the relevance of the data often outweighs its quantity [\(Mahapatra et al.,](#page-24-3) [2022;](#page-24-3) [Jantscher et al.,](#page-22-4) [2023\)](#page-22-4). In contrast to full encoder PT on billions or trillions of tokens, CPT with as few as 100K tokens can be beneficial, particularly for the newly introduced weights in PEFT methods [\(Jukic and](#page-22-3) ´ [Snajder,](#page-22-3) [2023;](#page-22-3) [Gnehm et al.,](#page-18-1) [2022\)](#page-18-1).

Catastrophic forgetting remains a risk, especially for longer training runs with decoders [\(Kala](#page-22-5)[jdzievski,](#page-22-5) [2024\)](#page-22-5), requiring regularization techniques [\(§ 5.4\)](#page-7-1) combined with PEFT methods [\(§ 3\)](#page-1-0) to preserve prior knowledge while adapting selectively [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022;](#page-24-0) [Jukic and Snajder](#page-22-3) ´ , [2023\)](#page-22-3). In contrast, encoder models benefit even from longer training on small in-domain corpora [\(Gnehm et al.,](#page-18-1) [2022\)](#page-18-1). Scaling laws for decoder model PT suggest equal scaling of PEFT parameters and data size [\(Kaplan et al.,](#page-22-6) [2020;](#page-22-6) [Hoffmann](#page-20-6) [et al.,](#page-20-6) [2022\)](#page-20-6). Crucially, careful attention to (inconspicuous) data contamination is essential, especially for public datasets, as the overlap between PT and evaluation sets can artificially inflate results [\(Ruder et al.,](#page-27-3) [2023\)](#page-27-3).

#### 4.2 Fine-tuning encoders and decoders

While encoder and decoder architectures differ in their PT objectives (masked vs. autoregressive language modeling), the challenge of adapting them with limited data shares many similarities. Both require strategies that enhance generalization, mitigate overfitting, and leverage inductive biases from PT. However, some methods naturally align more with one model type: encoderoriented techniques often operate in the representation space, while decoder-oriented ones emphasize task diversity and instruction-following. This section surveys approaches for both, highlighting where they converge and where their applicability diverges.

<span id="page-4-0"></span>Pattern-exploiting training (PET) reformulates classification as cloze-style language modeling for encoder models, aligning PT and FT objectives [\(Schick and Schütze,](#page-28-1) [2021a](#page-28-1)[,b\)](#page-28-2). It relies on handcrafted patterns and verbalizers to map inputs and labels to token sequences, making it effective for few-shot tasks in low-resource domains and languages [\(Lu et al.,](#page-24-2) [2023;](#page-24-2) [Ullah](#page-30-6) [et al.,](#page-30-6) [2023;](#page-30-6) [Song et al.,](#page-29-5) [2023;](#page-29-5) [Qi et al.,](#page-27-4) [2022\)](#page-27-4). Despite its success, inference often involves costly autoregressive decoding. Recent extensions mitigate this by introducing prototypical decoding [\(Karimi Mahabadi et al.,](#page-22-7) [2022\)](#page-22-7), label embedding learning, and evolutionary verbalizer optimization [\(Ling et al.,](#page-24-4) [2023\)](#page-24-4). While PET originated in the encoder setting, its principles (of using prompts to bridge PT and downstream objectives) have influenced instruction-tuning methods for decoders.

<span id="page-4-1"></span>Meta-learning. Another line of encoderfocused research improves generalization by learning inductive biases that facilitate rapid adaptation to new discriminative tasks. Metric-based methods encode class-specific prototypes in an embedding space for non-parametric inference [\(Snell et al.,](#page-28-3) [2017;](#page-28-3) [Wen et al.,](#page-31-1) [2021\)](#page-31-1), while contrastive and triplet-based strategies further refine the latent space [\(Wu et al.,](#page-31-2) [2024;](#page-31-2) [Pauli et al.,](#page-26-2) [2023\)](#page-26-2). Optimization-based methods, such as model-agnostic meta-learning, learn initialization parameters in a two-level optimization that can quickly adapt to new tasks with few updates [\(Finn](#page-17-2) [et al.,](#page-17-2) [2017;](#page-17-2) [Bansal et al.,](#page-14-4) [2020;](#page-14-4) [Li et al.,](#page-23-3) [2022;](#page-23-3) [Huang et al.,](#page-21-4) [2023b;](#page-21-4) [Chien et al.,](#page-15-3) [2023\)](#page-15-3). Although less common today, meta-learning's emphasis on representation regularization and rapid adaptation continues to influence newer methods that operate in the representation space of both encoder and decoder models.

<span id="page-4-2"></span>Intermediate fine-tuning. Encoders also benefit from intermediate FT (or task-adaptive PT), where models are fine-tuned on related, label-rich tasks before the target task. This improves transfer by injecting domain-relevant inductive signals [\(Pruksachatkun et al.,](#page-27-5) [2020;](#page-27-5) [Vu et al.,](#page-30-3) [2020;](#page-30-3) [Phang et al.,](#page-27-6) [2019\)](#page-27-6). A closely related idea in decoder models is multi-task instruction tuning, where the model is exposed to a wide variety of datasets and instructions, scaling the benefits of intermediate supervision to heterogeneous task distributions. This distinction reflects the architectures: encoders profit from staged specialization, whereas decoders gain generalization by broad coverage of instructions.

<span id="page-4-3"></span>Multi-task instruction tuning. For decoder models, multi-task instruction tuning is the dominant paradigm for generalization. By fine-tuning on diverse datasets and instruction styles, models acquire robust zero- and few-shot instructionfollowing abilities [\(Wei et al.,](#page-30-7) [2021;](#page-30-7) [Aribandi](#page-13-4) [et al.,](#page-13-4) [2021;](#page-13-4) [Sanh et al.,](#page-28-4) [2022\)](#page-28-4). Compared to encoder-style intermediate fine-tuning, this approach is broader in scope, exposing decoders to heterogeneous task mixtures that yield checkpoints with faster convergence and stronger generalization to unseen tasks [\(Longpre et al.,](#page-24-5) [2023;](#page-24-5) [Liu](#page-24-0) [et al.,](#page-24-0) [2022\)](#page-24-0). However, task selection and mixing require care, as diversity can improve generalization but also cause negative task transfer [\(Wang](#page-30-8) [et al.,](#page-30-8) [2023d;](#page-30-8) [Xia et al.,](#page-31-3) [2024\)](#page-31-3).

Mixture of experts. To mitigate such tradeoffs, mixture-of-experts (MoE) architectures have emerged as a modular alternative for decoders. Inputs are routed to specialized subnetworks based on input semantics [\(Shazeer et al.,](#page-28-5) [2017;](#page-28-5) [Fedus](#page-17-3) [et al.,](#page-17-3) [2022\)](#page-17-3), via retrieval [\(Jang et al.,](#page-22-8) [2023\)](#page-22-8) or dynamic gating mechanisms [\(Ostapenko et al.,](#page-26-3) [2024;](#page-26-3) [Ponti et al.,](#page-27-7) [2022\)](#page-27-7), improving modularity and continual learning. MoE systems often outperform monolithic instruction-tuned baselines in zero- and few-shot generalization, especially for compositional reasoning [\(Muqeeth et al.,](#page-25-2) [2024\)](#page-25-2). Recent large-scale decoders combine instruction tuning with massive MoE setups to reduce negative transfer while improving efficiency with fewer active parameters [\(OpenAI et al.,](#page-26-4) [2025;](#page-26-4) [Meta AI,](#page-25-3) [2025\)](#page-25-3).

<span id="page-5-4"></span>Effective instruction design. Decoder performance is also sensitive to instruction formatting. Generalization improves with prompt diversity, sequence packing, and careful task mixture balancing [\(Sanh et al.,](#page-28-4) [2022;](#page-28-4) [Iyer et al.,](#page-21-5) [2023\)](#page-21-5). Mixed prompting setups (zero-shot, fewshot, chain-of-thought) further enhance robustness [\(Longpre et al.,](#page-24-5) [2023\)](#page-24-5). Inverting inputoutput roles and increasing lexical diversity acquire task-agnostic learning abilities [\(Min et al.,](#page-25-4) [2022\)](#page-25-4). These strategies not only equip models with robust in-context learning and reasoning abilities (critical for low-shot generalization; [Wei](#page-30-9) [et al.,](#page-30-9) [2022;](#page-30-9) [Liu et al.,](#page-24-6) [2023d\)](#page-24-6), but also accelerate convergence in subsequent specialized FT [\(Long](#page-24-5)[pre et al.,](#page-24-5) [2023\)](#page-24-5).

To mask or not to mask? A subtle design choice in decoder fine-tuning is whether to apply loss only on output tokens or also on prompt tokens. While the dominant practice masks loss on instruction tokens [\(Grattafiori et al.,](#page-18-2) [2024;](#page-18-2) [Ouyang et al.,](#page-26-1) [2022;](#page-26-1) [Wei et al.,](#page-30-7) [2021\)](#page-30-7), recent findings show that including prompt tokens as regularization improves generalization when instructions are long and data is limited (<15k examples; [Shi et al.,](#page-28-6) [2024;](#page-28-6) [Huerta-Enochian and Ko,](#page-21-6) [2024\)](#page-21-6). Other forms of regularization, such as KL constraints against a base model may hurt instructionfollowing, while noise injection shows mixed results [\(Jain et al.,](#page-22-9) [2024\)](#page-22-9).

## <span id="page-5-0"></span>5 Fine-tuning for Specialization

Fine-tuning for specialization focuses on adapting models to domain- or language-specific tasks that fall outside general capabilities [\(Table 1\)](#page-3-1).

## <span id="page-5-1"></span>5.1 Embedding learning

Since fixed vocabularies limit generalization to new domains and languages, recent work retrains or augments token embeddings (often with frozen transformer body) to better match target data distributions [\(Hung et al.,](#page-21-7) [2023;](#page-21-7) [Artetxe et al.,](#page-13-5) [2020\)](#page-13-5). This can bridge vocabulary gaps efficiently, particularly in low-resource or cross-lingual settings. Subword-level tokenization remains standard for balancing efficiency and expressiveness [\(Kudo](#page-23-4) [and Richardson,](#page-23-4) [2018\)](#page-23-4), but it can fragment rare or domain-specific words. Entropy-based methods identify such tokens and selectively expand the vocabulary with new embeddings [\(Nag et al.,](#page-25-5) [2023\)](#page-25-5). Alternative strategies map embeddings from highresource to low-resource languages via seed dictionaries or aligned corpora [\(Mikolov et al.,](#page-25-6) [2013;](#page-25-6) [Minixhofer et al.,](#page-25-7) [2022\)](#page-25-7), or learn shared embedding spaces through alignment objectives [\(Cao](#page-14-5) [et al.,](#page-14-5) [2019;](#page-14-5) [Saadi et al.,](#page-28-7) [2022\)](#page-28-7).

#### 5.2 Contrastive and adversarial learning

Contrastive and adversarial objectives enhance adaptation by improving representation alignment across domains and languages and are compatible with PEFT methods [\(§ 3\)](#page-1-0).

<span id="page-5-2"></span>Contrastive learning pulls semantically similar pairs closer while pushing apart unrelated ones, improving both sentence- and word-level alignment in cross-lingual transfer [\(Chi et al.,](#page-15-4) [2021a](#page-15-4)[,b;](#page-15-5) [Gaschi et al.,](#page-18-3) [2023;](#page-18-3) [Hu et al.,](#page-21-8) [2023a\)](#page-21-8). It can be used on parallel corpora or via data augmentation for consistency, and integrates well with PEFT methods like adapters [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-7) [2023c,](#page-24-7)[b;](#page-24-8) [Ahmat](#page-13-6) [et al.,](#page-13-6) [2023\)](#page-13-6). In downstream tasks, contrastive objectives boost performance for classification and similarity tasks using class descriptions, anchor prompts, or QA reformulations [\(Gao et al.,](#page-17-4) [2021;](#page-17-4) [Pauli et al.,](#page-26-2) [2023;](#page-26-2) [Chen et al.,](#page-15-6) [2023c\)](#page-15-6).

<span id="page-5-3"></span>Adversarial learning improves generalization by training a discriminator that the model must deceive, encouraging domain- or language-invariant representations [\(Du et al.,](#page-16-9) [2020;](#page-16-9) [Grießhaber et al.,](#page-20-7) [2020;](#page-20-7) [Huang et al.,](#page-21-9) [2023c\)](#page-21-9). It is especially effective in the absence of parallel data and complements PEFT via language-specific adapters or domain-aware prefix tuning [\(Ngo Trung et al.,](#page-25-8) [2021;](#page-25-8) [Zhao et al.,](#page-33-0) [2022\)](#page-33-0).

### 5.3 Limited supervision

In low-resource scenarios, semi-supervised, unsupervised, and active learning methods can successfully leverage unlabeled data to boost model generalization and robustness.

<span id="page-6-0"></span>Semi-supervised learning uses unlabeled data to augment small labeled sets. Common approaches include self-training with pseudo-labels filtered by model confidence or entropy [\(Schick](#page-28-1) [and Schütze,](#page-28-1) [2021a;](#page-28-1) [Wang et al.,](#page-30-10) [2023b;](#page-30-10) [Chen](#page-15-7) [et al.,](#page-15-7) [2020\)](#page-15-7), and consistency regularization to encourage stable predictions under perturbations [\(Sohn et al.,](#page-29-6) [2020;](#page-29-6) [Xie et al.,](#page-31-4) [2020\)](#page-31-4). Co-training variants train multiple views of the data to improve robustness [\(Clark et al.,](#page-15-8) [2018\)](#page-15-8). These methods are particularly effective in low-resource adaptation, where pre-trained models provide valuable supervision signals to extend the limited annotated data.

<span id="page-6-1"></span>Unsupervised methods often rely on consistency conditions such as input perturbation or cycle-consistency across modalities or domains [\(Zhu et al.,](#page-33-2) [2017\)](#page-33-2). These methods enable learning bidirectional relationships without parallel data, useful for cross-domain, cross-lingual, or style transfer settings [\(Lample et al.,](#page-23-5) [2018;](#page-23-5) [Ren et al.,](#page-27-8) [2019;](#page-27-8) [Karisani,](#page-22-10) [2022\)](#page-22-10). However, they typically require large unlabeled corpora and are most effective when combined with supervised signals.

<span id="page-6-2"></span>Active learning (AL) reduces annotation cost by selecting the most informative examples for labeling, typically using uncertainty (e.g., confidence, entropy, perplexity) or diversity (e.g., gradient similarity) criteria [\(Muradoglu and Hulden,](#page-25-9) [2022;](#page-25-9) [Yuan et al.,](#page-32-7) [2020;](#page-32-7) [François and Gay,](#page-17-5) [2023;](#page-17-5) [Karamcheti et al.,](#page-22-11) [2021\)](#page-22-11). Iterative sampling and model re-initialization improve stability [\(Lem](#page-23-6)[mens and Daelemans,](#page-23-6) [2023\)](#page-23-6). AL reduces the amount of labeled data needed for classification task sometimes even to 30% of the original size; can be beneficial above 1k samples, below any aditional labeled data works [\(Lemmens and Daele](#page-23-6)[mans,](#page-23-6) [2023\)](#page-23-6). It integrates well with PEFT methods like adapters and UniPELT, boosting sample efficiency in specialized tasks [\(Jukic and Snajder](#page-22-3) ´ , [2023\)](#page-22-3). The focus of AL on selective data efficiency aligns with findings that data quality often outweighs quantity in low-resource adaptation (see [§ 5.4](#page-6-3) and [§ 6.2\)](#page-10-0).

#### 5.4 Instruction tuning for specialization

Instruction tuning specializes decoder models by aligning them with user intent and leveraging structured task descriptions.[1](#page-6-4) Beyond multi-task setups aimed at generalization [\(§ 4.2\)](#page-4-3), clear, specific, and contextually grounded instructions consistently improve task performance, even under severe data constraints [\(Wang et al.,](#page-30-11) [2023c;](#page-30-11) [Fleming](#page-17-6) [et al.,](#page-17-6) [2024;](#page-17-6) [Zhang et al.,](#page-32-8) [2023b\)](#page-32-8). In contrast to the diverse instruction design required by multitask models [\(§ 4.2\)](#page-5-4), instruction format variety only brings marginal gain in task-specific performance, and can even be counterproductive in some cases [\(Chen et al.,](#page-15-9) [2023a\)](#page-15-9). The lack of consistency between train and evaluation prompts can lead to performance drops, larger models being more robust to unseen formats [\(Gupta et al.,](#page-20-8) [2023\)](#page-20-8).

Specialization vs. generalization. Just as largescale multi-task instruction tuning can hurt singletask performance, specializing too narrowly can reduce generalization [\(Wang et al.,](#page-30-8) [2023d;](#page-30-8) [Zhang](#page-32-6) [et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a;](#page-32-6) [Alabi et al.,](#page-13-2) [2022\)](#page-13-2). The best way to address this is by fine-tuning instruction-tuned models on the downstream task, which drastically improves sample efficiency, converges faster, and leads to overall better performance [\(Gupta et al.,](#page-20-8) [2023;](#page-20-8) [Longpre et al.,](#page-24-5) [2023\)](#page-24-5). Adding general instruction data during SFT and applying regularization [\(§ 5.4\)](#page-7-1) help maintain broader capabilities.

Scaling models and data. For multilingual closed-generation tasks (like translation or summarization), [Zhang et al.](#page-32-6) [\(2023a\)](#page-32-6) find that SFT performance scales with data according to a power law with diminishing returns after an elbow point (around 10k–40k samples for PEFT), depending on the task. SFT data has a larger impact on full FT than on PEFT, but full FT becomes justified after 100k to 1M samples. Surprisingly, model size has a larger impact than FT or PT data size and scaling PEFT parameters is virtually ineffective [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-6) [2023a\)](#page-32-6). The rationalization is that PEFT relies heavily on pre-trained knowledge, making model capacity more critical, especially for in-distribution tasks. For more out-ofdistribution or highly specific tasks, however, scaling fine-tuning data becomes more important, particularly in the early training phase.

<span id="page-6-3"></span>Identifying influential data is key to developing specialized capabilities efficiently. Targeted instruction tuning can match full-data performance with as few as 10–15k samples, highlighting the value of high-quality supervision [\(Xia](#page-31-3) [et al.,](#page-31-3) [2024;](#page-31-3) [Chen et al.,](#page-15-9) [2023a\)](#page-15-9). These approaches rely on statistical properties, similarity metrics, or

gradient-based signals [\(Xie et al.,](#page-31-5) [2023;](#page-31-5) [Zhang](#page-32-9) [et al.,](#page-32-9) [2018;](#page-32-9) [Xia et al.,](#page-31-3) [2024\)](#page-31-3), and show that a core subset remains effective across tasks and model scales. Beyond SFT, similar principles have been applied to filter CPT data based on task relevance [\(Xie et al.,](#page-31-5) [2023;](#page-31-5) [Mahapatra et al.,](#page-24-3) [2022\)](#page-24-3), offering practical tools for denoising, data selection, or active augmentation [\(§ 5.3\)](#page-6-2).

<span id="page-7-1"></span>Catastrophic forgetting mitigation. To prevent overfitting and preserve pre-trained knowledge during SFT with limited data, regularization techniques are vital. Early methods like layer-wise learning rate decay (LLRD), weight decay, and Mixout remain widely used, especially in encoder models [\(Howard and Ruder,](#page-21-10) [2018;](#page-21-10) [Wiese et al.,](#page-31-6) [2017;](#page-31-6) [Lee et al.,](#page-23-7) [2019\)](#page-23-7). For instruction tuning tasks with short outputs (e.g., classification, QA), applying a small loss weight (0.01–0.1) to the prompt tokens helps to avoid overfitting [\(Huerta-](#page-21-6)[Enochian and Ko,](#page-21-6) [2024\)](#page-21-6). KL divergence is commonly used in LLM alignment to constrain outputs, but less so in standard SFT [\(Ouyang et al.,](#page-26-1) [2022\)](#page-26-1). Regardless of model size, learning rate warmup and small batch sizes consistently improve training stability with limited data [\(Buono](#page-14-6)[core et al.,](#page-14-6) [2023;](#page-14-6) [Xu et al.,](#page-31-7) [2021a\)](#page-31-7). Dropout and gradient clipping are also standard practices [\(Wolf](#page-31-8) [et al.,](#page-31-8) [2020;](#page-31-8) [von Werra et al.,](#page-31-9) [2020\)](#page-31-9).

Hyperparameters for low-resource SFT Despite task and model differences, several practical defaults consistently yield strong performance in low-resource SFT. Learning rates between 5e-6 and 5e-5 are typical, with 2e-5 proving effective across popular models like Alpaca and Llama [\(Pareja et al.,](#page-26-5) [2024;](#page-26-5) [Jin et al.,](#page-22-12) [2023;](#page-22-12) [Grattafiori](#page-18-2) [et al.,](#page-18-2) [2024\)](#page-18-2). When tuning, scaling the learning rate by a factor of 3 is a simple yet effective heuristic. Small batch sizes (1–8 per GPU) with gradient accumulation are favored for memory efficiency and better generalization [\(Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b;](#page-16-6) [Xu](#page-31-7) [et al.,](#page-31-7) [2021a\)](#page-31-7). While 2–3 epochs often suffice, training longer (up to 20-25 epochs) can help in extreme low-data regimes, provided early stopping is used to prevent overfitting [\(Muennighoff](#page-25-10) [et al.,](#page-25-10) [2023a;](#page-25-10) [Liu et al.,](#page-24-9) [2024a\)](#page-24-9). Other stable choices include AdamW, cosine decay learning rate schedule with warmup ratios of 3–5%, and gradient clipping set to 1. LoRA fine-tuning commonly uses rank and alpha values of 16 with 0.05 dropout [\(von Werra et al.,](#page-31-9) [2020\)](#page-31-9).

## <span id="page-7-0"></span>6 Preference Alignment

While fine-tuning equips PLMs with task- and domain-specific knowledge, it often falls short of aligning outputs with nuanced human preferences, especially when data is limited [\(Ganguli et al.,](#page-17-7) [2022;](#page-17-7) [Bai et al.,](#page-13-7) [2022a\)](#page-13-7). Preference alignment (PA; [Table 2\)](#page-8-0) addresses this gap by training models with ranked or scored feedback to better capture task-specific objectives like structure, helpfulness, truthfulness, or biases [\(Ziegler et al.,](#page-33-3) [2020;](#page-33-3) [Christiano et al.,](#page-15-10) [2017;](#page-15-10) [Ramamurthy et al.,](#page-27-9) [2022\)](#page-27-9). This step usually follows supervised fine-tuning (SFT), once the model has already adapted to the task and domain. Without this prior adaptation, models tend to struggle with preference learning and exhibit higher rates of hallucination [\(Tunstall](#page-29-7) [et al.,](#page-29-7) [2024;](#page-29-7) [Ethayarajh et al.,](#page-17-8) [2024;](#page-17-8) [Hong et al.,](#page-21-11) [2024\)](#page-21-11). Refer to [Table 3](#page-9-0) for a concise overview of preference alignment objectives.

<span id="page-7-2"></span>Alignment with RL. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) aligns model behavior by training a reward model to score outputs based on human preferences, followed by optimizing the model using RL algorithms like PPO [\(Ouyang et al.,](#page-26-1) [2022;](#page-26-1) [Ziegler et al.,](#page-33-3) [2020;](#page-33-3) [Schulman et al.,](#page-28-8) [2017\)](#page-28-8). While effective, this process is resource-intensive (requiring large-scale human annotations, careful hyperparameter tuning, and substantial compute), making it inaccessible for most organizations [\(Touvron et al.,](#page-29-8) [2023;](#page-29-8) [Rafailov et al.,](#page-27-10) [2023;](#page-27-10) [Gao et al.,](#page-17-9) [2023\)](#page-17-9). Variants like RLAIF replace human-labeled rewards with scores from an LLM during training, reducing cost while maintaining comparable performance [\(Lee](#page-23-8) [et al.,](#page-23-8) [2024\)](#page-23-8). However, RLAIF can be less coherent than RLHF, and both methods often produce longer outputs than SFT models, with occasional hallucinations. Despite RLHF's strengths, its instability and cost have spurred interest in more stable and efficient PA alternatives.

<span id="page-7-3"></span>Direct preference alignment. Recent methods simplify PA by eliminating the need for separate reward models and RL. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; [Rafailov et al.,](#page-27-10) [2023\)](#page-27-10) reframes alignment as a binary classification problem over preference pairs, avoiding LM sampling, reward model training, and heavy hyperparameter tuning. <span id="page-8-2"></span>Alignment during SFT. Another direction integrates PA directly into the SFT stage, removing the need for additional alignment steps. Careful curation of SFT datasets, such as filtering for specific structure or style, can yield strong alignment performance on its own, without dedicated preference data [\(Zhou et al.,](#page-33-4) [2023;](#page-33-4) [Li et al.,](#page-23-9) [2023;](#page-23-9) [Dong](#page-16-10) [et al.,](#page-16-10) [2023;](#page-16-10) [Yuan et al.,](#page-32-10) [2023\)](#page-32-10). Chain of Hindsight [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-10) [2023a\)](#page-24-10) presents both a good and bad response during training and teaches the model to prefer the good one at inference time, embedding preference signals without explicit reward modeling. These methods rely on high-quality SFT data and can serve as effective precursors to alignment techniques like DPO, emphasizing that data quality (and not quantity) is often the limiting factor [\(§ 6.2\)](#page-10-0), and that well-filtered SFT can be a lightweight, effective form of alignment.

IPO, especially, is less prone to reward hacking, achieving lower KL divergence under the same constraints [\(Rafailov](#page-27-11) [et al.,](#page-27-11) [2024\)](#page-27-11). DPO scales better than PPO to models of size up to 405B, but might require regualrization (e.g., additional negative-log-likelihood (NLL) term with a coefficient of 0.2; [Grattafiori](#page-18-2) [et al.,](#page-18-2) [2024\)](#page-18-2). Contrary to reward modeling, DPO variants can be susceptible to label noise, as rankings are rarely flipped during alignment [\(Chen](#page-14-8) [et al.,](#page-14-8) [2024a\)](#page-14-8). KTO is more sample efficient by learning from weaker binary signals and outperforms DPO in noisy or imbalanced datasets, especially in the 1B–30B parameter range [\(Etha](#page-17-8)[yarajh et al.,](#page-17-8) [2024\)](#page-17-8). JPO improves data efficiency by directly comparing instruction-response pairs, although a comparison might be ambiguous. In general, masking out formatting tokens is recommended in objectives of contrasting nature to avoid conflicting signals [\(Grattafiori et al.,](#page-18-2) [2024\)](#page-18-2).

Reference-free methods further reduce compute requirements by dispensing with reference models, and can be particularly effective in lowresource settings. For instance, CPO pushes translation performance beyond DPO using just 22k samples [\(Xu et al.,](#page-31-10) [2024\)](#page-31-10). ORPO is on par with DPO, but produces more consistent and diverse outputs [\(Hong et al.,](#page-21-11) [2024\)](#page-21-11). SimPO delivers superior performance across multiple benchmarks, although the underlying reasons for its effectiveness lack theoretical justification [\(Meng et al.,](#page-25-11) [2024\)](#page-25-11). Despite their efficiency, all reference-free approaches are more susceptible to reward hacking, especially when fine-tuning the full model [\(Chen et al.,](#page-14-8) [2024a\)](#page-14-8).

Combining alignment objectives with SFT loss improves stability and performance for reasoning or domain-specific tasks [\(Xu et al.,](#page-31-10) [2024;](#page-31-10) [Meng](#page-25-11) [et al.,](#page-25-11) [2024\)](#page-25-11). Integrating PA into SFT presents a lightweight alternative that requires only highquality instruction data [\(Zhou et al.,](#page-33-4) [2023\)](#page-33-4). SPIN, in particular, achieves DPO-level performance with less data, however, it requires multiple training iterations, trading memory efficiency for time [\(Chen et al.,](#page-15-11) [2024b\)](#page-15-11).

In practice, DPO and its variants remain strong defaults for general use. Reference-free methods are ideal for compute-constrained settings but demand careful regularization. Alignment during SFT is attractive when preference data is unavailable and curation quality is high. #### <span id="page-10-0"></span>6.2 Preference data criteria

Format and availability. Preference datasets typically consist of chosen/rejected pairs derived from instruction-response prompts [\(Ouyang et al.,](#page-26-1) [2022;](#page-26-1) [Rafailov et al.,](#page-27-10) [2023;](#page-27-10) [Meng et al.,](#page-25-11) [2024\)](#page-25-11). Some approaches extend this to comparisons across prompts (JPO) or simple binary signals on the output (KTO), but the overall availability remains limited, with most datasets ranging from thousands to 1M samples and predominantly in English [\(Argilla,](#page-13-9) [2024;](#page-13-9) [Liu,](#page-24-11) [2025\)](#page-24-11).

Synthetic feedback as a scalable solution. Collecting high-quality human preference data is resource-intensive. A widely adopted alternative is using synthetic feedback from strong LLMs guided by curated principles [\(Bai et al.,](#page-14-9) [2022b\)](#page-14-9). This enables broader scalability while maintaining quality when human oversight is present. Methods like SPIN go further by generating negative examples automatically via self-play, relying solely on an initial set of desirable outputs [\(Chen et al.,](#page-15-11) [2024b\)](#page-15-11). KTO also facilitates feedback collection by supporting imbalanced binary labels instead of strict pairwise comparisons [\(Ethayarajh et al.,](#page-17-8) [2024\)](#page-17-8).

When feedback is derived from strong teacher models (e.g., GPT-4 or Claude), interestingly, SFT has been observed to rival preference-based alignment methods like DPO or RL [\(Sharma et al.,](#page-28-10) [2024\)](#page-28-10). This can be partly due to insufficient diversity or a weak student model not exceeding the quality of the synthetic data during exploration. Diminishing returns beyond a few thousand samples suggest the importance of selection and coverage rather than raw volume.

Data quality over quantity. Multiple studies consistently show that a small number of highquality, diverse examples outperforms larger, noisier datasets [\(Zhou et al.,](#page-33-4) [2023;](#page-33-4) [Shen et al.,](#page-28-9) [2024\)](#page-28-9). For instance, SPIN matches its original 50ksample performance using just 1.8k well-curated examples, and ORPO remains effective at 141B scale with only 7k samples [\(Argilla and Man](#page-13-10)[tisNLP,](#page-13-10) [2024\)](#page-13-10). Similar trends hold across alignment methods: as few as 1k–10k diverse, welllabeled examples can outperform tens or hundreds of thousands of lower-quality instances [\(Rafailov](#page-27-10) [et al.,](#page-27-10) [2023;](#page-27-10) [Zhou et al.,](#page-33-4) [2023;](#page-33-4) [Shen et al.,](#page-28-9) [2024\)](#page-28-9). For certain desired behaviors, like adhering to output structure specifications or coherent multi-turn conversations, a handful of high-quality examples can remarkably boost alignment [\(Zhou et al.,](#page-33-4) [2023\)](#page-33-4). These results underscore the value of filtering and targeted data collection over naive scaling.

Both human-labeled and synthetic preference datasets frequently contain noise, contradictions, or intransitive preferences [\(Ethayarajh et al.,](#page-17-8) [2024\)](#page-17-8). These issues can undermine alignment objectives unless mitigated by clear annotation guidelines and consistent labeling practices. Additionally, prompting strategies like CoT can help improve the reliability of synthetic data, though techniques like few-shot prompting yield mixed results [\(Lee et al.,](#page-23-8) [2024\)](#page-23-8).

#### 6.3 Scaling dynamics for model and data

PA performance generally follows a logarithmic scaling trend with respect to both model size and dataset size [\(Gao et al.,](#page-17-9) [2023\)](#page-17-9). For RLHF, increasing the size of the reward or policy model yields diminishing returns, and more data leads to better generalization but with progressively smaller gains. Notably, larger models do not overfit faster nor diverge more from the initial policy, contradicting earlier intuitions. A minimum dataset size threshold is required to surpass near-random alignment performance. For example, at least 2k preference pairs are necessary for PPO to move beyond baseline loss, independent of the reward model size. Moreover, data scaling is generally more impactful than reward model scaling: a 2-fold increase in data is better than a 4-fold increase in model size. For instance, a 680M model trained on 16k (32k) samples outperforms a 3B model with only 8k (16k) (see Figure 10 in [Gao et al.,](#page-17-9) [2023\)](#page-17-9).

In both RLHF and direct alignment methods, higher KL divergence budgets tend to degrade performance, often even before a full epoch is completed. Models frequently reach optimal performance at partial epochs (e.g., ~23k samples) with lower KL constraints [\(Rafailov et al.,](#page-27-11) [2024\)](#page-27-11). Smaller models, under the same constraints, achieve higher KL divergence and exhibit reward hacking earlier, while models around 7B parameters or more show significantly improved sample efficiency and stability. With limited capacity or overly strict KL constraints, models tend to over-exploit spurious cues like verbosity. Length regularization alone does not fully prevent such behaviors [\(Rafailov et al.,](#page-27-11) [2024\)](#page-27-11).

Over-saturating SFT with synthetic completions can lead to marginal gains on alignment benchmarks, as observed with LLaMA-7B performance plateauing beyond 5k examples [\(Sharma et al.,](#page-28-10) [2024\)](#page-28-10). This suggests that, beyond a point, SFT may be insufficient to further improve alignment, especially when data quality is limited, highlighting the need for more targeted alignment methods.

## 6.4 Effect on downstream task performance

PA can have mixed effects on downstream task performance, depending on the alignment method, model initialization, and task type. While alignment methods often improve truthfulness, commonsense knowledge and reasoning likely due to overlap with preference data, they may slightly reduce performance on knowledge-intensive tasks [\(Meng et al.,](#page-25-11) [2024\)](#page-25-11). For instance, mathematical reasoning performance can significantly deteriorate for methods without a regularization SFT loss term, like CPO or ORPO. [Tunstall et al.](#page-29-7) [\(2024\)](#page-29-7) report that DPO alignment starts to hurt downstream performance after a single epoch.

Preference-aligned models often produce longer outputs than those trained with SFT alone, even when the objective includes length regularization [\(Park et al.,](#page-26-6) [2024;](#page-26-6) [Meng et al.,](#page-25-11) [2024\)](#page-25-11). However, verbosity shows weak correlation with KL divergence, implying that human preferences also depend on factors like repetition and tone, which may affect downstream performance. Overall, the effects of alignment strategies on downstream outcomes remain underexplored, particularly in relation to model scale, output style, and non-English settings, highlighting a need for more systematic evaluation.

## 7 Discussion

Encode or decode? Choosing the appropriate model architecture is critical for optimizing performance across NLP tasks, particularly in resource-constrained or domain-specific settings [\(Alabi et al.,](#page-13-11) [2020\)](#page-13-11). While large decoderonly models such as GPT-4 or LLaMA can be adapted to any NLP task via prompt-based reformulations [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-6) [2023d\)](#page-24-6), they often require substantial computational resources for training and inference. In contrast, encoder-only models like RoBERTa or DeBERTa [\(Liu et al.,](#page-24-12) [2019;](#page-24-12) [He et al.,](#page-20-9) [2020\)](#page-20-9) are generally more efficient for tasks centered on natural language understanding (NLU), such as classification, named entity recognition, and extractive QA, often outperforming decoder models with far fewer parameters [\(Benayas](#page-14-10) [et al.,](#page-14-10) [2024;](#page-14-10) [Qorib et al.,](#page-27-12) [2024;](#page-27-12) [García-Díaz et al.,](#page-17-10) [2023;](#page-17-10) [Schick and Schütze,](#page-28-1) [2021a\)](#page-28-1). Their bidirectional attention and dense token supervision (i.e., predicting multiple masked tokens per input sequence) during training enhances sample efficiency and makes them especially effective in lowresource scenarios [\(Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b;](#page-16-6) [Karimi Ma](#page-22-7)[habadi et al.,](#page-22-7) [2022\)](#page-22-7). For instance, RoBERTa (335M) achieves performance close to T5 (11B) despite having an order of magnitude fewer parameters [\(Ding et al.,](#page-16-6) [2023b\)](#page-16-6). While large decoder models dominate many mainstream applications, encoder models remain highly effective in domains requiring deep semantic understanding, limited supervision, and efficient inference. Their fixed-length embeddings and fast processing make them well-suited for tasks like retrieval, reranking, and domain-specific classification. Ultimately, architecture choice should be driven by task requirements, the trade-off between understanding and generation needs, and resource constraints. [Table 4](#page-34-0) summarizes effective encoderbased approaches for low-resource discriminative NLP tasks.

Cross-lingual transfer remains essential for deploying LLMs in multilingual settings, especially low-resource languages. Strong zero-shot generalization has been observed even when some languages were minimally present during PT [\(Armengol-Estapé et al.,](#page-13-12) [2022\)](#page-13-12). Transfer improves with language similarity (via word order or subword overlap; [Deshpande et al.,](#page-16-11) [2022\)](#page-16-11), and can further be enhanced using PEFT methods like adapters or prefix-tuning [\(Wu et al.,](#page-31-11) [2023;](#page-31-11) [Tu](#page-29-3) [et al.,](#page-29-3) [2024\)](#page-29-3). Cross-lingual prompting offers a training-free strategy for low-resource languages, with English prompts yielding more consistent results in few-shot settings [\(Lin et al.,](#page-23-10) [2022;](#page-23-10) [Huang](#page-21-12) [et al.,](#page-21-12) [2023a\)](#page-21-12). Prompting the model to think in English before responding in the target language further improves task performance via CoT reasoning [\(Qin et al.,](#page-27-13) [2023\)](#page-27-13). Translating training data generally outperforms direct transfer or translating test data in domain-specific tasks [\(Gaschi et al.,](#page-18-3) [2023\)](#page-18-3). Finally, SFT using multilingual prompts (not just English) leads to better cross-lingual generalization [\(Muennighoff et al.,](#page-25-12) [2023b\)](#page-25-12).

Model merging has emerged as a practical and effective strategy to enhance LLM capabilities without requiring additional training or ensembling overhead. By operating directly in weight space, merging enables the combination of models trained under different configurations, such as various data, hyperparameters, or training objectives (e.g., CPT, SFT, DPO/ORPO) into a single, stronger model [\(Lu et al.,](#page-24-13) [2025;](#page-24-13) [Goddard et al.,](#page-18-4) [2024\)](#page-18-4). Techniques like Model Soup [\(Wortsman](#page-31-12) [et al.,](#page-31-12) [2022\)](#page-31-12) and SLERP [\(Shoemake,](#page-28-11) [1985\)](#page-28-11) allow smooth interpolation between models, often leading to better downstream performance than any individual component. Notably, model merging contributed to top-ranked models on the Open LLM Leaderboard, being frequently adopted for the state of the art [\(Grattafiori et al.,](#page-18-2) [2024\)](#page-18-2). It has also proven effective for low-resource languages, where merging models with complementary capabilities outperforms conventional CPT followed by SFT [\(Tao et al.,](#page-29-9) [2024\)](#page-29-9). However, such benefits appear limited in smaller models (e.g., <2B parameters), suggesting scaling is crucial for emergent behaviors [\(Lu et al.,](#page-24-13) [2025\)](#page-24-13).

## 8 Conclusion

Fine-tuning language models under data-scarce conditions remains a central challenge for practitioners and researchers aiming to build highperforming systems without the extensive resources typically required for large-scale training. This survey presents a structured and pragmatic overview of the entire post-pretraining development pipeline, reviewing and organizing methods across parameter-efficient fine-tuning, domain and cross lingual generalization, specialization, and preference alignment. We highlight promising use cases, trade-offs, and best practices grounded in current empirical evidence. Our findings underscore that larger models are consistently more sample-efficient. Further, data quality remains more critical than quantity, supporting the view that most capabilities are learned during pretraining, with fine-tuning and alignment primarily shaping model behavior. We emphasize that despite the growing dominance of decoder-based models, encoder models remain competitive for discriminative tasks, and hybrid architectures provide promising avenues (e.g. RAG). In preference alignment, the lack of correlation between alignment loss and task performance [\(Rafailov](#page-27-11) [et al.,](#page-27-11) [2024\)](#page-27-11) points to a deeper calibration issue that requires better metrics and theoretical grounding. Model merging can be surprisingly effective, yet the nonlinear interactions underlying this behavior are poorly understood, motivating further research. Finally, we stress the importance of transparent reporting of datasets, training protocols, and evaluation metrics for reproducibility and fair comparison, especially for non-English and domain-specific tasks, where current benchmarks often fall short for assessing real-world applicability.

## <span id="page-33-1"></span>A Task-specific training methods

This section serves as justification and further contextualization for the task-specific suggestions in [Table 4.](#page-34-0)

## A.1 Text classification

In ultra-low-resource settings (2–8 examples per class), contrastive learning (CL) methods like fewshot sentence transformers trained with triplet loss and anchoring on class descriptions are effective [\(Pauli et al.,](#page-26-2) [2023\)](#page-26-2). Reformulating classification as NLI improves few-shot performance by enabling transfer from general-purpose NLI datasets [\(Laurer et al.,](#page-23-11) [2024\)](#page-23-11), and is especially effective with <1k examples. For similar data sizes, patternexploiting training (PET) consistently outperforms full FT across models like RoBERTa, XLM-R, and DistilBERT on NLI tasks, topic/sentiment classification [\(Schick and Schütze,](#page-28-1) [2021a;](#page-28-1) [Ullah](#page-30-6) [et al.,](#page-30-6) [2023;](#page-30-6) [Ling et al.,](#page-24-4) [2023\)](#page-24-4), and can be improved by using label embeddings and adapters [\(Karimi Mahabadi et al.,](#page-22-7) [2022\)](#page-22-7).

For <1k examples, prefix tuning and UniPELT outperform full FT with BERT, especially when combined with CPT on unlabeled data ( 75k tokens) [\(Jukic and Snajder](#page-22-3) ´ , [2023\)](#page-22-3). Soft prompt tuning is also superior in few-shot settings (2–64 shots), particularly with domain-specific models like ClinicalBERT and SciBERT [\(Goswami et al.,](#page-18-0) [2023\)](#page-18-0). Domain adaptation (CPT) with token millions, including vocabulary injection and last-layer tuning, further boosts performance on document classification [\(Gnehm et al.,](#page-18-1) [2022\)](#page-18-1). Prefix tuning works well up to 1k samples, while LoRA improves between 1k–10k, and full FT becomes competitive beyond that [\(Mao et al.,](#page-25-1) [2022\)](#page-25-1). Regularization methods like layerwise learning rate decay (LLRD) and mixout help stabilize training with BERT on GLUE tasks under 10k examples [\(Zhang et al.,](#page-32-11) [2021\)](#page-32-11). Active learning becomes impactful above 1k samples and can reduce labeling needs by up to 70% [\(Lemmens and Daelemans,](#page-23-6) [2023;](#page-23-6) [Ein-Dor et al.,](#page-16-12) [2020;](#page-16-12) [Yuan et al.,](#page-32-7) [2020\)](#page-32-7).

For higher-resource setups (~10k examples), full FT performs best, especially when paired with gradient clipping and small batch sizes for stability in reading comprehension [\(Xu et al.,](#page-31-7) [2021a\)](#page-31-7). Finally, for tasks like NLI, even a subset of 16k examples (1.9M tokens) selected through clustering and coreset sampling is sufficient to train competitive decoder models like Galactica 1.3B [\(Chen](#page-15-9) [et al.,](#page-15-9) [2023a\)](#page-15-9).

#### A.3 Text comparison

CL proves to be a powerful pretraining strategy for improving semantic representations in tasks like NLI or paraphrase detection. Models like DeBERTa large (435M) benefit significantly from CL pretraining when used for zero- or few-shot textual entailment [\(Kowsher et al.,](#page-23-12) [2023\)](#page-23-12). Similarly, SimCSE and ConSERT show that both unsupervised CL and supervised NLI-based learning greatly enhance sentence embeddings without labeled task data [\(Gao et al.,](#page-17-4) [2021;](#page-17-4) [Yan et al.,](#page-32-12) [2021\)](#page-32-12). For ultra-low supervision (16–64 examples), PEFT techniques consistently outperform full FT. RoBERTa large (355M), using a clozestyle format with adapters and label embedding learning, surpasses FT, BitFit, PET, and prompt tuning with just 16 samples per label [\(Karimi Ma](#page-22-7)[habadi et al.,](#page-22-7) [2022\)](#page-22-7). PET also performs well for multi-level implicit discourse relation recognition using RoBERTa [\(Zhao et al.,](#page-32-13) [2023a\)](#page-32-13). In multilingual and cross-lingual comparison tasks, PET combined with CL consistency loss shows strong results. On tasks like XNLI and paraphrase identification, models such as XLM-R base (279M), XLM-R large (561M), and InfoXLM large (561M) benefit from this hybrid approach, especially in 16–256-shot learning settings [\(Qi](#page-27-4) [et al.,](#page-27-4) [2022\)](#page-27-4). A CL framework using 500 parallel sentence pairs, supplemented with up to 10k nonparallel instances, establishes strong information retrieval performance across languages [\(Hu et al.,](#page-21-8) [2023a\)](#page-21-8). For 1k–10k instances, lightweight tuning strategies remain efficient: adapter-based tuning works well below 1k examples, while LoRA is effective for 1k–10k, as shown on BARTlarge across text comparison tasks [\(Mao et al.,](#page-25-1) [2022\)](#page-25-1).

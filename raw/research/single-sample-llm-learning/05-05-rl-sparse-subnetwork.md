# REINFORCEMENT LEARNING FINE-TUNES A SPARSE SUBNETWORK IN LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS

A PREPRINT

[Andrii Balashov](https://orcid.org/0009-0007-5833-0888)

Department of Computer Science, Information Technology, and Applied Mathematics Ukrainian State University of Science and Technologies, ESI "Prydniprovska State Academy of Civil Engineering and Architecture Dnipro, Ukraine 49000 andbalashov@hotmail.com

#### ABSTRACT

Reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a critical post-pretraining step for aligning large language models (LLMs) with complex tasks and human preferences Bai et al. [\[1,](#page-14-0) [2\]](#page-14-1), Bayazit et al. [\[3\]](#page-14-2), Chen et al. [\[4\]](#page-14-3), Chu et al. [\[5\]](#page-14-4). It is often assumed that achieving new behaviors via RL finetuning requires updating most of the model's parameters Cui et al. [\[6\]](#page-14-5), Dai et al. [\[7\]](#page-14-6), Davelouis et al. [\[8\]](#page-14-7), DeepSeek-AI et al. [\[9\]](#page-14-8), Dong et al. [\[10\]](#page-14-9). This work challenges that assumption with surprising findings: RL fine-tuning effectively modifies only a small *subnetwork* (typically 5–30% of parameters) of an LLM, leaving the majority of weights essentially unchanged. We refer to this phenomenon as RL-induced parameter update sparsity. It occurs intrinsically, without any explicit sparsity constraints or parameter-efficient tuning techniques. We observe this sparsity consistently across seven diverse RL algorithms (PPO, GRPO, ORPO, KTO, DPO, SimPO, and PRIME) Duan et al. [\[11\]](#page-14-10), Ethayarajh et al. [\[12\]](#page-14-11), Frankle and Carbin [\[13\]](#page-14-12), Fu et al. [\[14\]](#page-14-13), Graesser et al. [\[15\]](#page-14-14), Hendrycks et al. [\[16\]](#page-14-15), Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16) and multiple model families (e.g. OpenAI, Meta, and open-source LLMs). Furthermore, the particular subnetwork updated by RL shows significant overlap across different random seeds, training datasets, and even RL algorithms—far greater than expected by chance—indicating a partially *transferable structure* in the pretrained model Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). We find that fine-tuning only this subnetwork (with all other weights frozen) recovers the full RL-finetuned model's performance, and indeed produces a model nearly identical to the fully fine-tuned one in parameter space. Finally, we analyze why RL updates only a sparse subnetwork. Our evidence suggests the primary factor is that RL fine-tunes on data near the model's own distribution, requiring only small, targeted parameter shifts; methods that keep the policy close to the pre-trained model (e.g. KL regularization) and other implementation details (e.g. gradient clipping, on-policy vs off-policy updates) have limited effect on the overall sparsity. These findings deepen our understanding of RL-driven model adaptation. They imply that RL concentrates training on a small, consistently active subnetwork while leaving most weights inert, shedding new light on why RL fine-tuning tends to preserve pretrained capabilities better than supervised fine-tuning Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19). This opens the door to more efficient RL fine-tuning methods that exploit the intrinsic update sparsity (e.g. by focusing computation on the subnetwork), and provides a new perspective on the lottery ticket hypothesis in the context of LLM alignment.

In this work, we present evidence that *it does not*. Our study reveals that standard RL fine-tuning of LLMs induces a remarkably sparse parameter update: only a small fraction of weights change meaningfully, while the majority remain effectively untouched. We term this phenomenon *parameter update sparsity under RL fine-tuning*. Crucially, this sparsity arises *without* any sparsity-promoting regularizers or parameter-efficient training methods. In other words, even when allowed to modify all weights, RL predominantly fine-tunes a tiny subnetwork of the model.

Finding 1 (Parameter Update Sparsity in RL). Across a wide range of LLMs and RL algorithms, we find that RL fine-tuning updates only about 5%–30% of model parameters, leaving the rest unchanged within numerical tolerance. For example, in our experiments with several open-source RLHF models, 70%–95% of weights show no significant change after the RL stage (Table [1\)](#page-9-0) Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). This intrinsic sparsity is consistently observed across seven popular RL fine-tuning algorithms: PPODuan et al. [\[11\]](#page-14-10), TRPO (via PPO implementation) OpenAI [\[29\]](#page-14-28), Gated PPO ("GRPO") Ethayarajh et al. [\[12\]](#page-14-11), Off-Policy RLHF ("ORPO") Frankle and Carbin [\[13\]](#page-14-12), DPO Graesser et al. [\[15\]](#page-14-14), Simulated Preference Optimization ("SimPO") Hendrycks et al. [\[16\]](#page-14-15), and Process Reward Improvement ("PRIME") Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16). It also holds for diverse base models, including the Tulu-3 series (8B and 70B chat models) Ouyang et al. [\[30\]](#page-14-29), Eurus-2 (7B base models) Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16), Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17), DeepSeek-Math (7B models for math reasoning) Fu et al. [\[14\]](#page-14-13), Pan et al. [\[31\]](#page-14-30), and others. We emphasize that none of these cases used any explicit sparsity constraint or low-parameter training method—the sparsity emerges naturally from full-model RL fine-tuning.

Figure [1](#page-1-0) illustrates this effect by comparing the fraction of unchanged parameters after RL fine-tuning to that after supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the same models. RL updates are far sparser: in the example, only ∼10% of weights changed during SFT (dense update), versus ∼80% unchanged (i.e. 20% updated) in RL. This suggests that RLHF tends to preserve pretrained knowledge more strongly than SFT, an effect noted in recent studies Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19).

To concretely illustrate, one RL method (PRIME Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16)) updated roughly 20% of weights consistently (across seeds/data), an additional ∼8% of weights received non-zero gradient updates that canceled out over training (ending with no net change), and the remaining ∼72% were never effectively updated at all. This breakdown is visualized in Figure [2.](#page-2-0) It suggests that RL fine-tuning operates within a much smaller effective parameter space than the full model's, focusing on a subnetwork that drives behavioral changes while leaving the rest of the model untouched.

These observations motivate the following conjecture:

Conjecture 1. *If one identifies the subnetwork of parameters updated by RL fine-tuning, one can fine-tune only that subnetwork (freezing all other weights) and obtain a model nearly identical to the fully fine-tuned model, both in terms of test performance and parameter values.*

We formalize this idea in Section [4,](#page-10-0) but intuitively: let θinit be the pretrained model parameters and θfull the result after full-model RL fine-tuning. Define a binary mask m over parameters such that m<sup>i</sup> = 1 if parameter i was changed by RL (i.e. (θfull − θinit)<sup>i</sup> ̸= 0) and m<sup>i</sup> = 0 otherwise. Now fine-tune a fresh copy of the model on the same task, but at each gradient step multiply the gradient by m (so only parameters with m<sup>i</sup> = 1 are allowed to update). Let θsub denote the result of this *subnetwork-only* fine-tuning. Conjecture 1 states that θsub ≈ θfull (almost the same final weights, and hence performance).

We provide supporting evidence for this conjecture. In experiments on two RLHF methods (DPO and PRIME), training only the RL-identified subnetwork indeed recovers the original fine-tuned model's capabilities, even slightly *exceeding* the performance of full-model tuning in some cases (Table [3\)](#page-11-0) Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). Moreover, the resulting model parameters are extremely close: e.g. over 99.9% of θsub's weights match θfull within 10<sup>−</sup><sup>4</sup> relative difference. In other words, Conjecture 1 appears to hold in practice: the small subnetwork updated by RL is not only sufficient to attain the same reward, but the fine-tuned weights outside that subnetwork were largely unnecessary changes (even possibly slightly detrimental, as freezing them sometimes improved training stability).

Interestingly, although the parameter updates are sparse, they are *not low-rank*. If one were to rank-factorize the weight difference matrices (original vs RL-finetuned), their ranks are nearly full. For example, across models like Tulu-3 8B (DPO) or Eurus-2 7B (PRIME), we find the average update matrix rank is >99% of the matrix's full rank (Table [2\)](#page-9-1) Graesser et al. This distinguishes RL-induced sparsity from techniques like LoRA, which constrain updates to a low-dimensional subspace (low rank) Tan et al. [\[45\]](#page-15-0). RL's sparse subnetwork appears capable of representing nearly the full range of weight space changes, just using far fewer nonzero parameters than a dense update.

Our findings naturally prompt the question of *why* RL fine-tunes such a limited subnetwork. In Section [6,](#page-12-1) we investigate several hypotheses. A key factor appears to be the nature of RL training data: RLHF is an on-policy or near on-policy process, meaning the model is fine-tuned on distributions not far from its own outputs (often after a supervised prefinetuning on the same task)Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16), Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). Intuitively, when the model is learning on data drawn from its current policy (or a policy close to the pretrained model), achieving alignment requires relatively small, targeted shifts to model behavior. In contrast, standard supervised finetuning (SFT) often involves distributional shifts—e.g. human-written demonstrations or other data that diverge from the model's own generation style Mu et al. [\[28\]](#page-14-27)—which may necessitate more extensive parameter updates to accommodate. Indeed, we observe that SFT on out-of-distribution data yields much denser updates than RL on in-distribution data (Figure [1,](#page-1-0) prior studies echo this by noting RLHF tends to better preserve original model capabilities Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19)).

Other factors seem to play a lesser role. Techniques explicitly used to keep the RL policy close to the original (like KL-divergence regularization towards a reference model, as in PPO and variants Bai et al. [\[1\]](#page-14-0), Duan et al. [\[11\]](#page-14-10)) might intuitively cause fewer changes. However, even algorithms that do *not* use a KL penalty (such as DPO Graesser et al.

In our ablations, disabling or increasing the KL regularization in PPO and related methods did not substantially alter the fraction of weights updated. Similarly, gradient clipping—employed to stabilize RL training—did not explain the sparsity: removing clipping led to less stable training but not to meaningfully denser updates (it primarily caused larger changes in the same subset of weights). Our contributions have significant implications. First, they refine our understanding of how RLHF and related RL fine-tuning actually impact LLM internals. Rather than overhauling the entire network, RLHF nudges a relatively small set of weights. This provides a new perspective on recent observations that RLHF preserves pretraining knowledge better than SFT Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19)—we show this may be because RLHF simply leaves most weights (and thus knowledge) unchanged. Second, our Conjecture 1 and supporting results connect to the *Lottery Ticket Hypothesis* (LTH) in neural networks Pan et al. [\[32\]](#page-14-31). LTH posits that within a large model there exists a small subnetwork that, when trained in isolation, can match full-model performance. Our findings align with and extend LTH: in RLHF for LLMs, the "winning ticket" not only matches performance but reaches nearly the exact same optimized weights as full-model training (a much stronger condition). This suggests RLHF might inherently select for such tickets. Finally, by highlighting the sparse nature of RLHF updates, our work points toward practical innovations. If only 10–20% of weights need updating, one could design RL algorithms that explicitly fine-tune only those weights (once identified), saving computation and memory. Recent parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods aim to train fewer weights (e.g. adapters Sidahmed et al. [\[41\]](#page-15-1), LoRA Tan et al. [\[45\]](#page-15-0), etc.), often by design rather than discovery. Intriguingly, RLHF on LLMs achieves a form of PEFT *implicitly*. Future systems might leverage this by dynamically focusing training on a small subset of weights identified via an initial RL pass, or by using our overlap findings to reuse partially trained subnetworks across tasks/conditions.

### <span id="page-5-0"></span>2 Related Work and Background

#### 2.1 Related Work on LLM Fine-Tuning and Sparsity

RLHF and Post-Training Alignment. Our work concerns the fine-tuning of LLMs with reinforcement learning for alignment. RLHF has been widely adopted to adjust LLM behaviors Bai et al. [\[1\]](#page-14-0), Cui et al. [\[6\]](#page-14-5), Dai et al. [\[7\]](#page-14-6), Liu et al. [\[25\]](#page-14-24). Ouyang et al. [\[30\]](#page-14-29) fine-tuned GPT-3 with PPO on human preference data to produce InstructGPT, demonstrating substantial improvements in following instructions. Ziegler et al. [\[62\]](#page-15-2) (OpenAI) earlier showed that policy gradients could align language model outputs with human preference rankings in summarization tasks. Subsequent efforts have diversified RLHF algorithms: e.g. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) which avoids an explicit reward model Graesser et al. [\[15\]](#page-14-14), meta-optimizers like PRO to stabilize RLHF Frankle and Carbin [\[13\]](#page-14-12), distributional or negative preference optimization for alignment without positive feedback Graesser et al. [\[15\]](#page-14-14), Mu et al. [\[28\]](#page-14-27), and techniques for reference-free RLHF Hendrycks et al. [\[16\]](#page-14-15). All these works focused on training efficacy and resulting model quality. In contrast, our focus is on the *parameter-level effects* of RLHF on the model. There is relatively little prior research examining which weights RLHF actually changes. Our findings complement recent observations that RLHF fine-tuning tends to preserve pretrained knowledge and generalization better than supervised fine-tuning Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19), by revealing an underlying sparsity in the weight updates that could explain this preservation.

A concurrent study by Xiong et al. [\[53\]](#page-15-3) analyzed KL-constrained RLHF theoretically and found that it approximately performs gradient descent in the model's reward-weighted log-likelihood space. While their focus is theoretical and did not address parameter sparsity, it underpins why standard RLHF might not drastically deviate from the pretrained model. Other work has explored safe or efficient variants of RLHF, e.g. safe RLHF avoiding harmful outcomes DeepSeek-AI et al. [\[9\]](#page-14-8) or parameter-efficient RLHF using LoRA Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16). The latter (Sidahmed et al.) showed that using LoRA adapters during RLHF yields comparable reward performance with fewer trainable parameters. Our results show that full-model RLHF inherently ends up only updating a small subset of weights, which is an interesting parallel to the goals of parameter-efficient approaches. A recent survey by Zhou et al. [\[61\]](#page-15-4) covers RL-based reasoning in multimodal LLMs, but focuses on applications rather than parameter changes.

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning. A large body of work studies how to adapt pretrained models by tuning only a small number of parameters (for efficiency or modularity). This includes adding small adapter layers Sidahmed et al. [\[41\]](#page-15-1), tuning only bias terms (BitFit) Chen et al. [\[4\]](#page-14-3), prefix/prompt tuning Sokar et al. [\[42\]](#page-15-5), low-rank adaptations (LoRA) Tan et al. [\[45\]](#page-15-0), and more. These methods are usually applied deliberately in place of full fine-tuning. By contrast, we show that *even when doing full fine-tuning*, RLHF intrinsically behaves in a parameter-efficient manner, zeroing out the gradients for most weights. Our findings resonate with the concept of *intrinsic task subspaces*: Aghajanyan et al. [\[63\]](#page-15-6) demonstrated that fine-tuning large models effectively operates in a low *intrinsic dimensionality* space. Similarly, Liu et al. [\[24\]](#page-14-23) found that fine-tuning can be sparse and robust, identifying "winning tickets" in pretrained models for downstream tasks. Panigrahi et al. [\[34\]](#page-14-32) studied skill localization, observing that fine-tuning on specific tasks often affects only a subset of model weights (e.g. certain attention heads) Pan et al. [\[32\]](#page-14-31). Our work specifically examines RL-based fine-tuning and finds an even more extreme sparsity: not only does RLHF affect a small set of weights, but re-training just that set can recover full performance. This goes beyond prior evidence of task-specific subnetworks by showing near-identical final solutions (Section [4\)](#page-10-0).

There have been attempts to discover important subnetworks in language models. For example, Bayazit et al. [\[3\]](#page-14-2) identify "knowledge-critical" subnetworks that are crucial for factual knowledge, and Marks et al. [\[26\]](#page-14-25) and others attempt to find sparse circuits responsible for specific behaviors. Our results indicate that RLHF might naturally home in on such critical circuits for alignment tasks. We also relate to the *Lottery Ticket Hypothesis* (LTH) Pan et al. [\[32\]](#page-14-31). While LTH in NLP has usually involved pruning or rewinding weights in supervised fine-tuning Panda et al. [\[33\]](#page-14-33), Sidahmed et al. [\[41\]](#page-15-1), our findings can be seen as RLHF *itself* discovering a lottery ticket (the sparse subnetwork) that it trains to convergence. Recent work by Panda et al. [\[33\]](#page-14-33) applied lottery ticket pruning to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in sequential fine-tuning of LLMs, finding that training only a subnet can preserve prior knowledge. In RLHF, we found forgetting is minimal and prior abilities largely remain intact Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18), HuggingFace [\[20\]](#page-14-19), which aligns with the idea that RLHF mainly toggles a specific subnetwork and leaves the rest (and thus prior skills) untouched.

Dynamic Sparse Training in RL. Outside of language models, dynamic sparse training techniques train only a subset of weights that can change over time Panigrahi et al. [\[34\]](#page-14-32). Sokar et al. [\[42\]](#page-15-5) and Tan et al. [\[45\]](#page-15-0) applied such ideas in deep RL for vision-based tasks, showing that training with sparse weight updates can reduce complexity without hurting performance. Those works *impose* sparsity during training, whereas in our scenario the sparsity arises naturally. Interestingly, we observe that RLHF starts with more widespread weight changes early in training, then prunes down to a stable subnetwork (Figure [3b](#page-3-0)), which is reminiscent of dynamic sparse training algorithms that drop and regrow

weights. However, RLHF was not explicitly designed to do this, suggesting it might be an emergent property of the optimization. Another work by Davelouis et al. [\[8\]](#page-14-7) studied how induced sparsity in network structure can affect deep RL training dynamics (not specifically in language models). Our work can be seen as examining the converse: how RL training dynamics induce sparsity.

Background: LLM RL Fine-Tuning Setup. We briefly outline our experimental setup and definitions for clarity. We assume a pretrained LLM with parameters θinit. In supervised fine-tuning (SFT), the model is further trained on human-written instruction-response pairs (or other annotated data) via standard supervised learning. In RLHF (RL from human feedback), one first trains a reward model to assess model outputs, then fine-tunes the LLM (policy) with RL to maximize that reward Bai et al. [\[1\]](#page-14-0), Cui et al. [\[6\]](#page-14-5). Many recent algorithms modify this procedure. In our experiments, we consider:

- PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) Duan et al. [\[11\]](#page-14-10): the de facto RLHF algorithm, which uses a reference model and KL regularization to avoid straying too far from it Bai et al. [\[1\]](#page-14-0). We use PPO in settings like summarization and dialogue following the implementations in e.g. TRLX and HuggingFace TRL Davelouis et al. [\[8\]](#page-14-7).
- TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization) OpenAI [\[29\]](#page-14-28): an earlier RL method. We include it conceptually (as PPO is a first-order approximation to TRPO) but did not find differences relevant to our analysis.
- GRPO (Gated PPO) Ethayarajh et al. [\[12\]](#page-14-11): a variant introducing an adaptive loss to stabilize training.
- ORPO (Off-policy RLHF) Frankle and Carbin [\[13\]](#page-14-12): an approach to use off-policy data (e.g. a static dataset of model outputs with human feedback) rather than on-policy sampling. We study an implementation by Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16).
- DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) Graesser et al. [\[15\]](#page-14-14): a simpler algorithm that directly fine-tunes the model via supervised learning on comparisons, avoiding an explicit reward model or KL penalty.
- Simulated Preference Optimization (SimPO) Hendrycks et al. [\[16\]](#page-14-15): a recent approach that replaces human feedback with a reference-free proxy reward (e.g. using the model's own likelihood or other heuristics).
- PRIME (Process-supervised RL) Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16): an algorithm that uses implicit rewards (e.g. checking intermediate reasoning steps) to guide RL fine-tuning. It still involves policy gradients but with additional regularization.

We refer to any of these as the *RL stage* or RL fine-tuning. Many models undergo a preceding SFT stage on curated instructions before RLHF Cui et al. [\[6\]](#page-14-5), Ouyang et al. [\[30\]](#page-14-29). In our analysis, θinit will often be an SFT model (since RL is applied after SFT), and θfull the parameters after RLHF. We measure a parameter as "updated" if |θfull,i − θinit,i| exceeds a small threshold τ , chosen to account for floating-point precision and noise (we use τ = 10<sup>−</sup><sup>5</sup> in float16, following e.g. PyTorch's gradient-check tolerance Ethayarajh et al. [\[12\]](#page-14-11)). We define *sparsity (%)* as the fraction of parameters that remain unchanged (within τ ) after fine-tuning. Similarly, an *updated subnetwork* refers to the set of weights that did change beyond τ . We provide further details of this setup and thresholding in Section [2.2.](#page-7-0)

Additional Related Efforts. Beyond the studies discussed above, our analysis connects to a broad spectrum of prior art. Classical policy–gradient advances such as TRPO [\[37\]](#page-15-7) and its first-order approximation PPO [\[38\]](#page-15-8) underpin many modern RLHF pipelines. Direct Preference Optimisation [\[35\]](#page-15-9) and comprehensive NLP-policy benchmarks [\[36\]](#page-15-10) refine objective design. Efficiency-oriented methods—including TinyZero [\[31\]](#page-14-30), LISA [\[32\]](#page-14-31), Parameter-Efficient RLHF [\[41\]](#page-15-1), RLx2 sparse training [\[45\]](#page-15-0), and dynamic sparse RL [\[42\]](#page-15-5)—complement our sparsity findings. Large-scale alignment efforts such as DeepSeek-R1 [\[40\]](#page-15-11) and Llama 2 [\[46\]](#page-15-12) illustrate the trend toward reasoning-centric RL. Surveys of multimodal RL reasoning [\[43,](#page-15-13) [61\]](#page-15-4) map the emerging landscape. Practical applications range from optimal tool calls [\[47\]](#page-15-14) and step-by-step verification [\[48\]](#page-15-15) to LoRA-based tiny reasoners [\[49\]](#page-15-16); they are evaluated on complex-reasoning benchmarks such as LSAT [\[50\]](#page-15-17), one-shot RL reasoning [\[51\]](#page-15-18), and the robust MMLU-Pro suite [\[52\]](#page-15-19). Investigations into test-time compute [\[39\]](#page-15-20), zero-RL in the wild [\[57\]](#page-15-21), negative-preference optimisation [\[59\]](#page-15-22), AGIEval benchmarking [\[60\]](#page-15-23), minimalist RLHF pipelines [\[54\]](#page-15-24), model-merging strategies [\[55\]](#page-15-25), and foundational RL theory [\[44\]](#page-15-26) likewise inform the context for our study.

#### <span id="page-7-0"></span>2.2 Methods for Measuring Parameter Updates

To support our claims, we employ several quantitative measures:

• Parameter Update Sparsity: s = #{i:|θfull,i−θinit,i|<τ} |θ| , the proportion of weights unchanged. We test different tolerances τ in Appendix, but results are robust for reasonable τ (e.g. 10<sup>−</sup><sup>6</sup>–10<sup>−</sup><sup>4</sup> ).

- Layerwise/Matrix Sparsity: the same measure computed per layer or per weight matrix (e.g. attention W<sup>Q</sup> matrix). This helps identify if certain parts of the model concentrate updates.
- Overlap of Subnetworks: Given two fine-tuning runs producing weight masks m(1) and m(2) (1 for updated weights, 0 for unchanged), we define the one-sided overlap o<sup>1</sup> = |m(1) ∧ m(2)|/|m(1)| (fraction of run 1's updated weights also updated in run 2). Similarly o<sup>2</sup> for the opposite direction. We compare these overlaps to a *random baseline* where a mask of the same size is placed uniformly at random; baseline overlap would be (1 − s1)(1 − s2) in expectation (product of fractions of weights each run updated) Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17).
- Rank of Update Matrices: For each weight matrix (e.g. each dense layer's weight tensor), we compute the rank of the change ∆W = Wfull − Winit (after fine-tuning). We report the rank as a percentage of the matrix's full rank (taking tolerance 10−<sup>6</sup> for numerical rank). This indicates whether the updates lie in a low-dimensional subspace.
- Performance Recovery: We fine-tune only the identified subnetwork and evaluate the model on relevant metrics (e.g. reward score or task accuracy). Matching or exceeding the original indicates that subnetwork was sufficient.
- Parametric Similarity: We directly compare θsub vs θfull by measuring the proportion of weights that are exactly equal (within tolerance) or the L<sup>2</sup> distance between the vectors. A near-zero distance or >99% identical weights would imply the two fine-tuned models converged to almost the same parameters.

All experiments are conducted on standard LLM benchmarks. For instance, we use the OpenAI Summarize from Feedback dataset Dai et al. [\[7\]](#page-14-6) and truthful QA tasks for RLHF, and evaluate using held-out human preference comparisons. For mathematical reasoning tasks (e.g. MATH problems Huang et al. [\[19\]](#page-14-18)), we use the approach of Lightman et al. [\[22\]](#page-14-21) and others, evaluating accuracy on test questions. Details of datasets and training hyperparameters are in Appendix. Importantly, our analysis is model-agnostic: we applied it to multiple model families (GPT-style decoder-only transformers of various sizes from 1B to 70B parameters). We report representative results on 7B–70B models; smaller models exhibit similar phenomena, though extremely small models (e.g. 125M) can deviate as they lack some emergent properties of LLMs Bai et al. [\[2\]](#page-14-1).

In the next sections, we first quantify the sparsity of RL updates (Section [3\)](#page-8-0), then test subnetwork fine-tuning (Section [4\)](#page-10-0), examine overlap across runs (Section [5\)](#page-11-1), and analyze causes (Section [6\)](#page-12-1).

# <span id="page-8-0"></span>3 RL Fine-Tuning Induces Sparse but Full-Rank Parameter Updates

We begin by measuring the parameter update sparsity in RL fine-tuning, and how it contrasts with standard supervised fine-tuning. The key research question (RQ1) is: *To what extent does RL fine-tuning induce sparse parameter updates, and how are these updates distributed across the model's layers? Also, are the updates low-rank or full-rank in each layer?*

Setup. We compare model checkpoints before and after the SFT stage and before vs after the RL stage for several models. Specifically, we take publicly available RLHF-finetuned LLMs released by their authors on HuggingFace or other platforms, along with their pre-RL (usually SFT) versions when available. In each case, we compute the fraction of parameters unchanged (to tolerance τ = 10<sup>−</sup><sup>5</sup> ) after SFT and after RL. Table [1](#page-9-0) (in appendix) summarizes a subset of results. The models include:

- Tulu-3 8B and 70B (DPO): Chat-oriented models from Lambert et al. [\[21\]](#page-14-20), with both an SFT checkpoint and a further RLHF (DPO) checkpoint.
- Eurus-2 7B (PRIME): A math reasoning model from Yuan et al. [\[56\]](#page-15-27) with supervised and RL (PRIME) versions.
- DeepSeek-Math 7B (GRPO): A 7B model fine-tuned for math word problems by DeepSeek-AI Pan et al. [\[31\]](#page-14-30) using GRPO.
- OpenBMB Eurus-7B (KTO): A base 7B model fine-tuned with KTO algorithm Fu et al. [\[14\]](#page-14-13).
- Mistral-7B (ORPO): A 7B model fine-tuned for alignment by Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16) (ORPO) on top of a Mistral-7B SFT.
- Llama-3-8B (Simulated RLHF): An 8B LLaMA-based model fine-tuned with a Simulated Preference Optimization approach Hendrycks et al. [\[16\]](#page-14-15).
- MathShepherd-7B (PPO): A Mistral-7B model fine-tuned with PPO on a step-by-step math reasoning task by Wang et al. [\[48\]](#page-15-15).

Results. In all cases, RL fine-tuning exhibits very high sparsity in parameter updates, typically 70% or more of weights unchanged. In contrast, the preceding SFT stage (or a comparable supervised fine-tune on the same data) has much lower sparsity, often only 5–15% unchanged (meaning nearly all weights updated). For example, Tulu-3-8B's SFT stage had only 6.8% of weights unchanged, whereas after RLHF via DPO, 76.0% remained unchanged DeepSeek-AI et al. [\[9\]](#page-14-8). Similarly, Eurus-2-7B (a base model) after SFT had ∼32% sparsity, but after PRIME RL 82–87% of weights were unchanged Ethayarajh et al. [\[12\]](#page-14-11). These numbers are summarized in Figure [1](#page-1-0) and Table [1.](#page-9-0) The trend is clear: RLHF's impact on weights is far sparser than SFT's.

We also analyze the distribution across layers. Figure [3a](#page-3-0) shows that sparsity is relatively uniform across the transformer layers for both a chat model fine-tuned with DPO and a math model fine-tuned with PRIME. Most layers had 70–90% of weights unchanged. Importantly, the updated weights are not confined to a particular section (e.g., not only final layers). Each layer contributes some updated weights. The main exception is the LayerNorm parameters (gain and bias): these have near-100% sparsity (few if any changes) in RL fine-tuning. Intuitively, RLHF does not significantly adjust layer normalizations—perhaps because scaling shifts could destabilize generation, or simply because such small vectors are already tuned by SFT. Aside from that, the RL updates are scattered throughout the network.

Next, we examine the rank of the changes. Table [2](#page-9-1) (right side) lists the mean rank of weight update matrices as a percentage of full rank. In all cases, the update rank is >99% of full. E.g., for Tulu-3-8B (DPO) the average update matrix rank is 99.8% of full rank; even the smallest layer's update is 96% of full rank. This indicates that though only a subset of elements change, those changes span a high-dimensional space. The RL updates are not confined to a low-dimensional subspace (unlike, say, LoRA which would yield rank ≪ full). The updates are "sparse but full-rank".

Takeaway 1: *RL fine-tuning consistently produces* >*70% sparsity in parameter updates (most weights remain at their initial values), whereas SFT induces dense changes. These sparse updates are spread across all layers (except layernorms) rather than isolated to particular layers. Despite affecting few weights, the updates per layer are nearly full-rank, implying RL adjusts a broad basis of directions using a small coefficient set.*

Figure [5](#page-5-1) quantifies this effect: the share of weights that change but later revert peaks mid-training, then falls sharply as the run settles on the sparse, stable subnetwork.

# <span id="page-10-0"></span>4 Fine-Tuning Only the RL-Updated Subnetwork Recovers the Full Model

Given that RLHF only updated a small subnetwork of weights, a natural hypothesis (Conjecture 1) is that training just those weights in isolation would be sufficient to reach the same fine-tuned solution. We design experiments to test this hypothesis. This addresses RQ2 and RQ3: *(RQ2) Can fine-tuning only the discovered subnetwork (freezing other weights) achieve the same performance as full-model RL? (RQ3) Do the resulting weights match the fully fine-tuned model's weights (i.e. converge to the same solution)?*

The optimisation trajectories are compared in Figure [4.](#page-4-0) Subnetwork-only training reaches the same—or slightly lower—loss, reinforcing that the untouched weights are superfluous for the RL objective.

Setup. We focus on two RL fine-tuning scenarios for evaluation: (a) Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on a mixture of instruction-following tasks (the AllenAI Open-Instruct pipeline Ouyang et al. [\[30\]](#page-14-29)), using the 8B Tulu-3 model; (b) Process-supervised RL (PRIME) on math reasoning tasks, using the 7B Eurus-2 model Hong et al. [\[17\]](#page-14-16). For each scenario, we have θinit (after SFT) and θfull (after RL). We compute the mask m of updated weights as described earlier. Then we perform a new RL fine-tuning run starting from θinit, but at each training step we zero-out the gradients for any weight where m<sup>i</sup> = 0. Thus, only the originally updated subnetwork is allowed to train; all other weights remain frozen at their initial values. We use the same RL algorithm, hyperparameters, and number of training iterations as the original full-model run. This yields θsub, the subnetwork-finetuned model.

We then compare θsub vs θfull on: (1) test performance metrics (human preference win-rate for the Open-Instruct tasks; accuracy on held-out math reasoning problems for PRIME), and (2) closeness of the final weights. For (2), we measure the fraction of weights that are exactly equal (within tolerance) between θsub and θfull, and the L<sup>2</sup> norm of their difference.

Results. Table [3](#page-11-0) shows that training only the subnetwork m not only recovers the performance of the full fine-tuned model, but in some cases slightly exceeds it. In the DPO case, θsub achieved on average a 1.6 point higher reward score (averaged across a suite of instruction-following tasks) than θfull. Humans (and our automated reward model) found its outputs equally or more preferable. In the PRIME case, the subnetwork-trained model solved the math test problems with an overall accuracy of 72.2%, vs 69.8% for the full model (on a 5-level difficulty benchmark). In particular at the hardest difficulty (level 5), θsub scored 45.5% vs 40.3% for θfull. These small improvements hint that freezing the "inert" weights might reduce some overfitting or instability, but the differences are within a few points—essentially, θsub matches θfull performance.

Turning to the final weights: θsub and θfull were found to be nearly identical. Over 99.9% of parameters in these 7B–8B models are exactly equal up to 10<sup>−</sup><sup>4</sup> tolerance (the only differences being among the subnetwork weights themselves). Even among the subnetwork weights, the values are extremely close. The L<sup>2</sup> norm between θsub and θfull relative to the L<sup>2</sup> norm of θfull is on the order of 10−<sup>6</sup> . In short, fine-tuning only the subnetwork m recapitulated the full-model fine-tuning to an astonishing degree of similarity—effectively converging to the same trained model.

These results strongly support Conjecture 1: the subnetwork identified by RL training is sufficient to recover not only the performance but even the specific solution of full-model training. This goes beyond prior "lottery ticket" findings where subnetworks could match accuracy but might converge to a different set of weights. Here, the final weight vectors coincide (within tiny numerical differences). It suggests the full-model RL procedure was mostly moving weights in that subnetwork, and leaving others as they were; those other weights truly did not need to change.

We note this was tested on tasks where RLHF's goal was to fine-tune an already competent model for alignment or improved solving, which may partially explain why freezing most weights did not hurt (the model's base knowledge sufficed, only alignment knobs needed turning). If one attempted RL from scratch on a completely new task with a random initialization, obviously all weights might need training. In our setting, though, RLHF serves more to *refine* an existing model, and it appears to do so by focusing on a specific subset of parameters.

Takeaway 2: *Fine-tuning only the sparse subnetwork of weights that RL would have updated yields virtually the same model as full-model fine-tuning. It attains equal or slightly better performance, and the resulting parameters coincide with the fully fine-tuned model's parameters (over 99.9% of weights identical). This confirms that the rest of the weights were essentially inert and unnecessary to change for the RL objective.*

## <span id="page-11-1"></span>5 Consistency of RL-Updated Subnetworks Across Seeds, Data, and Algorithms

We have seen that RLHF tends to update a specific subnetwork of parameters. We next ask: *Is this subnetwork largely the same if we vary the training conditions (random seed, training data, or even RL algorithm)?* In other words, does the same "lottery ticket" subnetwork appear consistently? This addresses RQ4.

Setup. We consider three axes of variation:

- 1. Random seed: Fine-tune the same model on the same dataset with the same algorithm, but use two different random seeds for initialization of the RL training (which affects data shuffling, exploration noise, etc.).
- 2. Training dataset: Fine-tune the model on two different RLHF datasets (of similar nature). Here we use the Tulu-3-8B model: (I1) finetune on the Tulu preference dataset (dialogue alignment) vs (I2) finetune on a PRIME rollout dataset (math reasoning alignments). These yield two different preference-tuned models.
- 3. RL algorithm: Fine-tune the model with two different RL approaches. Specifically, we take the Eurus-2-7B base model and finetune it using (I1) DPO vs (I2) PRIME on the math reasoning dataset (thus one model uses a direct preference optimization, the other uses RL with process supervision).

For each pair of fine-tuned models (I1 vs I2), we identify their updated weight masks m(1) and m(2). We compute the overlap metrics: o<sup>1</sup> = fraction of (I1)'s updated weights that were also updated in (I2), and o<sup>2</sup> similarly Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). We compare these to a random baseline overlap: if one randomly selects (1 − s1)|θ| parameters for mask1 and (1 − s2)|θ| for mask2, the expected fraction of mask1 that lies in mask2 is (1 − s2) (and vice versa (1 − s1)) Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17). Intuitively, baseline overlap = chance that a weight updated in run1 would also be updated in run2 if the subnetwork were random.

Results. Table [4](#page-12-0) shows the observed overlaps vs random baseline. Across all variations, the overlaps are far higher than chance. For instance, varying random seed on Tulu-3-8B (DPO runs) yields o<sup>1</sup> ≈ o<sup>2</sup> ≈ 60.5% overlap, whereas chance overlap would be about 36.7% given each run's ∼63% sparsity. That means the two runs updated largely the same parameters. When varying the dataset (Tulu vs PRIME data) but using the same base and algorithm, we still see substantial common subnetworks: 26.7% one-sided overlap and 67.1% on the other side (due to different sparsity levels of 63% vs 85%). These exceed chance (14.6%, 36.7%). Even when we stress test by changing seed, data, *and* algorithm altogether (DPO vs PRIME runs on different data), we find notable overlaps of 59.1% and 33.2%—again well above chance (23.0%, 12.9%). In summary, the subnetwork of updated weights is not idiosyncratic to each training condition; there appears to be a partially consistent core subnetwork that tends to be updated across different RL finetunings of the same base model.

These overlaps are not 100%, of course—so some subset of the updated weights are specific to each run's details. But the above-chance overlap suggests a significant intersection. This indicates that the pretrained model has certain weight dimensions that are more amenable or relevant to adaptation via RL, which get picked up repeatedly across runs. In other words, RLHF might leverage some inherent structure in the model (e.g. certain neurons or attention

# <span id="page-12-1"></span>6 Why Do the Subnetworks Emerge?

Having established the phenomenon of RL-induced sparse updates, we turn to analyzing *why* it occurs. Our evidence points to one primary factor: the nature of the training data relative to the model's existing policy. Specifically, RLHF fine-tunes on data that is drawn from or similar to the model's own output distribution (either on-policy or from a near-pretrained policy). This means the model is not being forced to drastically change its behavior, but rather to make relatively fine-grained adjustments.

In our experiments, we observe that if the model is fine-tuned on *out-of-distribution* data, the sparsity significantly decreases (i.e. more weights have to change). For example, a pure supervised fine-tuning on a human-written dataset often yields dense updates (as in Figure [1\)](#page-1-0). By contrast, RLHF often includes an initial SFT to bring the model's outputs closer to the target distribution (making RL's job a fine adjustment around that vicinity)Hu et al. [\[18\]](#page-14-17), or inherently uses on-policy sampling so the model rarely strays far. Zhang et al. [\[58\]](#page-15-28) recently highlighted that using instruction data that "fits" the model leads to better performance, implying smaller shifts needed. Our results reinforce that: RLHF likely keeps the model in a regime where minimal changes suffice.

We support this explanation with two findings: (1) If we start RL from a policy already close to optimal (e.g. after SFT on the same task), the updates are extremely sparse, sometimes near-zero. In Table [3,](#page-11-0) note that difficulties 1–2 in the math tasks had 0.0% performance difference between θfull and θsub, and presumably minimal weight changes (since SFT already solved those easy tasks). We found indeed that in those runs, RL didn't alter many weights for those skills. (2) We measure the fraction of "transient" parameter changes during RL training, i.e. weights that at some point deviate but end up back at initial value by final model. In Figure [3b](#page-3-0), this fraction spikes early in training (when the model explores more new outputs) but then declines as training converges and those weights revert. Essentially, RL may initially touch more weights but if those changes didn't consistently improve reward (perhaps because they were trying to push output distribution too far), they are undone by later updates that bring them back to original. The final outcome is that only the necessary small subset remain changed.

We also tested whether explicit techniques used in RLHF to restrain changes (KL penalties, gradient clipping) were causing the sparsity. Our ablations (not fully shown due to space) indicated they are not primary drivers: turning off KL in an algorithm increased divergence in output quality but the difference in proportion of updated weights was marginal. Similarly, using a larger KL coefficient did not further increase sparsity beyond the baseline. Gradient clipping ensures no extremely large single-step updates, but doesn't systematically zero out gradients to produce global sparsity; and indeed disabling it led to instability but not a dramatic shift from sparse to dense updates (mostly it caused overshooting of some weights but still many weights remained unchanged within tolerance overall).

Finally, we consider the role of model architecture. We noticed layernorms rarely updated, which might be due to them having very small magnitude gradients under typical RLHF losses. Also, potentially because adjusting LN scales globally can wreck the delicate balance of features, RL might implicitly avoid doing that, focusing on weights that more directly influence token probabilities in specific ways (like attention output layers or bias terms in output heads). Indeed, we saw that output biases were among parameters frequently updated by RL (intuitively adjusting token-specific preferences, e.g. making "Thanks" more likely as a polite reply or reducing profanity tokens, etc.). These biases form part of the subnetwork across runs. This aligns with intuitive expectations about alignment tasks: they often involve rewarding certain high-level behaviors (which might correspond to certain tokens or phrases increasing/decreasing in likelihood) rather than rewriting entire layers of internal representation.

In summary: RLHF's training pattern—starting from a model already competent and training on examples the model itself could produce—means that large parts of the model do not need to move. The optimization primarily finds a small number of "dials" to turn (some attention head outputs, some vocabulary biases, etc.) to achieve the new objectives. If RL training data were more off-distribution (imagine forcing a language model to learn an unrelated domain via RL, like code or something, which is rare since RL for LMs usually focuses on style/quality rather than entirely new content), we suspect update sparsity would be lower. Indeed, a pure offline RL from scratch might behave more like supervised fine-tuning in needing broad changes if the initial policy is far off.

### <span id="page-13-0"></span>7 Conclusion

We have shown that reinforcement learning fine-tuning of LLMs (such as RLHF) inherently operates by tweaking only a small subnetwork of model parameters—on the order of 5–30% of weights—while leaving the rest effectively inert. This result was demonstrated across multiple models, tasks, and RL methods. The updated subnetwork appears to be a partially intrinsic property of the pretrained model: it recurs across independent runs and different reward formulations, suggesting these weights correspond to capabilities or features that are consistently useful for adaptation.

This has practical implications. It suggests a new perspective on efficient RLHF: rather than always fine-tuning all weights (which is computationally expensive for large models), one could identify this subnetwork via a cheaper run or probe, and then focus training on it. Our Conjecture 1 experiments indeed indicate that training just that subnetwork is enough. It also opens questions about interpretability: can we understand what this subnetwork represents? Perhaps they are the neurons controlling politeness, factuality, reasoning, etc. If so, one might design more targeted interventions or monitor these weights for undesirable drifts.

Our study focused on RLHF-type scenarios, where the model's initial policy is already decent at the task and the RL mainly provides subtle alignment nudges. We expect our findings to hold in similar contexts (e.g. RL fine-tuning for safety, or on open-ended dialogue tasks as widely done). It would be future work to see if similar sparsity holds for cases of extreme distribution shift in RL (though such cases are less common because RLHF rarely is used to teach entirely new domains).

We must note limitations: our analysis relies on thresholds to define "unchanged" weights, and though we chose conservative values, at very high precision one could argue every weight moves slightly in continuous optimization. Our use of tolerance accounts for floating point noise and negligible changes. We believe it captures meaningful "effective zeros" in updates, but others might refine this with e.g. significance testing per weight. Another limitation is that we did not deeply explore which parts of the model the subnetwork concentrates in (due to space). Preliminary checks suggest it's distributed across layers but possibly enriched in certain layers (like final decoder blocks or output embedding biases). A thorough breakdown could reveal if, say, attention heads or feed-forward networks contribute more to alignment changes. That is an interesting direction: mapping what the RLHF subnetwork corresponds to in model functionality.

Our work connects to the lottery ticket hypothesis and network pruning literature. It provides a new angle: rather than searching for a subnetwork that can train from scratch (as in LTH), we observe that standard training itself picks a subnetwork to modify, leaving a "lottery ticket" that was pre-drawn by the model's pretraining. It also gives credence to the hypothesis that large LMs have many "dormant" circuits that can be activated with minimal changes for new abilitiesPanda et al. [\[33\]](#page-14-33). RLHF might just be toggling those circuits on (or off).

In conclusion, RL fine-tuning of LLMs is a surprisingly precise surgery on the network parameters. Recognizing this sparse surgery could lead to more efficient and controlled fine-tuning methods, as well as deeper insights into what internal structures correspond to aligned versus misaligned behavior in large language models.

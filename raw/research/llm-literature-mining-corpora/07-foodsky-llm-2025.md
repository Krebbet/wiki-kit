## Summary


Food is the cornerstone of both survival and social life. With the increasing complexity of global dietary cultures, there is a growing demand for food intelligence to enable tasks like recipe recommendations and diet\-disease correlation discovery. To address this, we introduce the food\-oriented large language model (LLM) FoodSky, which offers fine\-grained perception and reasoning on food data. We constructed a food corpus, FoodEarth, from various authoritative sources to enhance FoodSky’s knowledge. We also developed the topic\-based selective state space model and hierarchical topic retrieval augmented generation algorithms to improve FoodSky’s ability to capture fine\-grained food semantics and generate context\-aware food\-relevant text. Extensive experiments show that FoodSky significantly outperforms general\-purpose LLMs on the Chinese National Chef Examination and Dietetic Examination, achieving an accuracy of 83\.3% and 91\.2%, respectively. Beyond enhancing culinary creativity and promoting healthier eating patterns, FoodSky aims to establish a new benchmark for domain\-specific LLMs in addressing real\-world food\-related challenges.


**Keywords:** food computing, large language models, instruction tuning, retrieval augmented generation

## The bigger picture


Food and nutrition are fundamental to human well\-being, but understanding and managing dietary information can be complex and challenging. Our study on FoodSky shows that a domain\-specific large language model (LLM) has greater potential to enable real\-world food\-related tasks. FoodSky is designed to comprehend and reason about food\-oriented knowledge, allowing it to perform at a more professional level in both cooking and dietary tasks. By addressing the unique challenges of food computing, FoodSky offers more precise recipe recommendations, improved nutritional guidance, and enhanced culinary creativity. Furthermore, by training on a high\-quality food instruction dataset and utilizing algorithms specifically tailored to the food domain, FoodSky demonstrates the capability to pass the chef and dietetic examinations, which are typically difficult for non\-professionals. This bridges the gap between general AI capabilities and the specialized needs of the food sector while also inspiring domain\-specific LLM methods across other disciplines. Overall, our work advances food computing research and offers practical benefits for public health, culinary education, and food industry innovation. By making complex food\-related information more accessible and actionable, FoodSky contributes to a future where AI helps improve public dietary health, supports culinary education, and fosters a deeper understanding of food, ultimately leading to more sustainable outcomes.

## Introduction


Food is fundamental to human survival and culture, closely linked to social values and personal habits.[1](#bib1),[2](#bib2) The rich diversity not only enhances our gastronomic experience but also contributes to the complex world of food data.[3](#bib3),[4](#bib4) As society continues to evolve, so does the complexity of the food system, leading to the accumulation of a vast amount of data related to culinary practices,[5](#bib5),[6](#bib6) consumption patterns,[1](#bib1),[7](#bib7) and nutritional content.[8](#bib8),[9](#bib9) In this context, food computing has become a key interdisciplinary field toward food intelligence. It leverages this wide range of data to enable critical applications from farm to table,[10](#bib10),[11](#bib11) such as agricultural advice,[12](#bib12),[13](#bib13) food robot control,[14](#bib14),[15](#bib15) culinary creation,[16](#bib16),[17](#bib17) dietary tracking,[18](#bib18),[19](#bib19) and chronic disease prevention.[20](#bib20)


Within the broad spectrum of food computing, cuisine and nutrition stand out as two critical topics due to their direct impact on people’s health and well\-being.[16](#bib16),[21](#bib21) Recent studies have explored various tasks, such as ingredient recognition,[22](#bib22),[23](#bib23) recipe retrieval,[5](#bib5),[24](#bib24) and nutrition assessment.[25](#bib25),[26](#bib26) Among these tasks, dietary agent is an important topic that helps consumers make smarter and healthier dietary choices.[27](#bib27),[28](#bib28) The research on dietary agent is initially driven by advances in food recommendations[29](#bib29),[30](#bib30),[31](#bib31) and recipe retrieval.[5](#bib5),[32](#bib32) Since large language models (LLMs) have recently emerged to address complex real\-world issues in various fields, researchers have begun to implement LLMs in the domain of dietary agent. For example, Qarajeh et al.[33](#bib33) employed LLMs to plan healthier diets to prevent diseases such as hyperkalemia and hyperphosphatemia. Yin et al.[34](#bib34) proposed a versatile dietary assistant, FoodLMM, based on LLaVA,[35](#bib35) which is capable of food recognition, ingredient recognition, food segmentation, recipe generation, and nutrition estimation. However, further improvement is limited due to the lack of a fundamental food\-specific LLM. Rezayi et al.[36](#bib36) proposed AgriBERT to improve task\-specific performance in agricultural areas such as crop disease detection and yield prediction. However, foundational LLMs for downstream food applications have not been fully considered.


Driven by the growth of food systems, the transition from analyzing local data to accumulating massive global datasets is providing new solutions in food computing.[3](#bib3),[4](#bib4) This shift toward large\-scale food data can support the building of a food\-oriented LLM, inspired by the success of LLMs in other domains,[37](#bib37),[38](#bib38),[39](#bib39) including medicine,[40](#bib40),[41](#bib41) education,[42](#bib42) and finance.[43](#bib43) In medicine, LLMs such as ChatGPT have shown potential in medical education,[44](#bib44) radiology decision\-making,[45](#bib45) and phenotype recognition.[46](#bib46) Medical LLMs such as Baize,[47](#bib47) Zhongjing,[48](#bib48) and Huatuo[49](#bib49) have achieved better performance in these medical tasks. In education, LLMs can generate personalized tutorials[50](#bib50) and provide real\-time feedback in the self\-study process.[51](#bib51) In finance, financial LLMs, including InvestLM,[52](#bib52) FinGPT,[53](#bib53) and BloombergGPT,[54](#bib54) are enhancing customer service and providing financial advice on risk assessment, algorithmic trading, and market prediction.[53](#bib53)


To address the lack of foundational food\-oriented LLMs, we introduce FoodSky, a domain\-specific LLM designed for the culinary and dietetic fields. However, the development of FoodSky faced significant challenges. First, there is a lack of a large\-scale food corpus. Unlike fields such as news and media, where data are abundant, food data are relatively limited and scattered across various sources, including cooking websites, recipe databases, food blogs, etc. The quality of these data also varies, with spelling and grammar errors, duplicate invalid data, and irrelevant information that complicates the data cleaning process. Second, the food domain covers a wide range of topics, including ingredients, cuisines, eating habits, and nutritional information, presenting a challenge for the model to understand and handle these diverse topics comprehensively and effectively. Last, the cross\-cultural nature of food requires the ability to process food\-related knowledge in various cultural contexts. Different regions and cultures have distinct eating habits, taste preferences, and cooking traditions, which adds complexity for LLMs in handling food queries from various backgrounds.


Based on the FoodEarth database, the topic\-based selective state space model (TS3M), and hierarchical topic retrieval augmented generation (HTRAG), the proposed FoodSky demonstrates expert\-level performance in both chef and dietetic examinations. In particular, FoodSky passes the Chinese National Chef Exam and Dietetic Examination with a zero\-shot accuracy of 83\.3% and 91\.2%, respectively. More experiments, including both qualitative and quantitative evaluations, further show that FoodSky achieves better performance compared to existing LLMs, including Qwen2\.5[55](#bib55) and ChatGPT,[56](#bib56) on the chef exam and dietetic exam, demonstrating its ability to provide reliable advice for various culinary and dietetic questions. As shown in [Figure 1](#fig1), the proposed FoodSky aims to enhance culinary creativity, promote dietary health, and benefit diverse groups of people across various scenarios and applications.

## Results


### Summary


The established framework of FoodSky is illustrated in [Figure 2](#fig2). In FoodSky, we proposed two key algorithms, TS3M to enhance the accuracy of the model by integrating topic\-relevant information and HTRAG to generate responses that are richer in informational content. We implemented FoodSky using two different backbone frameworks, Chinese LLaMA\-2 (CLLaMA2\)[57](#bib57) and the latest Qwen2\.5,[55](#bib55) named FoodSky\-CL and FoodSky\-Qw, respectively. To assess the performance of FoodSky, we tested the baseline models and various versions and sizes of FoodSky on the chef and dietetic examinations (CDE) benchmark, the food long conversation (FoodLongConv) benchmark, and the food question and answer (FoodQA) benchmark. We designed two versions of the CDE benchmark to evaluate different aspects of model performance. The CDE\-739 benchmark provides an efficient assessment with a smaller set of multiple\-choice questions derived from the CDE. To facilitate more comprehensive evaluations, we developed the CDE\-12K benchmark, an extended version of the CDE. Specifically, CDE\-12K comprises 628 multiple\-choice questions from the Chinese chef examination and 650 multiple\-choice questions from the Chinese dietetic examination. Accuracy on the CDE benchmark serves as the main metric for evaluating LLMs, as it objectively determines the correctness of the models’ answers. Some examples from the benchmark are shown in [Figure 3](#fig3).

To evaluate the conversation and question\-answering capabilities of LLMs, we built the FoodLongConv benchmark and the FoodQA benchmark. FoodLongConv contains 22 essay questions that require long answers, where 10 questions are extracted from the Chinese chef examination, and 12 questions are extracted from the Chinese dietetic examination. We used a variety of automatic evaluation metrics, including bilingual evaluation understudy (BLEU)\-1–4,[58](#bib65) Google\-BLEU (GLEU),[59](#bib66) recall\-oriented understudy for gisting evaluation (ROUGE)\-1, ROUGE\-2, and ROUGE\-L[60](#bib67) to evaluate the quality of LLM responses on FoodLongConv. We also employed GPT\-4[61](#bib68) as the judge of subjective scores, including fluent, logical, professional, and informative scores on FoodQA. FoodQA is a benchmark focused on comparing different performances of the model’s answers and contains 27 short\-answer questions and essay questions, where 12 questions are extracted from the Chinese chef examination, and 15 questions are extracted from the Chinese dietetic examination.

### Ablation studies


#### Ablation on model structures


To investigate the impact of different components in FoodSky, we conducted comprehensive ablation studies by removing key components and evaluating the performance on the CDE\-12K, FoodLongConv, and FoodQA benchmarks. To swiftly verify the validity of the proposed methods, we used FoodEarth\-680K, a high\-quality version of the FoodEarth dataset with 680,000 instruction data to train our FoodSky. [Table 3](#tbl3) presents the results of ablation studies on model structure. The baseline FoodSky\-CL\-7B model, without any additional components, obtains an average accuracy of 55\.5% in the CDE\-12K benchmark. Incorporating TS3M improved the average accuracy to 60\.7%, an improvement of 5\.2%, demonstrating its importance in enhancing topic understanding. When evaluating HTRAG independently, we implemented it following a standard retrieval\-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline without the topic\-based selective mechanism, meaning the retrieval was performed using traditional passage chunk retrieval while maintaining the hierarchical structure. This HTRAG\-only configuration results in a score of 59\.7%, with an improvement of 4\.2% over the baseline, suggesting its effect in providing contextually relevant answers by leveraging external knowledge.

## Discussion


### Food knowledge in FoodSky for enhanced domain\-specific performance


Unlike previous LLMs,[46](#bib46),[76](#bib76),[77](#bib77) FoodSky addresses critical challenges in the food domain, such as dietary analysis, food recommendations, and nutritional, diet, and cooking advice, through understanding the culinary arts and nutritional science. While general\-purpose LLMs may struggle with diverse food topics and cultural backgrounds, FoodSky is able to deliver precise information perception and reasoning capabilities tailored to the food domain via massive training on food data. Moreover, the TS3M architecture utilized in FoodSky enables it to capture fine\-grained topic differences, and the proposed HTRAG ensures a model with more precise and background\-aware responses. The integration of food computing with LLMs in FoodSky provides data\-centric insights into food research, enhances user experiences in food applications, and paves a new way for food intelligence that is different from the conventional paradigm.[78](#bib78) This capability sets FoodSky apart as a unique tool that can influence culinary professionals, dietitians, and consumers.


We conduct extensive qualitative experiments for detailed analysis and discussion. As illustrated in [Figure 8](#fig8), the qualitative experimental results in the dietary education scenario for adolescents reveal the different foci and depths of analysis of different LLMs. In this example, FoodSky delivers the most in\-depth exploration of the topic, focusing on the direct health impacts of fast food snacks, promoting moderate consumption for teenagers. The answers of Intern are lengthy, as they offer too much detail on health effects and extensive dietary advice. ChatGPT presents a balanced view that integrates moderate consumption of fast food with healthier dietary habits. Overall, FoodSky is capable of quickly identifying the immediate health risks associated with high\-calorie and high\-fat diets and providing actionable recommendations, which are critical for promoting healthier eating habits in teenagers. More qualitative results for other scenarios can be found in the [supplemental information](#appsec2).

### Enhancing domain\-specific LLM performance with TS3M and HTRAG


The FoodSky model’s integration of the TS3M and HTRAG directly addresses the need for higher accuracy and context relevance in food\-specific question answering. TS3M enhances the model’s capability to prioritize relevant information during the generation process, effectively narrowing down the vast search space of potential responses to those most relevant to the queried topics. HTRAG complements this by dynamically retrieving external knowledge that aligns with the ongoing dialogue context, enriching the responses with detailed, up\-to\-date knowledge. The improvement based on these modules proves the feasibility of adapting general LLM frameworks to specialized domains, demonstrating that the integration of topic\-focused processing and dynamic information retrieval can substantially enhance the LLM’s applicability and performance in fields requiring expert\-level interactions.

### Generalization and scalability of foundation models


FoodSky’s superior performance in the FoodLongConv and FoodQA benchmarks demonstrates its ability to handle extended dialogues and complex questions, which are common in professional environments. Additional fine\-tuning experiments on regional cuisines further verify FoodSky’s generalization capabilities. The higher scores in BLEU and ROUGE evaluations indicate that FoodSky not only generates contextually appropriate responses with enhanced food knowledge but also maintains coherent and informative dialogue structures across different cuisines.


However, training large\-scale models like FoodSky\-Qw\-7B demands significant computational resources and extensive datasets. For example, training FoodSky\-Qw\-7B involved approximately 80 graphics processing unit (GPU) hours and a total 640 GB of memory alongside a dataset of over 800,000 food\-related samples. These factors, including high\-performance GPUs and significant memory, critically impact both training time and resource allocation. To address these challenges, we investigated the possibility of fine\-tuning smaller datasets using a foundation model. This measure explored efficient fine\-tuning on task\-specific datasets, reducing the need for extensive computational resources. Preliminary experiments show that fine\-tuning FoodSky\-Qw\-7B on specialized datasets maintains significant performance levels to address fine\-grained domain issues. Future work will aim to further optimize the foundation model architecture and fine\-tuning techniques to improve scalability and adaptability across various food\-related applications.

### Limitations and future prospects of the study


Despite the advances demonstrated by FoodSky in applying LLMs to the food and nutrition domain, there are still several limitations to consider. While FoodSky leverages a comprehensive food corpus for model training, its performance still heavily depends on the quality and scope of the underlying data. This reliance could limit the model’s ability to accurately analyze less common or rare food items and practices. Currently, FoodSky focuses solely on text\-based data and cannot yet handle additional modalities like visual, olfactory, or taste data, restricting its applicability to multimodal QA tasks, such as food image analysis or sensory feedback integration. In addition, the evaluation metrics used, such as BLEU and ROUGE, focus on linguistic similarities rather than the actual usefulness or accuracy of the information provided, potentially not reflecting the model’s effectiveness in real\-world scenarios, where nuanced understanding and adaptability are crucial. These issues underscore the need for improvements in data diversity, modality coverage, and evaluation methods.


In the future, we aim to enhance the reasoning ability in the food\-specific foundation model by adapting the chain\-of\-thought and process reward model for efficient food reasoning in dietetic scenarios. It is expected to facilitate efficient fine\-tuning on smaller, task\-specific datasets, reducing the required computational resources while maintaining high performance. FoodSky is also to be extended to a multimodal LLM to handle visual inputs like food images for recipe suggestions, dietary monitoring, and nutritional analysis, allowing healthcare professionals and patients to more effectively analyze meal compositions and caloric content. We will also incorporate reinforcement learning with user feedback to improve response quality and integrate more comprehensive health data, including patient dietary records, clinical guidelines, and nutritional information related to chronic diseases, to allow personalized dietary recommendations. Furthermore, expanding FoodSky’s industrial applications, such as food design, supply chain management, and medical nutrition therapy, is a critical direction to ensure its broader impact across both the food and healthcare industries.

### Inspiring LLMs in specialized knowledge domains


The effectiveness of FoodSky in the food and nutrition domain opens avenues for similar applications across other specialized fields that require precise and informed responses. The model’s ability to systematically integrate and apply domain\-specific knowledge could serve as a blueprint for developing LLMs in areas such as medicine, law, and engineering, where accuracy and depth of knowledge are crucial. Such developments could significantly impact these fields by providing tools that augment human expertise and facilitate science popularity and more accessible information. The promising results from FoodSky suggest a scalable model for deploying LLMs in various professional fields, where they can act as both educational tools and expert systems, enhancing the efficiency and reach of professional advice and decision\-making.

Based on the evaluation results, we used the gte\-Qwen1\.5\-7B\-instruct embedding method for data filtering and further clustering processing. Initially, the embedding model was used to obtain the embedding representation of all instruction data in the dataset. Then, we calculated the cosine similarity between each piece of data. We followed the threshold setting of experiments on the mini\-test set and set the threshold to 0\.9\. Any two pieces of data with cosine similarity greater than the threshold were regarded as similar data. The shorter piece of data was removed in similar data pairs. However, this method is often inaccurate when the text content of data is similar, but the actual intention is different. Therefore, we further adopted a clustering method that jointly embedded representations of intentions and performed similarity filtering on the dataset. The process is as follows.


* (1\)LLMs are used to perform semantic analysis on question texts, identify their core intents, and classify them into corresponding intent categories. These intent categories describe the topic of the question and user needs and inherent goals. The diversity and prevalence of intents are modeled through probability distribution to guide the clustering process.
* (2\)The processed question\-answering data and their corresponding intent categories are entered into the gte\-Qwen1\.5\-7B\-instruct text embedding model and then converted into a high\-dimensional vector representation, retaining the original semantic information and reflecting the semantic similarity between texts.
* (3\)Agglomerative hierarchical clustering (AHC) is used to classify the data after obtaining the embedding representation. AHC forms a hierarchical structure by iteratively merging the most similar data points. We incorporate the intent distribution information into the clustering algorithm and ensure that the clustering results are based on both semantic similarity and the importance of each intent category through weight assignment. The weights are adjusted according to the probability and frequency in the intent distribution model, making data points of common intent categories more influential in the clustering.
* (4\)The formation of the final dataset depends on the selection of representative data points. This process takes into account the centrality of the data points in the micro\-category to ensure that the dataset is diverse and representative. The distance between each data point and the center of the corresponding micro\-category is calculated, and the data point with the smallest distance is selected to demonstrate the characteristics of each category.


Finally, the dataset is optimized to improve its quality and diversity. We invite two experts to conduct secondary validation on this refined dataset to ensure the usability of each final piece, resulting in the final version of FoodEarth with 811,491 Chinese instruction data.

## Methods


In FoodSky, we propose two key algorithms, TS3M to enhance the accuracy of the model by integrating topic\-relevant information and HTRAG to generate responses that are richer in informational content. The instruction\-tuning of the backbone is first introduced to provide a technical background for the proposed algorithm.


### Instruction fine\-tuning of the backbone


The backbone model serves as the foundation for our proposed extensions, including TS3M and HTRAG, which can further enhance the comprehension ability of the proposed model and generate responses to food\-related instructions and questions.


Specifically, our backbone model  is an LLM (e.g., LLaMA\-2 or Qwen2\.5\) pretrained on the large\-scale Chinese corpus and fine\-tuned on a Chinese instruction dataset from the general domain. To adapt our backbone  to effectively follow instructions and generate appropriate responses in the food domain, we employ an instruction fine\-tuning approach with two stages: general instruction tuning and food\-specific instruction tuning. Through the first stage of general instruction tuning on the large\-scale general\-domain dataset, we obtained the instructed tuned backbone . This model has the ability to understand and follow instructions in context, providing basic instruction\-following capabilities.


After the general instruction tuning stage, the model has obtained a solid foundation in instruction following. However, to specialize in the food domain, we proceed to the second stage: food\-specific instruction tuning. In this stage, we employ an iterative fine\-tuning approach, where we gradually increase the complexity and specificity of the food\-related instructions during training. This allows the model to progressively adapt to the fine\-grained characteristics and challenges of the food domain, improving its performance with more specialized capability. By focusing on food\-specific instructions, the model learns to understand and generate responses that are tailored to the unique terminology and challenges of the food domain, taking into account factors like ingredient properties, cooking methods, and dietary restrictions.

### HTRAG


The HTRAG mechanism is proposed to enhance the generation capabilities of the instruction\-tuned model by integrating the retrieved information during inference. It aims to provide the model with relevant and context\-specific knowledge to improve the accuracy and information richness of generated responses.


HTRAG operates through a multi\-step process that hierarchically retrieves and integrates topic\-relevant information from an external knowledge base, which includes food encyclopedias, recipe databases, nutritional information, and culinary techniques. The knowledge base is preprocessed and indexed by FAISS[85](#bib86) to facilitate efficient retrieval based on the input instructions. The gte\-Qwen2\-7B\-instruct is implemented as an embedding model for retrieval.

### Training details


To investigate the impact of model size on performance, we trained two different backbone models: CLLaMA2\-7B and CLLaMA2\-13B. After the food\-specific fine\-tuning, we referred to the resulting models as FoodSky\-7B for the 7 billion\-parameter variant and FoodSky\-13B for the 13 billion\-parameter variant, which were trained on a server with 8 NVIDIA V100 and a server with 8 NVIDIA A100, respectively. Our methods were trained using a low\-rank adaptation parameter\-efficient tuning method.[86](#bib87) We used the transformers[87](#bib88) and the peft[88](#bib89) libraries based on Pytorch. To make a trade\-off between the training efficiency and performance, we implemented fp12 precision with ZeRO\-3 and a gradient accumulation strategy based on DeepSpeed.[89](#bib90) We limited the length of a single response to 1,500\. We used a dropout rate of 0\.1, a learning rate of , and a cosine learning rate scheduler based on Adam.[90](#bib91) We evaluated the last convergent checkpoint as the final result. In order to maintain the stability of the training, we halved the loss when the gradient burst and halved the attenuation learning rate. The losses of all training phases are successfully converged within an effective range.
